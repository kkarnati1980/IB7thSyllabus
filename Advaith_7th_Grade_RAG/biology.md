## Chapter 1.1 — The Four Biomolecules

### Concept: Biomolecules and their monomers
**Definition.** Biomolecules are the large organic molecules that make up living things and food. There are four main types — **carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids** — each built from smaller repeating units called **monomers**.

| Biomolecule | Monomer (building block) | Key elements |
|-------------|--------------------------|--------------|
| Carbohydrate | Monosaccharide (e.g. glucose) | C, H, O |
| Protein | Amino acid | C, H, O, N |
| Lipid | Fatty acids + glycerol | C, H, O |
| Nucleic acid | Nucleotide | C, H, O, N, P |

**Real-world example.** A slice of bread contains carbohydrate (starch), some protein (gluten), a little lipid, and traces of nucleic acids from the wheat cells.

**Analogy.** Monomers are like Lego bricks; polymers (starch, proteins, DNA) are the models built from many identical or similar bricks joined together.

**Why it matters.** Your body breaks food polymers back into monomers during digestion so cells can absorb and reuse them — you literally rebuild yourself from the building blocks in your food.

**Common misconception.** *"Carbohydrates are unhealthy and cause weight gain."* Believed because of popular low-carb diets. Wrong because carbohydrates are the body's primary and cleanest energy source; the issue is quantity and type (refined sugars vs. whole grains), not carbohydrates as a category.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** the four biomolecules and state the monomer of each.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** which biomolecule a food is richest in if it contains nitrogen in every monomer.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why breaking polymers into monomers is essential before absorption.

---

### Concept: Carbohydrates
**Definition.** Carbohydrates are biomolecules made of C, H and O (usually H:O in a 2:1 ratio). They are classified as **monosaccharides** (single sugars, e.g. glucose, fructose), **disaccharides** (two sugars, e.g. sucrose, maltose), and **polysaccharides** (many sugars, e.g. starch, glycogen, cellulose).

**Functions:** immediate **energy source** (glucose), **energy storage** (starch in plants, glycogen in animals), and **structural** support (cellulose in plant cell walls).

**Real-world example.** Rice and potatoes store starch; the cellulose in vegetables provides dietary fibre.

**Analogy.** A monosaccharide is a single train carriage; a polysaccharide is a long train of many carriages linked together.

**Why it matters.** Carbohydrates fuel almost every cell, especially the brain and muscles during activity.

**Verbal diagram.** Picture glucose as a six-carbon ring. Two rings joined = a disaccharide (with water removed). Hundreds of rings branched or coiled = starch/glycogen.

**Misconception.** *"All sugars are the same."* Wrong — simple sugars are absorbed quickly (rapid energy spike), while complex polysaccharides release energy slowly, giving steadier fuel.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the three classes of carbohydrate with one example each.
- *(Apply)* **Outline** why an athlete eats pasta ("carb-loading") before a marathon.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Compare** the roles of starch and cellulose in a plant.

---

### Concept: Proteins
**Definition.** Proteins are polymers of **amino acids** containing C, H, O and **N** (nitrogen). Around 20 different amino acids combine in chains that fold into specific 3-D shapes.

**Functions:** **enzymes** (biological catalysts), **structural** (collagen, keratin, muscle), **transport** (haemoglobin carries oxygen), and **hormones** (e.g. insulin).

**Real-world example.** Egg white, meat, beans and lentils are protein-rich; muscle growth after exercise needs dietary protein.

**Analogy.** Amino acids are letters; a protein is a word whose meaning (function) depends on the exact sequence and folding.

**Why it matters.** Proteins do most of the "work" in cells; without dietary protein the body cannot grow or repair.

**Misconception.** *"More protein always builds more muscle."* Wrong — muscle growth needs training plus adequate (not excessive) protein; surplus is broken down or stored as fat.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the four elements found in all proteins.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the protein function represented by haemoglobin.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how a change in amino acid sequence could stop a protein working.

---

### Concept: Lipids (fats and oils)
**Definition.** Lipids are biomolecules made of C, H and O, built from **fatty acids and glycerol**. A **triglyceride** is one glycerol joined to three fatty acids.

**Functions:** long-term **energy storage** (more energy per gram than carbohydrate), **membranes** (phospholipids form cell membranes), **insulation** and protection of organs.

**Real-world example.** Butter and oils are lipids; blubber insulates whales; the fat layer under skin keeps humans warm.

**Analogy.** A triglyceride is like a capital "E" — the spine is glycerol and the three arms are fatty acid tails.

**Why it matters.** Lipids store energy compactly, cushion organs, and every cell is wrapped in a lipid membrane.

**Misconception.** *"All fats are bad for you."* Wrong — unsaturated fats (nuts, fish, olive oil) are essential; the concern is excess saturated and trans fats.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the building blocks of a lipid.
- *(Apply)* **Outline** two reasons a seal has a thick fat layer.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Discuss** why lipids store more energy per gram than carbohydrates.

---

### Concept: Nucleic acids
**Definition.** Nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) are polymers of **nucleotides** and contain C, H, O, N and **P** (phosphorus). Each nucleotide has a sugar, a phosphate group and a nitrogenous base.

**Function:** store and carry **genetic information** — the instructions to build proteins and to pass traits to offspring.

**Real-world example.** DNA in every cell nucleus determines eye colour and other inherited features.

**Analogy.** DNA is a twisted ladder (double helix); the rungs are pairs of bases spelling a coded instruction manual.

**Why it matters.** Nucleic acids are the information system that makes each organism unique and directs all cell activity.

**Verbal diagram.** Imagine two long strands twisting around each other (double helix). Between them, paired bases (A–T, C–G) form the rungs; a sugar-phosphate backbone forms the rails.

**Misconception.** *"DNA is only found in humans / only in the nucleus."* Wrong — all living cells contain DNA, and small amounts also sit in mitochondria (and chloroplasts in plants).

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the monomer of a nucleic acid and name the two types of nucleic acid.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** which element distinguishes nucleic acids from carbohydrates.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why nucleic acids are described as "information molecules."

## Chapter 1.2 — Nutrition, Diet and Food Choices

### Concept: Macronutrients vs. micronutrients & deficiency diseases
**Definition.** **Macronutrients** are nutrients needed in large amounts — **carbohydrates, proteins, fats** (plus water and fibre). **Micronutrients** are needed in tiny amounts — **vitamins and minerals**. A shortage of a nutrient causes a **deficiency disease**.

| Micronutrient | Main role | Deficiency effect |
|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Vitamin A | Vision, immune health | Night blindness |
| Vitamin C | Skin, healing, immunity | Scurvy (bleeding gums) |
| Vitamin D | Calcium absorption, bones | Rickets (soft/bent bones) |
| Calcium (mineral) | Bones, teeth, nerves | Weak bones, osteoporosis |
| Iron (mineral) | Haemoglobin in red blood cells | Anaemia (tiredness, pale) |

**Real-world example.** Sailors on long voyages without fresh fruit developed scurvy from lack of vitamin C.

**Analogy.** Macronutrients are the bricks and fuel of a building; micronutrients are the small screws and wiring — tiny, but the building fails without them.

**Why it matters.** Even a well-fed person can be ill if missing a single vitamin or mineral.

**Misconception.** *"If I eat enough food I can't be malnourished."* Wrong — you can eat plenty of calories yet lack specific micronutrients ("hidden hunger").

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** two vitamins and two minerals with one function each.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the likely deficiency in a child with soft, bowed leg bones.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why vitamin D and calcium deficiencies produce similar bone problems.

---

### Concept: A balanced diet, food groups and dietary requirements
**Definition.** A **balanced diet** provides all nutrients in the correct proportions and amounts to meet the body's needs. It is usually shown as a **food pyramid** or **food plate** dividing food into groups (grains, fruits, vegetables, protein foods, dairy, and small amounts of fats/sugars).

**Requirements vary by:**
- **Age:** infants need lots of energy and protein for growth; teenagers need calcium for bones; the elderly need fewer calories but adequate protein and calcium.
- **Gender:** menstruating females need more **iron** to replace blood loss.
- **Activity level:** athletes and manual workers need more energy (calories).
- **Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR):** the energy the body uses at complete rest just to stay alive (breathing, heartbeat, temperature). A higher BMR means higher baseline calorie needs.

**Real-world example.** A pregnant woman needs extra iron, calcium and folate; an office worker needs fewer calories than a construction worker of the same size.

**Analogy.** A balanced diet is like a balanced budget — spend (calories in) roughly matched to needs (calories out), with the right mix of "currencies" (nutrients).

**Why it matters.** Matching intake to individual needs prevents both deficiency and obesity.

**Misconception.** *"Everyone needs the same diet."* Wrong — needs change with age, sex, size and activity; a plan good for an athlete may overfeed a sedentary person.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** basal metabolic rate.
- *(Apply)* **Outline** why a menstruating teenage girl needs more iron than a boy of the same age.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Discuss** how activity level and BMR together determine daily calorie needs.

### Concept: Factors influencing food choices
**Definition.** Food choices are shaped by many factors beyond nutrition: **cultural/religious** rules (e.g. halal, vegetarianism), **personal** taste and ethics, **nutritional knowledge**, **socioeconomic** status (cost/income), **availability** (what is local/seasonal), and **marketing/advertising**.

**Real-world example.** A family may avoid pork for religious reasons, buy cheaper staples due to budget, and choose a cereal because of a TV advert.

**Analogy.** Food choice is like choosing clothes — need matters, but culture, cost, fashion (marketing) and availability all pull on the decision.

**Why it matters.** Understanding these factors helps explain unhealthy eating and how to design better food policy or education.

**Misconception.** *"People eat badly only because they lack willpower."* Wrong — cost, access and marketing strongly constrain choices, especially for low-income families.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** four factors that influence food choice.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** which factor best explains a sudden rise in sales after an advert.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** which factor most limits healthy eating in low-income communities.

### Concept: Reading nutrition labels
**Definition.** A **nutrition label** summarises a food's contents, including **serving size**, **calories (energy)**, **macronutrients** (carbs, sugars, protein, fat, saturated fat), **micronutrients** (some vitamins/minerals), and the **ingredients list** (in descending order by quantity).

**Real-world example.** A drink may look low-calorie until you notice the label lists two servings per bottle.

**Analogy.** A nutrition label is a "spec sheet" for food, like the specifications printed on a gadget's box.

**Why it matters.** Reading labels lets you compare products and control sugar, salt and calorie intake.

**Misconception.** *"The calories shown are for the whole packet."* Wrong — values are usually **per serving**, and one packet can contain several servings.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** three pieces of information found on a nutrition label.
- *(Apply)* **Calculate** the total sugar in a bottle if one serving has 15 g and the bottle holds 2.5 servings.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why serving size can make an unhealthy product look healthy.

---

### Concept: Marketing strategies & USP in food advertising
**Definition.** Advertisers use **marketing strategies** (celebrity endorsement, catchy slogans, health claims, appealing images, free gifts) and highlight a **Unique Selling Point (USP)** — the feature that makes a product stand out ("now with added vitamins", "sugar-free"). **Critical analysis** means questioning whether claims are true, exaggerated or misleading.

**Real-world example.** A sugary cereal advertised as "part of a balanced breakfast" places tiny print near colourful cartoon characters aimed at children.

**Analogy.** An advert is a highlight reel — it shows only a product's best moments and hides the rest.

**Why it matters.** Critical consumers resist manipulation and make evidence-based choices.

**Misconception.** *"If a food is advertised as healthy, it is healthy."* Wrong — claims like "low fat" can mask high sugar; always check the label.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** "unique selling point (USP)."
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the marketing strategy in an advert using a famous footballer.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** how a "90% fat-free" claim could mislead a shopper.

## Chapter 1.3 — Food Tests (Practical)

### Concept: Testing foods for nutrients
**Definition.** Chemical **food tests** detect specific biomolecules by a colour change or physical result.

| Nutrient tested | Test / reagent | Method summary | Positive result |
|-----------------|----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Starch | **Iodine** solution | Add iodine drops to food | Orange-brown → **blue-black** |
| Reducing sugar (e.g. glucose) | **Benedict's** solution | Add Benedict's, heat in water bath | Blue → green/yellow/**brick-red** |
| Protein | **Biuret** reagent | Add Biuret solution, mix (no heat) | Blue → **purple/lilac** |
| Fat/lipid | **Grease-spot** or **emulsion** test | Rub on paper / mix with ethanol then add water | Translucent spot / **cloudy white emulsion** |

**Practical notes (variables).**
- *Independent variable:* the food sample tested.
- *Dependent variable:* the colour/result observed.
- *Controlled variables:* volume of food, volume of reagent, temperature/heating time (for Benedict's), same observation lighting.
- *Control:* distilled water as a negative control (should show no colour change).

**Real-world example.** Iodine turning a potato slice blue-black shows it stores starch.

**Analogy.** Each reagent is a "detector" tuned to one nutrient, like a metal detector that beeps only for a specific metal.

**Why it matters.** These tests let scientists and students identify what a food contains without special machines.

**Misconception.** *"Benedict's works without heat."* Wrong — Benedict's test needs heating in a water bath; without heat the colour will not develop.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the reagent and positive result for the protein test.
- *(Apply)* **Describe** how you would test a slice of bread for both starch and reducing sugar.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why a control of distilled water improves the reliability of a food test.

## Chapter 1.4 — Teeth and Dental Health

### Concept: Structure of teeth and types of teeth
**Definition.** A tooth has an outer **enamel** (hardest layer), **dentine** (softer, sensitive layer beneath), a **pulp** cavity (nerves and blood vessels), **cementum** (covers the root), and a **root** anchoring it in the jaw.

**Types & functions:** **incisors** (biting/cutting), **canines** (tearing), **premolars and molars** (grinding/chewing).

**Verbal diagram.** From the top down: white enamel crown → yellowish dentine underneath → soft pulp in the centre → the root descending into the gum, coated in cementum and held by fibres.

**Real-world example.** You cut an apple with sharp incisors, then grind it with flat molars.

**Analogy.** A tooth is like a tree: the crown is the visible trunk, the root is buried underground, and the pulp is the living core carrying nutrients.

**Why it matters.** Different tooth shapes let humans eat a varied diet; enamel protects the living pulp.

**Misconception.** *"Enamel grows back if damaged."* Wrong — enamel has no living cells and cannot regenerate, so decay is permanent damage.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Label** the four main layers of a tooth.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** which teeth a carnivore uses most to tear meat.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why a cavity that reaches the pulp is painful whereas early enamel decay is not.

---

### Concept: Tooth decay and prevention (incl. fluoride)
**Definition.** **Tooth decay** occurs when **bacteria** in plaque feed on **sugar** and produce acid that dissolves enamel; **poor hygiene** lets plaque build up. **Prevention** includes **brushing**, **flossing**, and eating **less sugar**. **Fluoride** strengthens enamel and reduces decay; **water fluoridation** (adding fluoride to public water) is debated.

**Real-world example.** Frequent sugary drinks plus infrequent brushing lead to cavities.

**Analogy.** Bacteria + sugar = a tiny acid factory eating away at your enamel wall; brushing removes the factory's raw material.

**Why it matters.** Decay is largely preventable, and dental disease affects health, nutrition and confidence.

**Fluoride controversy (for Criterion D).**
- *Benefits:* fewer cavities across a population, cheap, especially helps those without dental care.
- *Concerns:* mass medication without individual consent; risk of dental fluorosis (mottled teeth) at high doses; debate over freedom of choice.

**Misconception.** *"Sugar directly rots teeth."* Wrong — sugar itself is harmless to enamel; it is the **acid produced by bacteria feeding on sugar** that causes decay.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** three causes of tooth decay.
- *(Apply)* **Outline** three habits that prevent decay.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Discuss** the arguments for and against adding fluoride to public water.

## Chapter 1.5 — The Digestive System

### Concept: Digestive processes and organs
**Definition.** Digestion converts food into absorbable nutrients through five processes: **ingestion** (taking in food), **digestion** (breaking it down), **absorption** (nutrients pass into blood), **assimilation** (cells use the nutrients), and **egestion** (removing undigested waste). Digestion is both **mechanical** (physical breakdown — chewing, churning) and **chemical** (enzymes breaking bonds).

**Parts & functions (in order):** mouth (chewing, saliva) → oesophagus (transports via peristalsis) → stomach (acid + enzymes churn food) → small intestine (most digestion and **absorption**) → large intestine (absorbs water) → rectum/anus (egestion). Accessory organs: liver (makes bile), gall bladder (stores bile), pancreas (releases enzymes).

**Real-world example.** A sandwich is chewed (mechanical), broken by enzymes (chemical), absorbed in the small intestine, and its waste egested a day later.

**Analogy.** The gut is a disassembly line: food enters whole, is taken apart station by station, useful parts are collected, and scrap is discarded.

**Why it matters.** Without digestion, food polymers are too big to enter cells — you would starve on a full stomach.

**Misconception.** *"Egestion and excretion are the same."* Wrong — **egestion** removes undigested food that never entered cells; **excretion** removes waste products made by cells (e.g. urea, CO₂).

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** the five processes of digestion in order.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** where most absorption occurs and one reason why.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Distinguish** between egestion and excretion.

---

### Concept: Villi — structure and function
**Definition.** **Villi** are tiny finger-like projections lining the small intestine that greatly increase surface area for **absorption**. Each villus has a thin wall (one cell thick), a network of blood **capillaries**, and a **lacteal** (for absorbed fats). Microscopic **microvilli** increase surface area further.

**Verbal diagram.** Imagine the intestine wall folded into countless finger-like bumps; each finger is carpeted with even smaller hairs, and inside each finger run tiny blood vessels and a lymph channel.

**Real-world example.** The huge folded surface of villi is why the small intestine, though only a few centimetres wide, has a surface area comparable to a tennis court.

**Analogy.** Villi are like the loops of a towel — thousands of small projections dramatically increase absorbing surface compared with a flat sheet.

**Why it matters.** Large surface area + thin walls + rich blood supply make absorption fast and efficient.

**Misconception.** *"Food is absorbed mainly in the stomach."* Wrong — most absorption happens in the small intestine because of the villi.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** two features of a villus that aid absorption.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** how a thin (one-cell) villus wall speeds absorption.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how villi and microvilli together maximise absorption efficiency.

### Concept: Digestive enzymes and reaction conditions
**Definition.** **Enzymes** are protein catalysts that speed up chemical digestion. Each enzyme acts on a specific **substrate**, converting it into **products**. Enzymes work best at an **optimal pH** and temperature.

| Enzyme | Substrate | Products | Optimal pH / site |
|--------|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Amylase | Starch | Maltose (sugar) | ~pH 7 (mouth) |
| Protease (e.g. pepsin) | Protein | Amino acids | ~pH 2 (acidic stomach) |
| Lipase | Fats (lipids) | Fatty acids + glycerol | ~pH 8 (alkaline small intestine) |

**Conditions affecting enzymes:**
- **pH:** each enzyme has an optimum; the stomach is acidic for pepsin, the small intestine alkaline for lipase — this is why pH differs along the gut.
- **Temperature:** rate rises with temperature up to ~37 °C (body temperature); too hot **denatures** the enzyme (permanent shape change) and it stops working.
- **Substrate concentration:** rate rises as substrate increases, then levels off when all enzymes are busy.

**Real-world example.** Amylase in saliva begins digesting starch — a cracker held in the mouth starts to taste sweet as maltose forms.

**Analogy.** An enzyme is a lock and the substrate its specific key; extreme heat or wrong pH bends the lock so the key no longer fits (denaturation).

**Why it matters.** Enzymes make digestion fast enough for life at body temperature; understanding their conditions explains gut design.

**Practical note — effect of temperature/pH on amylase.**
- *Independent variable:* temperature (or pH).
- *Dependent variable:* time for starch to be digested (tested with iodine, which stops going blue-black when starch is gone).
- *Controlled variables:* enzyme concentration, starch concentration, volumes.
- *Expected result:* fastest digestion near 37 °C / optimal pH; no digestion when denatured.

**Misconception.** *"A denatured enzyme will work again once it cools down."* Wrong — denaturation is permanent; the enzyme's shape (active site) is destroyed and cannot reform.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the substrate and products of lipase.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** why pepsin works in the stomach but amylase does not.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain**, in terms of the active site, why enzymes stop working above their optimum temperature.

## Chapter 2.1 — Photosynthesis

### Concept: Definition and equations of photosynthesis
**Definition.** **Photosynthesis** is the process by which green plants use **sunlight**, **water** and **carbon dioxide** to make **oxygen** and **sugar (glucose)**. It occurs in **chloroplasts**.

**Word equation:** carbon dioxide + water → (light + chlorophyll) → glucose + oxygen

**Chemical equation:** 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ *(in the presence of light and chlorophyll)*

**Real-world example.** A leafy tree removes CO₂ from the air and releases the oxygen we breathe.

**Analogy.** A chloroplast is a tiny solar-powered kitchen: it takes cheap ingredients (water, CO₂), uses sunlight as the cooker, and makes food (glucose), with oxygen as the "steam" released.

**Why it matters.** Photosynthesis is the origin of almost all food energy and atmospheric oxygen on Earth.

**Misconception.** *"Plants get their food (mass) mainly from the soil."* Wrong — most of a plant's mass comes from **carbon dioxide in the air** converted into sugar; soil provides water and minerals, not bulk food.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the balanced chemical equation for photosynthesis.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the source of the carbon atoms in glucose.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why photosynthesis is essential for both plants and animals.

---

### Concept: The role of chlorophyll
**Definition.** **Chlorophyll** is the green pigment in chloroplasts that **absorbs light energy** (mainly red and blue wavelengths, **reflecting green**, which is why leaves look green) and **converts light energy into chemical energy**.

**Real-world example.** In autumn, chlorophyll breaks down and leaves reveal orange/red pigments.

**Analogy.** Chlorophyll is a solar panel that catches sunlight and converts it into usable energy for the plant.

**Why it matters.** Without chlorophyll a plant cannot capture light energy, so photosynthesis cannot happen.

**Misconception.** *"Leaves are green because they absorb green light."* Wrong — leaves **reflect** green light (that's why we see green) and absorb red and blue.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the function of chlorophyll.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** why most leaves appear green.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Predict** how a plant kept under only green light might grow, and justify your answer.

---

### Concept: Use and storage of carbohydrates in plants
**Definition.** The glucose made in photosynthesis is used for **respiration** (energy) and **growth**, or converted to **starch** for storage in **roots, stems and fruits**. Glucose (soluble) is stored as **starch** (insoluble) so it doesn't affect water balance.

**Real-world example.** Potatoes store starch in underground stems (tubers); fruits store sugars.

**Analogy.** Converting glucose to starch is like turning spendable cash into a fixed savings deposit — stored compactly until needed.

**Why it matters.** Storage lets plants survive when light is unavailable (night, winter) and provides the food humans harvest.

**Misconception.** *"Plants store energy as glucose."* Wrong — they convert it to **starch** for storage because starch is insoluble and osmotically inactive.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** three uses of glucose in a plant.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** why glucose is converted to starch for storage.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Discuss** how a potato plant benefits from storing starch in tubers.

---

### Concept: Investigating the requirements for photosynthesis
**Definition.** Classic experiments test whether **chlorophyll, light and CO₂** are each necessary, using the starch test on leaves (boil in ethanol to remove chlorophyll, then add iodine — blue-black = starch = photosynthesis occurred).

**Practical designs.**
- **Chlorophyll needed?** Use a **variegated** leaf (partly white). Only the green parts turn blue-black with iodine.
- **Light needed?** Cover part of a leaf with foil; the covered (dark) part shows no starch.
- **CO₂ needed?** Enclose a plant with **sodium hydroxide** (absorbs CO₂); that leaf shows no starch.

**Variables (general).**
- *Independent:* the factor removed (chlorophyll / light / CO₂).
- *Dependent:* presence of starch (iodine test).
- *Controlled:* same plant, temperature, time, water; **destarch** the plant first (keep in dark 24–48 h) so any starch found is newly made.

**Real-world example.** A geranium destarched then partly covered with foil proves light is required.

**Analogy.** Removing one ingredient at a time is like unplugging one wire to find which one the machine needs.

**Why it matters.** Controlled experiments show which factors are essential and model good scientific method (Criteria B & C).

**Misconception.** *"You can test a leaf for starch without destarching first."* Wrong — pre-existing starch would give a false positive; the plant must be destarched so results are valid.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** why a plant is kept in the dark before a photosynthesis experiment.
- *(Apply)* **Design** a control to show light is needed for photosynthesis.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why a variegated leaf is ideal for testing whether chlorophyll is needed.

### Concept: Rate of photosynthesis and limiting factors
**Definition.** The **rate of photosynthesis** changes with **light intensity, CO₂ concentration and temperature**. A **limiting factor** is the factor in shortest supply that caps the rate — increasing other factors won't help until it is raised.

**How each factor acts:**
- **Light intensity:** rate rises with light, then plateaus when another factor becomes limiting.
- **CO₂ concentration:** rate rises with CO₂, then plateaus.
- **Temperature:** rate rises with temperature up to an optimum, then falls as enzymes denature.

**Greenhouses** optimise all three (extra light, added CO₂, controlled warmth) to maximise crop yield — of **economic** (more food/profit) and **environmental** importance (efficient land use, but energy cost).

**Practical note — rate using pondweed.**
- *Independent variable:* light intensity (change lamp distance).
- *Dependent variable:* bubbles of oxygen per minute (rate).
- *Controlled variables:* temperature, CO₂ (sodium hydrogen carbonate), same plant.
- *Expected result:* more bubbles closer to the lamp, plateauing when CO₂ or temperature limits.
- *Graphing:* plot rate (y) against factor (x); look for the plateau where a limiting factor takes over.

**Real-world example.** Tomato growers pump CO₂ and use grow-lights in winter greenhouses to raise yields.

**Analogy.** Limiting factors are like a chain — the weakest link (scarcest factor) sets the strength (rate) no matter how strong the others.

**Why it matters.** Understanding limiting factors improves food production and reveals how climate affects plant growth.

**Misconception.** *"More light always means more photosynthesis."* Wrong — beyond a point, CO₂ or temperature becomes limiting and extra light has no effect.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** "limiting factor."
- *(Apply)* **Interpret** a graph where rate plateaus despite rising light.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** the economic and environmental costs and benefits of greenhouse farming.

### Concept: Carbon footprint, the carbon cycle and climate change
**Definition.** A **carbon footprint** is the total greenhouse gases (mainly CO₂) released by a person, activity or product. Photosynthesis **removes** CO₂ from the air (part of the **carbon cycle**), so plants and forests offset emissions. Excess CO₂ enhances the greenhouse effect, driving **climate change**.

**Ways to reduce it:** use less fossil-fuel energy, travel efficiently, eat local/less meat, plant trees, reduce/reuse/recycle.

**Real-world example.** Flying long distances or eating out-of-season imported food raises your carbon footprint; planting trees lowers net CO₂.

**Analogy.** The carbon cycle is a bathtub — photosynthesis is the drain, burning fuel is the tap; too much inflow overflows the tub (warming).

**Why it matters.** Links plant biology to the biggest environmental challenge — a key Criterion D reflection.

**Misconception.** *"Only factories and cars have carbon footprints."* Wrong — everyday choices (diet, electricity, shopping) all contribute.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** carbon footprint.
- *(Apply)* **Outline** how photosynthesis connects to the carbon cycle.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** two actions a family could take to reduce its carbon footprint.

## Chapter 2.2 — Leaf Structure and Minerals

### Concept: Internal leaf structure and essential minerals
**Definition.** A leaf cross-section shows: **upper epidermis** (transparent protective layer, often waxy), **palisade mesophyll** (tightly packed cells full of chloroplasts — main site of photosynthesis), **spongy mesophyll** (loosely packed cells with air spaces for gas exchange), **lower epidermis** with **stomata** (pores) and **guard cells**. **Xylem** and **phloem** run in veins.

Plants also need **minerals**: **nitrogen (N)** for proteins/growth, **phosphorus (P)** for roots/energy, **potassium (K)** for flowers/enzymes.

| Mineral | Use | Deficiency symptom |
|---------|-----|--------------------|
| Nitrogen (N) | Proteins, leaf growth | Yellow older leaves, poor growth |
| Phosphorus (P) | Roots, DNA, energy (ATP) | Poor root growth, purple leaves |
| Potassium (K) | Enzymes, flowers/fruit | Yellow leaf edges, weak stems |

**Verbal diagram.** Top to bottom: waxy cuticle → upper epidermis → column-like palisade cells (packed with chloroplasts) → airy spongy layer → lower epidermis with stomata guarded by two curved guard cells.

**Real-world example.** Fertilisers are labelled "N-P-K" because these are the minerals crops need most.

**Analogy.** The palisade layer is the factory floor (most machines/chloroplasts); the spongy layer is the ventilation system letting gases move.

**Why it matters.** Leaf structure maximises light capture and gas exchange; minerals are needed for healthy growth even though they aren't made in photosynthesis.

**Misconception.** *"Minerals give plants energy."* Wrong — minerals are raw materials for building molecules; **energy** comes from glucose made in photosynthesis.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Label** the main layers of a leaf from top to bottom.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** why palisade cells are near the top of the leaf.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how nitrogen deficiency and light shortage could produce similarly poor growth.

## Chapter 2.3 — Movement In and Out of Cells

### Concept: Diffusion
**Definition.** **Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from a region of **higher concentration** to a region of **lower concentration** (down a concentration gradient) until evenly spread. It is a **passive** process (no energy needed).

Factors affecting rate: steeper concentration gradient, higher temperature, larger surface area, and shorter distance all speed diffusion.

**Real-world example.** Oxygen diffuses from air spaces in the leaf into cells; the smell of food spreads across a room.

**Analogy.** Diffusion is like a crowd leaving a packed room through open doors — people spread out until evenly distributed, no pushing required.

**Why it matters.** Diffusion moves gases (O₂, CO₂) and nutrients in and out of cells everywhere in living things.

**Misconception.** *"Diffusion needs energy from the cell."* Wrong — diffusion is passive; particles move on their own kinetic energy.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** diffusion.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** two factors that would speed up diffusion into a cell.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how a leaf's structure increases the rate of gas diffusion.

---

### Concept: Osmosis
**Definition.** **Osmosis** is the diffusion of **water** molecules across a **partially (semi-)permeable membrane** from a region of higher water concentration (dilute) to lower water concentration (concentrated).

Solution terms: **hypotonic** (more dilute than the cell — water enters, cell swells), **hypertonic** (more concentrated — water leaves, cell shrinks), **isotonic** (equal — no net movement).

**Practical note — potato osmosis.**
- *Independent variable:* salt/sugar concentration of the solution.
- *Dependent variable:* change in mass/length of potato chips.
- *Controlled variables:* size of chip, time, temperature, potato type.
- *Expected result:* chips gain mass in dilute (hypotonic) solutions, lose mass in concentrated (hypertonic) solutions, unchanged in isotonic.

**Real-world example.** Salting vegetables draws water out (hypertonic), making them limp; a wilted plant firms up in fresh water.

**Analogy.** A semi-permeable membrane is a fence with gaps too small for large solute "cows" but big enough for water "sheep" to pass through.

**Why it matters.** Osmosis controls how cells gain/lose water — vital for plant support (turgor) and animal cell survival.

**Misconception.** *"In osmosis the salt/sugar moves."* Wrong — it is **water** that moves across the membrane; the solute (usually) cannot pass.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** osmosis.
- *(Apply)* **Predict** what happens to a potato chip in pure water.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain**, using osmosis, why salted slug/vegetable loses water and shrivels.

## Chapter 2.4 — Transport in Plants

### Concept: Water uptake and the xylem
**Definition.** Water enters through **root hair cells** (long extensions that increase surface area) by osmosis, then travels up **xylem** vessels (hollow, dead, tube-like cells strengthened with lignin) from **soil → root → stem → leaves**. Evaporation from leaves creates a **transpiration pull** that draws the water column upward.

**Verbal diagram.** A root hair cell is a thin projection reaching between soil particles; xylem vessels are stacked hollow pipes running unbroken from root to leaf.

**Real-world example.** Celery placed in coloured water shows dye rising through the xylem.

**Analogy.** Xylem is a network of drinking straws; transpiration pull is the "sip" at the top that pulls water all the way up.

**Why it matters.** Water carries minerals, keeps cells turgid, and supplies photosynthesis.

**Misconception.** *"Plants pump water up using energy like a heart."* Wrong — water rises mainly by passive **transpiration pull** and cohesion, not by an active pump.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the function of xylem.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** how root hair cells are adapted for water uptake.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how evaporation at the leaves moves water up a tall tree.

---

### Concept: Translocation and the phloem
**Definition.** **Translocation** is the transport of **dissolved sugars** (and amino acids) made in the leaves to other parts of the plant, through **phloem** tissue (living sieve tubes with companion cells). Sugar moves from a **source** (where it's made/stored, e.g. leaves) to a **sink** (where it's used/stored, e.g. roots, fruits).

**Real-world example.** In spring, sugar stored in a tree's roots is translocated up to growing buds.

**Analogy.** Phloem is a two-way delivery service moving food from producers (source) to consumers (sink), unlike the one-way xylem "water main."

**Why it matters.** Translocation distributes energy to non-photosynthetic parts (roots, fruits, seeds) so the whole plant is fed.

**Misconception.** *"Xylem and phloem both carry water upward only."* Wrong — xylem carries water/minerals upward; phloem carries sugars in **either direction** (source to sink).

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** translocation.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the source and sink when a potato tuber is forming.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Compare** the structure and function of xylem and phloem.

---

### Concept: Transpiration
**Definition.** **Transpiration** is the loss of water vapour from a plant's leaves, mainly through **stomata**. It cools the plant, transports minerals, and drives water uptake. Excess water loss (more lost than absorbed) causes **wilting**.

**Factors increasing rate:** higher **temperature**, lower **humidity**, more wind, and more light (stomata open).

**Practical note — potometer.**
- *Purpose:* measures water uptake as a proxy for transpiration rate.
- *Setup:* a leafy shoot sealed into a water-filled tube; an air bubble's movement along a scale shows uptake speed.
- *Independent variable:* one environmental factor (e.g. temperature, humidity, wind).
- *Dependent variable:* distance the bubble moves per minute.
- *Controlled variables:* same shoot, leaf area, time.
- *Expected result:* faster uptake in hot, dry, windy conditions.

**Real-world example.** On a hot dry day, garden plants wilt because transpiration exceeds water uptake.

**Analogy.** Transpiration is like sweating — water evaporating from the surface both cools the organism and pulls more water up from within.

**Why it matters.** Transpiration powers water and mineral transport; understanding it explains wilting and irrigation needs.

**Misconception.** *"A potometer measures the exact amount of water transpired."* Wrong — it measures water **uptake**; a little water is used in photosynthesis/kept in cells, so uptake slightly overestimates or approximates transpiration.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Define** transpiration.
- *(Apply)* **Predict** how increasing humidity affects transpiration rate.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why a plant wilts on a hot, dry, windy day.

## Chapter 3.1 — The Respiratory System and Gas Exchange

### Concept: Respiratory system anatomy and physiology
**Definition.** The **respiratory system** brings air into the body and exchanges gases. Air passes: **nose/mouth → trachea (windpipe) → bronchi → bronchioles → alveoli (air sacs)** in the lungs. The **diaphragm** and **rib muscles** change chest volume to breathe (ventilation).

**Verbal diagram.** The trachea is a single tube branching into two bronchi (one per lung), which branch repeatedly into fine bronchioles ending in clusters of tiny alveoli, each wrapped in capillaries — like an upside-down tree ending in bunches of grapes.

**Real-world example.** When you inhale, the diaphragm flattens and ribs rise, drawing air into the alveoli.

**Analogy.** The lungs are like a tree of pipes ending in millions of tiny balloons (alveoli) surrounded by blood vessels.

**Why it matters.** Efficient gas exchange supplies O₂ for respiration and removes CO₂.

**Misconception.** *"Lungs actively suck in air by themselves."* Wrong — breathing is driven by the **diaphragm and rib muscles** changing chest volume and pressure; lungs have no muscle of their own.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** the structures air passes through from nose to alveoli.
- *(Apply)* **Describe** what the diaphragm does during inhalation.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how the structure of alveoli maximises gas exchange.

---

### Concept: Gas exchange in the lungs
**Definition.** In the **alveoli**, **oxygen diffuses** from air into blood capillaries, and **carbon dioxide diffuses** from blood into the alveoli to be breathed out. Alveoli are adapted: huge surface area, thin (one-cell) walls, moist lining, and a rich blood supply maintaining the concentration gradient.

**Real-world example.** Oxygen breathed in reaches the blood within seconds through alveolar walls.

**Analogy.** Alveoli are like a market where two goods are swapped at once — O₂ handed to the blood, CO₂ handed back to be exhaled.

**Why it matters.** Gas exchange delivers the oxygen every cell needs and removes waste CO₂.

**Misconception.** *"Lungs turn CO₂ into O₂."* Wrong — lungs do not convert gases; they simply allow O₂ in and CO₂ out by **diffusion**.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** which gas diffuses from alveoli into the blood.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** how a thin alveolar wall aids gas exchange.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how a rich blood supply maintains a steep concentration gradient for oxygen.

### Concept: Effect of exercise on breathing rate (investigation)
**Definition.** During exercise, muscles respire faster and need more O₂ while producing more CO₂, so **breathing rate and depth increase** to meet demand and clear CO₂.

**Practical note.**
- *Independent variable:* level/duration of exercise.
- *Dependent variable:* breaths per minute (and recovery time).
- *Controlled variables:* same person, rest between trials, same measurement method.
- *Method:* record resting breathing rate, exercise (e.g. step-ups) for set times, record rate immediately after; repeat and average.
- *Expected result/graphing:* breathing rate rises with exercise intensity; plot rate (y) against time or intensity (x).

**Real-world example.** After sprinting, you pant to repay oxygen and remove built-up CO₂.

**Analogy.** Exercising muscles are like a busier engine needing more fuel and air — the "intake" (breathing) speeds up to supply it.

**Why it matters.** Shows how the body responds to demand and links breathing to cellular respiration.

**Misconception.** *"You breathe faster after exercise only to get oxygen."* Wrong — you also breathe faster to **remove excess carbon dioxide** (and repay oxygen debt).

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** how breathing rate changes during exercise.
- *(Apply)* **Describe** a method to measure the effect of exercise on breathing rate.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why breathing stays fast for a while even after you stop exercising.

## Chapter 3.2 — Cellular Respiration

### Concept: Uses of energy from respiration
**Definition.** **Cellular respiration** releases energy from glucose, stored/carried as **ATP**, which powers cell processes. Uses include: **movement/muscle contraction**, **growth and repair** (building molecules), **active transport**, **maintaining body temperature**, and **nerve impulses**.

**Real-world example.** ATP from respiration powers your muscles when you run and keeps you warm at rest.

**Analogy.** ATP is like a rechargeable battery — respiration charges it, and cells "spend" it to do work.

**Why it matters.** Every living activity needs energy; respiration is the universal energy-release process in cells.

**Misconception.** *"Respiration and breathing are the same thing."* Wrong — **breathing** (ventilation) moves air; **respiration** is the chemical release of energy inside cells.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** three uses of the energy released by respiration.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the molecule that carries energy for cell processes.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Distinguish** between breathing and respiration.

---

### Concept: Aerobic respiration
**Definition.** **Aerobic respiration** releases energy from glucose using **oxygen**, producing carbon dioxide and water. It releases a large amount of energy.

**Word equation:** glucose + oxygen → carbon dioxide + water (+ energy)
**Chemical equation:** C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O (+ energy)

**Real-world example.** Sitting and walking use aerobic respiration, fully breaking down glucose.

**Analogy.** Aerobic respiration is like a clean, complete fire — plenty of oxygen means fuel burns fully, releasing maximum energy.

**Why it matters.** It is the body's main, most efficient energy supply.

**Misconception.** *"Respiration happens only in the lungs."* Wrong — aerobic respiration happens in **every cell** (in mitochondria), not in the lungs.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the word equation for aerobic respiration.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the waste products of aerobic respiration.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why aerobic respiration releases more energy than anaerobic respiration.

---

### Concept: Anaerobic respiration and oxygen debt
**Definition.** **Anaerobic respiration** releases energy from glucose **without oxygen**. In **humans (muscles)** it produces **lactic acid** and a small amount of energy.

**Word equation (humans):** glucose → lactic acid (+ a little energy)

Because glucose is not fully broken down, energy yield is low and **lactic acid** builds up, causing muscle fatigue. Afterwards, extra oxygen is needed to break down the lactic acid — the **oxygen debt** — which is why you keep breathing hard after intense exercise.

**Real-world example.** A sprinter's legs ache during a 100 m dash as lactic acid accumulates.

**Analogy.** Anaerobic respiration is like a fire with the window shut — burning is incomplete, gives less heat, and leaves smoky residue (lactic acid) to clear later.

**Why it matters.** It supplies quick energy when oxygen can't be delivered fast enough (intense exercise).

**Misconception.** *"Lactic acid is removed instantly when you stop."* Wrong — repaying the **oxygen debt** takes time, which is why heavy breathing continues after exercise.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the products of anaerobic respiration in humans.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** why a sprinter breathes heavily after the race (oxygen debt).
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Compare** the energy yield and products of aerobic and anaerobic respiration.

### Concept: The respirometer
**Definition.** A **respirometer** measures the **rate of respiration** by detecting oxygen used (or CO₂ produced) by living organisms (e.g. germinating seeds or small invertebrates). Oxygen use lowers gas volume/pressure, moving a coloured liquid or bubble along a scale (CO₂ is usually absorbed by soda lime/KOH).

**Practical note.**
- *Purpose:* measure respiration rate under different conditions.
- *Independent variable:* e.g. temperature, or organism type.
- *Dependent variable:* movement of liquid/bubble per minute (O₂ used).
- *Controlled variables:* mass of organisms, volume of apparatus, time.
- *Interpreting data:* greater/faster movement = higher respiration rate.

**Real-world example.** Germinating peas respire faster and move the fluid more than dormant seeds.

**Analogy.** A respirometer is a "breath-o-meter" — as organisms use up oxygen, the shrinking gas pulls the marker along like air leaving a balloon.

**Why it matters.** Provides direct evidence that living things respire and shows how conditions change respiration rate.

**Misconception.** *"The liquid moves because the organisms breathe out air."* Wrong — the liquid moves because **oxygen is consumed** (and CO₂ absorbed by chemical), reducing gas volume.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** what a respirometer measures.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** why soda lime/KOH is included in the apparatus.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Interpret** respirometer data showing faster fluid movement at higher temperature.

### Concept: The lime water test for carbon dioxide
**Definition.** **Lime water** (calcium hydroxide solution) is used to test for **carbon dioxide**. When CO₂ is bubbled through, clear lime water turns **cloudy/milky**. Exhaled air turns lime water cloudy faster than inhaled air, showing respiration produces CO₂.

**Practical note.**
- *Method:* breathe out through a straw into lime water; compare with air drawn in through separate lime water.
- *Expected result:* the lime water breathed into goes cloudy first, proving exhaled air is richer in CO₂.

**Real-world example.** Blowing through a straw into lime water in class turns it milky within seconds.

**Analogy.** Lime water is a CO₂ "detector" that fogs up in the presence of the gas, like a mirror misting with breath.

**Why it matters.** Gives simple evidence that respiration releases carbon dioxide.

**Misconception.** *"Lime water tests for oxygen."* Wrong — lime water specifically detects **carbon dioxide** by turning cloudy.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** the colour change of lime water with CO₂.
- *(Apply)* **Describe** how to show that exhaled air contains more CO₂ than inhaled air.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** how the lime water test provides evidence for respiration.

## Chapter 3.3 — Adaptations, Health and Movement

### Concept: Adaptations of the trachea
**Definition.** The **trachea (windpipe)** is adapted to keep the airway open and clean: **C-shaped rings of cartilage** hold it open (prevent collapse when breathing); **ciliated cells** (tiny hairs) sweep mucus and trapped dust/germs upward; **mucus** (from goblet cells) traps particles.

**Real-world example.** Dust breathed in is caught in mucus and swept up by cilia to be swallowed or coughed out.

**Analogy.** The trachea is like a flexible vacuum hose: rings stop it collapsing, and a moving "conveyor belt" of cilia and mucus carries dirt away.

**Why it matters.** These adaptations protect the lungs from blockage and infection.

**Misconception.** *"Cartilage rings are complete circles."* Wrong — they are **C-shaped** (incomplete) so the oesophagus behind can expand when swallowing.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **State** two adaptations of the trachea.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** the role of cilia and mucus in the trachea.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why the cartilage rings are C-shaped rather than complete.

### Concept: Effects of smoking
**Definition.** **Smoking** harms the respiratory system. Effects include: **tar** paralysing/destroying cilia (so mucus and germs build up → "smoker's cough", bronchitis); damage to alveoli causing **emphysema** (reduced surface area, breathlessness); **carbon monoxide** reducing the blood's oxygen-carrying ability; **nicotine** addiction; and increased risk of **lung cancer**.

**Real-world example.** Long-term smokers often develop a chronic cough and shortness of breath due to damaged cilia and alveoli.

**Analogy.** Tar clogging the cilia is like gumming up a conveyor belt — dirt can no longer be cleared, so it piles up.

**Why it matters.** Links lifestyle choices to health; strong Criterion D discussion on personal freedom vs. public health.

**Misconception.** *"Only the smoker is harmed."* Wrong — **second-hand smoke** harms non-smokers nearby, especially children.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** three health effects of smoking on the respiratory system.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** how tar leads to a smoker's cough.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Discuss** whether smoking should be banned in all public places.

### Concept: The skeletal system and movement
**Definition.** The **skeleton** supports the body, protects organs, allows movement, and makes blood cells. Major bones include the **skull** (protects brain), **ribs** (protect heart/lungs), **spine/vertebrae** (support, protect spinal cord), **femur** (thigh — largest bone), **humerus** (upper arm), **pelvis**.

Movement needs **bones, muscles, tendons and ligaments** working together: **muscles** contract to pull bones; **tendons** attach muscle to bone; **ligaments** attach bone to bone at joints. Muscles work in **antagonistic pairs** (e.g. biceps and triceps) — one contracts while the other relaxes.

**Verbal diagram.** At the elbow: the biceps (front) attaches by a tendon across the joint; when it contracts it bends the arm, while the triceps (back) relaxes; to straighten, the triceps contracts and biceps relaxes.

**Real-world example.** Lifting a bag: the biceps contracts (pulling the forearm up); lowering it, the triceps contracts.

**Analogy.** Muscles and bones are like ropes and levers — muscles (ropes) pull on bones (levers) that pivot at joints.

**Why it matters.** Explains how the body moves and why muscles must work in pairs (they can only pull, not push).

**Misconception.** *"Muscles can push bones as well as pull."* Wrong — muscles can only **pull** by contracting, which is why they work in antagonistic pairs.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **List** four functions of the skeleton.
- *(Apply)* **Explain** the roles of tendons and ligaments.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Explain** why muscles must work in antagonistic pairs to move a joint.

---

### Concept: Joints
**Definition.** A **joint** is where two or more bones meet, allowing movement (or holding bones firmly). Three main types:
- **Hinge joint** (e.g. elbow, knee) — allows back-and-forth movement in one plane, like a door hinge.
- **Ball-and-socket joint** (e.g. shoulder, hip) — allows movement in many directions (rotation).
- **Fixed/immovable joint** (e.g. between skull bones) — no movement, provides protection.

Joints contain **cartilage** (cushions bone ends), **synovial fluid** (lubricates), and are held by **ligaments**.

**Verbal diagram.** A ball-and-socket: the rounded head of one bone (the "ball", e.g. femur) sits in a cup-shaped socket (e.g. pelvis), allowing wide rotation.

**Real-world example.** Your shoulder (ball-and-socket) can circle fully; your knee (hinge) mainly bends and straightens.

**Analogy.** A hinge joint is a door hinge; a ball-and-socket joint is a joystick that moves in all directions.

**Why it matters.** Different joints allow the range of movements the body needs while protecting bones.

**Misconception.** *"All joints allow movement."* Wrong — **fixed joints** (like those in the skull) do not move; they protect and provide structure.

**Command-term questions**
- *(Recall)* **Name** the three main types of joint with one example each.
- *(Apply)* **Identify** the joint type at the knee and describe its movement.
- *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Compare** how a hinge joint and a ball-and-socket joint differ in movement.

# Subject-Wide: Common Misconceptions Master List

1. **"Carbohydrates are inherently fattening/unhealthy."** They are the body's main clean energy source; type and quantity matter, not the category.
2. **"All sugars/fats/proteins are interchangeable."** Simple vs. complex carbs, saturated vs. unsaturated fats, and different amino acid sequences behave very differently.
3. **"Eating enough calories means you can't be malnourished."** Micronutrient deficiencies ("hidden hunger") occur even with adequate calories.
4. **"Sugar directly rots teeth."** Bacteria produce acid from sugar; the acid causes decay. Enamel cannot regrow.
5. **"Egestion = excretion."** Egestion removes undigested food; excretion removes metabolic waste from cells.
6. **"A denatured enzyme recovers when cooled."** Denaturation permanently destroys the active site.
7. **"Plants get their mass/food from the soil."** Most mass comes from CO₂ fixed in photosynthesis; soil supplies water and minerals.
8. **"Leaves absorb green light."** They reflect green and absorb red/blue.
9. **"Plants store energy as glucose."** They store it as insoluble starch.
10. **"More light always increases photosynthesis."** Only until another factor becomes limiting.
11. **"In osmosis the solute moves."** Water moves across the membrane; solute usually cannot.
12. **"Plants pump water up using energy."** Water rises passively by transpiration pull and cohesion.
13. **"Breathing and respiration are the same."** Breathing moves air; respiration releases energy in cells.
14. **"Lungs convert CO₂ into O₂ / actively suck air."** Gases move by diffusion; breathing is driven by the diaphragm and rib muscles.
15. **"You breathe fast after exercise only for oxygen."** Also to remove CO₂ and repay the oxygen debt (clear lactic acid).
16. **"Muscles push and pull."** Muscles only pull (contract), so they work in antagonistic pairs.
17. **"All joints move."** Fixed joints (e.g. skull) do not.
18. **"Only the smoker is harmed by smoking."** Second-hand smoke harms others too.

- Acids, bases and the **pH scale** explain enzyme conditions and stomach acid; **catalysts** parallel enzymes.
- Combustion vs. respiration: both release energy from glucose/fuel using oxygen and produce CO₂ and water — a direct chemistry link.
- Chemical tests and colour-change indicators (iodine, Benedict's, Biuret, lime water) connect to qualitative analysis.

**PAHE (Physical & Health Education)**
- Diet, balanced nutrition, BMR and caloric needs directly support fitness and training planning.
- Effect of exercise on breathing/heart rate; aerobic vs. anaerobic respiration and oxygen debt explain warm-ups, endurance and muscle fatigue.
- Skeleton, joints, muscles, tendons and ligaments underpin movement, posture and injury prevention.
- Effects of smoking connect to healthy-lifestyle choices and well-being.

**Geography**
- The **carbon cycle**, **carbon footprint** and **climate change** link plant biology to global environmental systems and sustainability.
- Food security, agriculture and **greenhouse farming** relate to resource management and economic geography.
- Deficiency diseases and access to nutritious food connect to development, poverty and global health patterns (Fairness and development).
- Deforestation vs. photosynthesis and oxygen/CO₂ balance ties ecosystems to human impact on the environment.
