# IISH MYP Grade 7 Mathematics — Knowledge Base (2026–2027)

## Subject Overview

**MYP subject group:** Mathematics. In the IB Middle Years Programme, mathematics equips students to recognise structure, reason logically, model real situations, and communicate quantitative thinking with precision. Grade 7 sits in the middle of the MYP continuum: students consolidate number and algebra foundations while beginning to reason abstractly and to justify (not merely compute).

**The four MYP mathematics criteria (each marked out of 8):**

| Criterion | Title | What it assesses |
|-----------|-------|------------------|
| A | Knowing and understanding | Recall of facts/procedures; selecting and applying maths in familiar and unfamiliar situations; solving problems in mathematical and real-life contexts. |
| B | Investigating patterns | Applying mathematical problem-solving techniques to recognise patterns; describing patterns as relationships/general rules; verifying and justifying (proving) those rules. |
| C | Communicating | Using appropriate mathematical language, notation, forms of representation (symbols, diagrams, tables, graphs); moving between representations; coherent, complete, logical lines of reasoning. |
| D | Applying mathematics in real-life contexts | Identifying relevant elements of a real situation; selecting a model/strategy; applying maths to reach a solution; justifying the degree of accuracy and the validity/reasonableness of the solution. |

**Mathematics Related Concepts (drawn on across MYP):** Change, Equivalence, Generalization, Justification, Measurement, Model, Pattern, Quantity, Representation, Simplification, Space, System.

**Key Concepts most used in maths:** Form, Logic, Relationships (with Systems appearing frequently in this course).

**How to read this file:** Content is organised **Unit → Sub-topic (chapter) → concept entries**. Each unit opens with its conceptual framing (Key Concept, Related Concepts, Global Context, Statement of Inquiry, inquiry questions). Each concept gives a definition, a worked example, a real-world use, an analogy, method steps, at least one misconception, and three IB command-term questions. Math is written in plain notation (e.g., `(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2`).

# UNIT 1: Numerical Reasoning

**Key Concept:** Form
**Related Concepts:** Quantity, Equivalence, Representation, Simplification
**Global Context:** Globalization and sustainability (how quantities, rates and currencies let us trade, tax, grow and compare across a connected world).
**Statement of Inquiry:** Representing quantities in equivalent forms lets people measure, compare and make fair decisions about resources across a globalized world.

**Inquiry questions**
- *Factual:* How do we convert between fractions, decimals and percentages, and between units and time zones?
- *Conceptual:* Why can the same quantity be written in many equivalent forms without changing its value?
- *Debatable:* Is a percentage always the fairest way to describe a change or a comparison?

## Chapter 1.1 — Percentages

### Concept: Percentage as a form of a quantity (fractions → decimals → percentages)
**Definition:** A percentage is a fraction out of 100; it is one equivalent form of a proportion. `1/4 = 0.25 = 25%`.
**Worked example:** Convert 3/8. `3 ÷ 8 = 0.375 = 37.5%`. Reverse: `62% = 0.62 = 62/100 = 31/50`.
**Real-world use:** Discounts, exam scores, interest rates are all read as percentages.
**Analogy:** A percentage is like slicing any pizza into exactly 100 pieces so different pizzas can be compared piece-for-piece.
**Method:** fraction → divide top by bottom → decimal → ×100 → %. Reverse: % → ÷100 → decimal → simplify to fraction.
**Misconception:** "To make a decimal a percentage, just remove the decimal point." Students see 0.5 → 5%. Wrong because you must multiply by 100: `0.5 = 50%`. Corrective: `0.5 × 100 = 50%`, and `0.05 = 5%`.
**Questions:** (1) *State* 7/20 as a percentage. (2) *Calculate* the decimal form of 84%. (3) *Explain* why 1/3 gives a recurring decimal but 3/8 does not.

### Concept: Percentage increase and decrease (finding change and final value)
**Definition:** A percentage change scales a quantity up or down by a stated percent. Final = original × (1 ± rate).
**Worked example:** A €40 jacket rises 15%. Change `= 0.15 × 40 = €6`; final `= 40 × 1.15 = €46`. A €40 jacket falls 15%: final `= 40 × 0.85 = €34`.
**Real-world use:** Sale prices, salary rises, population growth.
**Analogy:** A multiplier is like a photocopier zoom setting — 115% enlarges, 85% shrinks.
**Method:** rate as decimal → multiply original by (1+rate) for increase or (1−rate) for decrease.
**Misconception:** "Add 15% then take 15% off returns the start." Wrong: `40×1.15×0.85 = 39.10`, not 40, because the second percentage acts on a different base.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the final value when 250 increases by 8%. (2) *Find* the change when 90 decreases by 30%. (3) *Analyse* whether a 20% rise followed by a 20% fall returns the original value; justify.

### Concept: Percentage change from original and final values
**Definition:** `Percentage change = (change ÷ original value) × 100`, where change = final − original.
**Worked example:** Price goes from 80 to 100. Change `= 20`; `% change = 20/80 × 100 = 25%` increase. From 100 to 80: `−20/100 × 100 = −20%`.
**Real-world use:** Reporting inflation, comparing this year's sales to last year's.
**Analogy:** Judging a change by the *original* is like measuring a growth spurt against a child's starting height, not the adult's.
**Method:** subtract to get change → divide by the ORIGINAL → ×100 → state increase/decrease.
**Misconception:** Dividing by the final value. Corrective: 100→80 is 20% decrease (÷100), but 80→100 is 25% increase (÷80) — the two are not equal because the bases differ.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the percentage change from 50 to 65. (2) *Determine* whether a rise from 200 to 250 or a fall from 250 to 200 has the larger percentage size. (3) *Evaluate* the claim "a 50% loss needs a 50% gain to recover."

### Concept: Reverse percentages (finding original from a percentage change + final)
**Definition:** When the final value already includes a change, divide by the multiplier to recover the original. `Original = final ÷ (1 ± rate)`.
**Worked example:** After a 20% discount, a bag costs €48. Original `= 48 ÷ 0.80 = €60`. After a 5% tax, a bill is €105 → pre-tax `= 105 ÷ 1.05 = €100`.
**Real-world use:** Working out the pre-VAT price, or a salary before a raise.
**Analogy:** Undoing a percentage is like reversing a recipe scaling to find the original portion.
**Method:** identify multiplier (1+rate or 1−rate) → divide the final value by it.
**Misconception:** Taking a percentage of the final price. Students do `48 × 1.20 = 57.60`. Wrong — the 20% was of the *original*. Corrective: `48 ÷ 0.8 = 60`, check `60 × 0.8 = 48`.
**Questions:** (1) *Find* the original price if €66 is the price after a 10% rise. (2) *Calculate* the pre-discount price when €153 follows a 15% reduction. (3) *Justify* why we divide rather than multiply to reverse a percentage.

### Concept: Applications — tax, commission, profit, loss, discount
**Definition:** Profit/loss and discount are percentage changes on a cost or marked price; tax and commission are percentages added/earned.
**Worked example:** Cost €200, sold €260 → profit `= 60`, `profit% = 60/200 ×100 = 30%`. Salesperson earns 4% commission on €5000 sales `= €200`.
**Real-world use:** Retail, salaries, government revenue.
**Analogy:** Profit percentage is a "score card" measured against what you paid, not what you charged.
**Method:** identify the base (cost price for profit/loss, marked price for discount) → apply percentage.
**Misconception:** Calculating profit% on selling price. Corrective: profit% is always on **cost price** unless stated otherwise.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the selling price for a €150 item marked up 20%. (2) *Determine* the loss percentage if a €90 item sells for €72. (3) *Discuss* whether "30% profit" and "30% discount" on the same number give the same money.

### Concept: Repeated percentage change (growth and decay)
**Definition:** Applying a percentage repeatedly compounds it: `final = original × (1 ± rate)^n`. The overall change is NOT n × the single change.
**Worked example:** €1000 grows 10% per year for 3 years: `1000 × 1.1^3 = €1331` (overall +33.1%, not +30%). A car worth €20000 depreciates 15%/yr for 2 yrs: `20000 × 0.85^2 = €14450`.
**Real-world use:** Compound interest, population growth, radioactive/asset decay.
**Analogy:** Interest on interest is like a snowball rolling downhill — each turn adds to a bigger ball.
**Method:** find multiplier → raise to power n → multiply original.
**Misconception:** "Three years of 10% = 30%." Wrong: compounding gives 33.1%. Corrective: `1.1^3 = 1.331`.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the value of €500 after 2 years at 6% compound growth. (2) *Compare* 3 years of 10% growth with a single 30% rise. (3) *Evaluate* which is better for a saver: 20% once or 10% twice.

## Chapter 1.2 — Numbers (Classification & Number Theory)

### Concept: Classification of numbers (natural, whole, integers, rational, real)
**Definition:** Naturals `{1,2,3,...}` ⊂ wholes `{0,1,2,...}` ⊂ integers `{...,−2,−1,0,1,2,...}` ⊂ rationals ⊂ reals. Reals = rationals + irrationals.
**Worked example:** −3 is an integer and rational but not whole; 2/5 is rational not integer; √2 is real, irrational.
**Real-world use:** Choosing a number type — you can have 3 children (natural) but a temperature of −4°C (integer).
**Analogy:** Number sets are nested Russian dolls; every natural sits inside all the larger sets.
**Method:** ask: negative? decimal/fraction? terminating or repeating? non-repeating infinite? → classify.
**Misconception:** "Zero is natural / all decimals are irrational." Corrective: 0 is whole not natural (IB convention); 0.75 is rational.
**Questions:** (1) *Classify* −7, 0, 3/4, √9, √3. (2) *State* the smallest set containing −5. (3) *Explain* why every integer is rational but not every rational is an integer.

### Concept: Rational number theory (p/q, q ≠ 0)
**Definition:** A rational number can be written as `p/q` with p, q integers and `q ≠ 0`. Its decimal terminates or recurs.
**Worked example:** `0.6 = 6/10 = 3/5`; `7 = 7/1`; `0.333... = 1/3`.
**Real-world use:** Money and measurements are rationals (2.75 m = 11/4 m).
**Analogy:** A rational is any number you can hit exactly by cutting a whole into equal parts.
**Method:** express as integer over integer; forbid zero denominator.
**Misconception:** "q can be any number." Corrective: `q ≠ 0` — division by zero is undefined.
**Questions:** (1) *Express* 0.45 as p/q in simplest form. (2) *State* why 5 is rational. (3) *Explain* why q cannot be 0.

### Concept: Rational vs irrational decimals; surds; and π
**Definition:** Rationals give terminating or recurring decimals; irrationals give infinite non-recurring decimals. A **surd** is an irrational root (e.g. √2, √3) left in root form. **π** (≈3.14159…) is a special irrational (the circle's circumference:diameter ratio).
**Worked example:** √4 = 2 (rational); √2 = 1.41421356… (irrational, a surd). π has no repeating block.
**Real-world use:** π appears in every circular measurement; surds appear in exact Pythagoras answers (√2 diagonal of a unit square).
**Analogy:** An irrational decimal is like a number that never lets you "land" exactly — it keeps running with no pattern.
**Method:** if a root is not a perfect square/cube it is a surd → irrational; leave exact or round as required.
**Misconception:** "π = 22/7." Corrective: 22/7 is only an approximation; π is irrational, 22/7 is rational.
**Questions:** (1) *Identify* which are surds: √9, √5, √16, √7. (2) *Explain* why √2 is irrational. (3) *Discuss* why π cannot be written exactly as a fraction.

## Chapter 1.3 — Ratio and Proportion

### Concept: Meaning of a ratio; ratio as fraction; ratio vs fraction
**Definition:** A ratio `a : b` compares two quantities part-to-part. As a fraction it is a part-to-whole idea; `a:b` means a is `a/(a+b)` of the total.
**Worked example:** Mixing paint 2:3 (blue:yellow). Blue is `2/5` of the mix, yellow `3/5` — the fraction 2/3 compares blue to yellow, not to the whole.
**Real-world use:** Recipes, map scales, screen aspect ratios (16:9).
**Analogy:** A ratio is a recipe rule that holds no matter how big the batch.
**Method:** read the order of the quantities; convert to fraction of total by summing parts.
**Misconception:** "2:3 means 2/3 of the total is blue." Wrong — 2/3 compares blue to yellow; blue is 2/5 of the total.
**Questions:** (1) *Write* the ratio 4:6 in simplest form. (2) *State* what fraction of a 3:5 mixture is the first part. (3) *Explain* the difference between the ratio 3:4 and the fraction 3/4.

### Concept: Simplifying ratios and finding quantities
**Definition:** Simplify by dividing all parts by their common factor; share a total by summing parts and scaling.
**Worked example:** Simplify 12:18 → ÷6 → 2:3. Share €50 in 2:3: parts total 5, one part `= 50/5 = 10`, so `€20 : €30`. Given the first share is €20 in 2:3, the other is `20/2 × 3 = €30`.
**Real-world use:** Splitting bills, dividing profits between partners.
**Analogy:** "Parts" are equal buckets; find one bucket, then fill the rest.
**Method:** sum the ratio parts → total ÷ sum = one part → multiply each part.
**Misconception:** Dividing the total by 2 for a 2:3 split. Corrective: divide by 5 (the number of parts), not 2.
**Questions:** (1) *Simplify* 30:45. (2) *Calculate* the two amounts when €120 is shared 5:3. (3) *Determine* the total when the larger part of a 2:7 share is 63.

### Concept: Proportion as variation
**Definition:** Two quantities are in (direct) proportion when their ratio stays constant: `y = kx`. As one grows, the other grows by the same factor.
**Worked example:** 3 pens cost €4.50, so 1 pen = €1.50 → 7 pens = €10.50. The unit rate k = 1.50 is constant.
**Real-world use:** Scaling recipes, currency conversion, unit pricing.
**Analogy:** Direct proportion is a fixed gear ratio — turn the pedal twice, the wheel turns twice as much.
**Method:** find the unit rate (k) → multiply by the new quantity.
**Misconception:** Adding a constant difference instead of multiplying. Corrective: proportion multiplies (×), it does not add a fixed amount.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the cost of 9 items if 4 cost €10. (2) *Determine* the constant of proportionality when y = 15 at x = 3. (3) *Explain* why "buy 2 get more expensive per unit" is NOT proportional.

## Chapter 1.4 — Conversions, Rates, Time & Recurring Decimals

### Concept: Unit conversions (length, area, volume, time)
**Definition:** Convert by multiplying/dividing by the conversion factor. Area factors are squared, volume factors cubed.
**Worked example:** `2.5 km = 2500 m`; `3 m² = 3 × 100² cm² = 30000 cm²`; `1 L = 1000 cm³`; `2 h 15 min = 135 min`.
**Real-world use:** Engineering, cooking, buying flooring by area.
**Analogy:** Converting units is swapping currencies of measurement at a fixed exchange rate.
**Method:** write the factor; length ×10/100/1000, area ×(factor)², volume ×(factor)³.
**Misconception:** "1 m² = 100 cm²." Wrong — it is `100² = 10000 cm²`. Corrective: square the linear factor.
**Questions:** (1) *Convert* 4.2 m to cm. (2) *Calculate* how many cm² in 5 m². (3) *Explain* why area conversions square the factor while length does not.

### Concept: 12-hour ↔ 24-hour clock and adding/subtracting time
**Definition:** 24-hour time labels the day 00:00–23:59; pm hours add 12. Time arithmetic carries in base 60.
**Worked example:** 3:45 pm = 15:45. A train leaves 14:50, journey 1 h 35 min → arrives 16:25 (`50+35 = 85 min = 1 h 25`).
**Real-world use:** Timetables, flight schedules, alarms.
**Analogy:** Minutes roll over at 60 like an odometer, not at 100.
**Method:** convert to 24-hour → add hours and minutes separately → carry 60 min = 1 h.
**Misconception:** Treating minutes as base 100 (50 + 35 = 85 "= 1.85 h"). Corrective: 85 min = 1 h 25 min.
**Questions:** (1) *Convert* 9:20 pm to 24-hour time. (2) *Calculate* the arrival time: depart 22:40, travel 2 h 50 min. (3) *Determine* the journey length between 08:15 and 13:05.

### Concept: Rates, unit rates, and converting rates & currencies
**Definition:** A rate compares quantities in different units (km/h, €/kg). A unit rate is "per one". Convert rates by converting each unit.
**Worked example:** 150 km in 2.5 h → `60 km/h`. At £1 = €1.17, £250 = `€292.50`. 72 km/h = `72000 m ÷ 3600 s = 20 m/s`.
**Real-world use:** Speed limits, shopping "best buy", exchanging money for travel.
**Analogy:** A unit rate is the "price tag per single item" that lets you compare unfairly-sized packs.
**Method:** divide to get "per one"; for currency multiply by the exchange rate; keep units in the working.
**Misconception:** Multiplying when you should divide converting to per-unit. Corrective: km/h → m/s multiply by 1000 and divide by 3600.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the speed for 210 km in 3 h. (2) *Convert* €90 to pounds at £1 = €1.20. (3) *Analyse* which is the better buy: 500 g for €2.20 or 750 g for €3.15.

### Concept: Work / Time / Distance–Time simple problems
**Definition:** `distance = speed × time`; rearrange for any variable. Work problems use rate × time = amount.
**Worked example:** A cyclist at 24 km/h for 45 min (0.75 h) covers `24 × 0.75 = 18 km`. Time for 60 km at 40 km/h `= 60/40 = 1.5 h`.
**Real-world use:** Trip planning, delivery estimates.
**Analogy:** The D=S×T triangle is a see-saw — cover any corner to find it from the other two.
**Method:** convert time to hours → pick the correct rearrangement → substitute.
**Misconception:** Leaving 45 min as "45" in km/h working. Corrective: use 0.75 h.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the distance at 50 km/h for 2 h. (2) *Find* the time for 30 km at 12 km/h. (3) *Evaluate* whether doubling speed halves the journey time.

### Concept: Time zones and calculating times across them
**Definition:** The world is divided into time zones offset from UTC; travelling east adds hours, west subtracts. This lets people coordinate globally.
**Worked example:** London (UTC+0) 12:00 → New York (UTC−5) is 07:00; → Tokyo (UTC+9) is 21:00. A 09:00 call in Delhi (UTC+5:30) is 03:30 UTC = 22:30 previous day in New York.
**Real-world use:** Scheduling international meetings, flights, live sport.
**Analogy:** Time zones are like everyone reading the same clock but each standing in a different "hour lane".
**Method:** find each zone's UTC offset → convert to UTC → add the target offset.
**Misconception:** "Every country is a whole number of hours from UTC." Corrective: India is +5:30, Nepal +5:45.
**Questions:** (1) *State* the time in Tokyo (UTC+9) when it is 10:00 UTC. (2) *Calculate* the New York time (UTC−5) for a 15:00 London meeting. (3) *Discuss* why time zones make global business scheduling difficult.

### Concept: Recurring decimals ↔ fractions; conditions for recurring
**Definition:** A recurring decimal has a repeating block; convert using algebra (×10^k, subtract). A fraction recurs when the simplified denominator has a prime factor other than 2 or 5.
**Worked example:** `x = 0.444...`; `10x = 4.444...`; `9x = 4` → `x = 4/9`. `0.272727... = 27/99 = 3/11`. `3/8` terminates (denominator 2³); `1/6` recurs (factor 3).
**Real-world use:** Understanding calculator rounding, exact fraction answers.
**Analogy:** Multiplying by 10 "lines up" the repeating tail so it cancels when subtracted.
**Method:** let x = decimal → multiply by 10^(length of block) → subtract → solve → simplify.
**Misconception:** "0.999... < 1." Corrective: `x=0.999..., 10x=9.999..., 9x=9, x=1` exactly.
**Questions:** (1) *Convert* 0.6 recurring to a fraction. (2) *Determine* whether 7/20 terminates or recurs, and justify. (3) *Explain* why 1/3 recurs but 1/4 terminates.

### Concept: Nearest perfect square via the range method
**Definition:** Estimate a square root by finding the two perfect squares it lies between.
**Worked example:** √50: since `7²=49` and `8²=64`, √50 is between 7 and 8, very close to 7 (7.07…). Nearest perfect square to 50 is 49.
**Real-world use:** Quick mental estimates, checking calculator answers, sizing a square area.
**Analogy:** Placing a number on a ruler between two known marks.
**Method:** list squares around the number → identify the bracketing pair → pick the closer.
**Misconception:** "√50 ≈ 25." Corrective: that halves it; √50 ≈ 7.1 because 7²=49.
**Questions:** (1) *State* the two integers √30 lies between. (2) *Estimate* √85 to one decimal place. (3) *Explain* the range method to a peer.

## Chapter 1.5 — Patterns and Number Sequences

### Concept: Identifying patterns and number sequences; stating the rule
**Definition:** A sequence is an ordered list following a rule (term-to-term or position-to-term/nth term).
**Worked example:** 3, 7, 11, 15, … adds 4 (term-to-term); nth term `= 4n − 1`. Check n=1 → 3. For 1, 4, 9, 16 the rule is `n²`.
**Real-world use:** Predicting seating rows, tiling, saving schedules.
**Analogy:** The nth-term rule is a "machine" — input a position, output that term directly.
**Method:** find the common difference d → nth term `= dn + (first term − d)`.
**Misconception:** "The rule is just +4" is enough. For the 100th term you need the nth-term formula, not repeated adding.
**Questions:** (1) *State* the next two terms of 5, 8, 11, 14. (2) *Find* the nth term of 2, 5, 8, 11. (3) *Determine* whether 100 is a term of the sequence 4n − 1; justify.

## Chapter 2.1 — Indices

### Concept: Laws of indices
**Definition:** Index (power) laws are rules for combining powers of the same base.

| Law | Rule | Example |
|-----|------|---------|
| Product | `a^m × a^n = a^(m+n)` | `2^3 × 2^4 = 2^7` |
| Quotient | `a^m ÷ a^n = a^(m−n)` | `x^5 ÷ x^2 = x^3` |
| Power of a power | `(a^m)^n = a^(mn)` | `(x^2)^3 = x^6` |
| Distributing | `(ab)^n = a^n b^n` | `(2x)^3 = 8x^3` |
| Zero power | `a^0 = 1 (a≠0)` | `5^0 = 1` |
| Negative power | `a^(−n) = 1/a^n` | `2^(−3) = 1/8` |

**Worked example:** `3x^4 × 2x^3 = 6x^7`; `12x^6 ÷ 4x^2 = 3x^4`; `(2x^2)^3 = 8x^6`.
**Real-world use:** Scientific notation, computing storage (2^10 bytes), growth models.
**Analogy:** Multiplying powers is stacking identical Lego blocks — you just count how many.
**Method:** same base? add (×) or subtract (÷) indices; power of power multiplies.
**Misconception:** "`a^m × a^n = a^(mn)`." Wrong — you ADD indices for a product: `a^2 × a^3 = a^5`, not a^6.
**Questions:** (1) *Simplify* `y^7 × y^2`. (2) *Evaluate* `(3x^2)^2`. (3) *Explain* why `a^0 = 1` using the quotient law.

### Concept: Fractional indices in radical form
**Definition:** `a^(1/n) = nth root of a`; `a^(m/n) = (nth root of a)^m`. The number under the root is the radicand.
**Worked example:** `9^(1/2) = √9 = 3`; `8^(2/3) = (cube root 8)^2 = 2² = 4`; `x^(1/2) = √x`.
**Real-world use:** Formulas for periods, areas, and scaling laws.
**Analogy:** A fractional index is a "half-power" instruction — square rooting is powering by one-half.
**Method:** denominator = root, numerator = power; take the root first, then the power.
**Misconception:** "`a^(1/2)` means `a ÷ 2`." Corrective: `9^(1/2)=3`, not 4.5.
**Questions:** (1) *Evaluate* `16^(1/2)`. (2) *Write* `x^(3/2)` in radical form. (3) *Explain* why `27^(1/3) = 3`.

## Chapter 2.2 — Manipulation with Brackets (Expanding)

### Concept: Binomial × binomial (area of a rectangle model)
**Definition:** Expanding `(x+a)(x+b)` multiplies every term in the first bracket by every term in the second; the rectangle-area model shows the four pieces.
**Worked example:** `(x+3)(x+5) = x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15`.
**Real-world use:** Finding areas of extended plots, algebra behind quadratics.
**Analogy:** Splitting a rectangle of sides (x+3) and (x+5) into four smaller tiles that sum to the area.
**Method:** use FOIL / grid → multiply all pairs → collect like terms.
**Misconception:** Only multiplying "firsts and lasts" (x² + 15). Corrective: include both middle products, 5x + 3x.
**Questions:** (1) *Expand* `(x+2)(x+6)`. (2) *Expand* `(x−4)(x+3)`. (3) *Explain* how the area model shows the middle term.

### Concept: Squaring a binomial and the key identities
**Definition:** `(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2`; `(a−b)^2 = a^2 − 2ab + b^2`; `(a+b)(a−b) = a^2 − b^2` (difference of squares).
**Worked example:** `(x+4)^2 = x² + 8x + 16`; `(x−3)^2 = x² − 6x + 9`; `(x+5)(x−5) = x² − 25`.
**Real-world use:** Fast mental squaring (e.g., 31² = (30+1)² = 961) and simplifying.
**Analogy:** `(a+b)²` is a big square split into two square tiles (a², b²) plus two identical rectangles (2ab).
**Method:** square first, square last, add twice the product (sign from the middle).
**Misconception:** "`(a+b)^2 = a^2 + b^2`." VERY common. Students distribute the power. Wrong because it drops the `2ab` cross-term. Corrective: `(3+2)² = 25` but `3² + 2² = 13`; the missing `2·3·2 = 12`.
**Questions:** (1) *Expand* `(x+7)^2`. (2) *Expand* `(2x−1)^2`. (3) *Analyse* the error in "`(a+b)^2 = a^2 + b^2`" with a numeric counterexample.

## Chapter 2.3 — Factorising

### Concept: Common-factor factorising (and negative common factors)
**Definition:** Factorising reverses expansion by pulling out the highest common factor (HCF). Terms decrease; factors increase.
**Worked example:** `ac − bc = c(a − b)`; `6x² + 9x = 3x(2x + 3)`; `−ac − bc = −c(a + b)`; `abc + bcd + bcf = bc(a + d + f)`. With 1: `x² + x = x(x + 1)`.
**Real-world use:** Simplifying formulas before substituting; solving equations.
**Analogy:** Un-distributing is repacking loose items back into a shared box (the common factor).
**Method:** find the HCF of all terms → divide each term by it → write factor(HCF)(remaining).
**Misconception:** Forgetting the invisible 1: `x² + x = x(x)` (dropping the +1). Corrective: `x(x+1)`, check by expanding.
**Questions:** (1) *Factorise* `8x + 12`. (2) *Factorise* `x² − x` fully. (3) *Explain* why factorising increases the number of factors but decreases the terms inside.

### Concept: Factorising by grouping and difference of squares
**Definition:** Grouping pairs terms to expose a common bracket; difference of squares uses `a² − b² = (a+b)(a−b)`.
**Worked example:** `ba + ac − db − dc = a(b+c) − d(b+c) = (b+c)(a−d)`. `x² − 16 = (x+4)(x−4)`.
**Real-world use:** Simplifying algebraic fractions before cancelling.
**Analogy:** Grouping is sorting mixed socks into pairs, then noticing every pair shares a common label.
**Method:** group in pairs → factor each pair → factor the common bracket. For a²−b²: square-root each term.
**Misconception:** "`x² − 16` doesn't factor because there's no middle term." Corrective: it is a difference of squares → `(x+4)(x−4)`.
**Questions:** (1) *Factorise* `xy + 2x + 3y + 6`. (2) *Factorise* `x² − 49`. (3) *Determine* whether `x² + 9` is a difference of squares; justify.

## Chapter 2.4 — Algebraic Fractions

### Concept: Meaning of an algebraic fraction; add/subtract with numeric denominators
**Definition:** An algebraic fraction has an algebraic expression in numerator/denominator. Add/subtract using a common denominator, like numeric fractions.
**Worked example:** `x/2 + x/3 = 3x/6 + 2x/6 = 5x/6`. `(2x)/5 − x/5 = x/5`.
**Real-world use:** Combining rates, proportions in formulas.
**Analogy:** You can only add slices of the same size — first cut both into the same size (common denominator).
**Method:** find the LCM of denominators → rewrite each fraction → add/subtract numerators.
**Misconception:** Adding numerators AND denominators: `x/2 + x/3 = 2x/5`. Wrong. Corrective: common denominator 6 → 5x/6.
**Questions:** (1) *Simplify* `x/4 + x/2`. (2) *Simplify* `(3x)/7 − x/7`. (3) *Explain* why you cannot just add the denominators.

### Concept: Multiplying and dividing algebraic fractions (with factorising)
**Definition:** Multiply straight across; divide by multiplying by the reciprocal; cancel common factors (factorise first if needed).
**Worked example:** `(x/3) × (6/x²) = 6x/(3x²) = 2/x`. `(x/4) ÷ (x/2) = (x/4)(2/x) = 2/4 = 1/2`. Using factors: `(x²−9)/(x+3) = ((x+3)(x−3))/(x+3) = x−3`.
**Real-world use:** Simplifying compound rate and scale expressions.
**Analogy:** Dividing by a fraction is asking "how many of these fit?" — flip and multiply.
**Method:** factorise → cancel common factors → multiply (flip the divisor when dividing).
**Misconception:** Cancelling terms not factors: `(x+3)/3 = x` (cancelling the 3s). Wrong — you may only cancel whole factors, not parts of a sum.
**Questions:** (1) *Simplify* `(x/5) × (10/x)`. (2) *Simplify* `((x+2)(x−1))/(x+2)`. (3) *Analyse* why `(x+3)/3` cannot be simplified to `x+1`.

## Chapter 2.5 — Linear Equations in One Variable

### Concept: Expressions vs equations; LCM/HCF of algebraic terms
**Definition:** An expression has no equals sign (it can be simplified); an equation states two expressions are equal (it can be solved). HCF/LCM extend to algebraic terms.
**Worked example:** `3x + 2` is an expression; `3x + 2 = 11` is an equation (x = 3). HCF of `6x²` and `9x` is `3x`; LCM is `18x²`.
**Real-world use:** Knowing when there is something to "solve" versus "simplify".
**Analogy:** An expression is a phrase; an equation is a full sentence making a claim.
**Method:** look for `=`. HCF: lowest powers × number HCF; LCM: highest powers × number LCM.
**Misconception:** "You can solve `3x + 2`." Corrective: with no equals sign there is nothing to solve — only simplify.
**Questions:** (1) *State* whether `5x − 1` is an expression or equation. (2) *Find* the HCF of `8x³` and `12x²`. (3) *Explain* the difference between simplifying and solving.

### Concept: The balance-scale model and forming equations
**Definition:** An equation is a balance; do the same to both sides to keep it level. Real situations can be modelled as equations.
**Worked example:** `x + 3 = 7` → subtract 3 → `x = 4`. `3x = 9` → ÷3 → `x = 3`. `x/3 = 5` → ×3 → `x = 15`. Model: "5 more than twice a number is 17" → `2x + 5 = 17`.
**Real-world use:** Turning word problems (costs, ages, totals) into solvable equations.
**Analogy:** A pan balance — remove 3 kg from one side, remove 3 kg from the other to stay level.
**Method:** identify the unknown → build the equation from the words → keep both sides balanced.
**Misconception:** Changing only one side. Corrective: whatever you do to one side you must do to the other.
**Questions:** (1) *Solve* `x − 3 = 5`. (2) *Form* an equation for "a number tripled equals 21" and solve. (3) *Explain* why both sides must change together.

### Concept: Solving by transposition (multiple steps, with brackets)
**Definition:** Transposition moves terms across the equals sign, changing their operation to the inverse, to isolate the variable.
**Worked example:** `2(x + 3) = 16` → expand `2x + 6 = 16` → `2x = 10` → `x = 5`. `5x − 4 = 3x + 8` → `2x = 12` → `x = 6`.
**Real-world use:** Rearranging any formula to solve for a needed quantity.
**Analogy:** Moving a term across the "=" is like moving a weight to the other pan — it flips its effect.
**Method:** expand brackets → collect variables one side, numbers the other → divide by the coefficient.
**Misconception:** Not flipping the sign when moving: `2x + 6 = 16 → 2x = 16 + 6`. Corrective: subtract 6 from both sides → `2x = 10`.
**Questions:** (1) *Solve* `3x + 4 = 19`. (2) *Solve* `4(x − 2) = 12`. (3) *Solve and check* `7x − 5 = 2x + 15`.

## Chapter 3.1 — Angles of Quadrilaterals

### Concept: Angle sum of a quadrilateral (via two triangles)
**Definition:** Any quadrilateral splits into two triangles by a diagonal; since each triangle's angles sum to 180°, a quadrilateral's interior angles sum to `360°`.
**Worked example:** Three angles are 100°, 85°, 95°; the fourth `= 360 − 280 = 80°`.
**Real-world use:** Checking a window frame or tile is a true quadrilateral.
**Analogy:** Cutting a quadrilateral corner-to-corner makes two triangular "pizza slices" of 180° each.
**Method:** sum known angles → subtract from 360.
**Misconception:** "Angles of a quadrilateral sum to 180°." Corrective: 180° is a triangle; a quadrilateral is two triangles = 360°.
**Questions:** (1) *State* the interior angle sum of a quadrilateral. (2) *Find* the missing angle given 70°, 110°, 90°. (3) *Explain*, using triangles, why the sum is 360°.

### Concept: Classifying quadrilaterals and their properties
**Definition:** Squares, rectangles, rhombi, parallelograms, kites and trapezia are classified by sides, angles, parallelism and diagonals.
**Worked example:** A rhombus has 4 equal sides and diagonals that bisect each other at right angles; a kite has two pairs of adjacent equal sides and one diagonal bisecting the other; a trapezium has exactly one pair of parallel sides.
**Real-world use:** Recognising shapes in design, architecture, kites and signs.
**Analogy:** A family tree: a square is a special rectangle which is a special parallelogram.
**Method:** check sides equal? parallel? angles right? diagonals' behaviour → name it.
**Misconception:** "A square is not a rectangle." Corrective: a square satisfies all rectangle properties, so it IS a special rectangle.
**Questions:** (1) *State* two properties of a rhombus. (2) *Classify* a shape with one pair of parallel sides. (3) *Explain* why every square is also a parallelogram.

### Concept: Congruence and the mid-point theorem
**Definition:** Congruent figures are identical in shape and size (SSS, SAS, ASA, RHS). The **mid-point theorem**: the segment joining midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and half its length.
**Worked example:** In triangle ABC, D and E are midpoints of AB and AC → `DE ∥ BC` and `DE = ½ BC`; if BC = 12, DE = 6.
**Real-world use:** Proving structures identical (manufacturing), scaling in design.
**Analogy:** Congruent triangles are like two keys cut from the same template — interchangeable.
**Method:** match equal sides/angles to a congruence rule; for midpoints apply DE = ½ (third side).
**Misconception:** "Same angles = congruent." Corrective: equal angles give *similar* (same shape) but not necessarily congruent (same size).
**Questions:** (1) *State* the four triangle congruence conditions. (2) *Calculate* DE if the joined midpoints face a side of length 18. (3) *Justify* whether two triangles with all angles equal must be congruent.

## Chapter 3.2 — Angles of Polygons

### Concept: Interior and exterior angles of polygons
**Definition:** A polygon of n sides splits into (n−2) triangles, so interior angles sum to `(n−2)×180°`. Exterior angles always sum to `360°`. Regular = all sides/angles equal.
**Worked example:** Hexagon interior sum `= (6−2)×180 = 720°`; each interior angle of a regular hexagon `= 720/6 = 120°`; each exterior `= 360/6 = 60°`. Irregular pentagon missing angle: sum 540°, known 100+120+90+110 = 420 → missing 120°.
**Real-world use:** Tiling, floor patterns, nut/bolt design, football panels.
**Analogy:** Walking around a polygon, the exterior turns always add up to one full 360° spin.
**Method:** interior sum `(n−2)180`; regular interior `= sum/n`; exterior `= 360/n`.
**Misconception:** "Exterior angles sum depends on n." Corrective: exterior sum is always 360°, for every convex polygon.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the interior angle sum of an octagon. (2) *Find* each interior angle of a regular pentagon. (3) *Explain* why exterior angles always sum to 360°.

## Chapter 3.3 — Perimeter and Area

### Concept: Areas of quadrilaterals (parallelogram, rhombus, kite, trapezium)
**Definition:** Area formulas: parallelogram `= base × height`; rhombus/kite `= ½ × d1 × d2` (diagonals); trapezium `= ½(a+b) × h`.
**Worked example:** Parallelogram base 8, height 5 → `40`. Kite diagonals 6 and 10 → `½×6×10 = 30`. Trapezium parallel sides 7 and 11, height 4 → `½(18)(4) = 36`.
**Real-world use:** Land area, flooring, fabric cutting, roof panels.
**Analogy:** A parallelogram is a rectangle with a triangle slid from one end to the other — same area, `base × height`.
**Method:** identify the shape → pick the formula → use PERPENDICULAR height, not the slant side.
**Misconception:** Using the slanted side as the height of a parallelogram. Corrective: height is the perpendicular distance between the parallel sides.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the area of a trapezium with parallel sides 5 and 9, height 6. (2) *Find* a rhombus area with diagonals 8 and 12. (3) *Explain* why a parallelogram uses perpendicular height.

### Concept: Compound shapes and circles (incl. concentric circles)
**Definition:** Compound areas are found by splitting into known shapes and adding, or subtracting a hole. Circle: `area = πr²`, `circumference = 2πr`. Concentric circles share a centre; a ring (annulus) area `= π(R² − r²)`.
**Worked example:** L-shape = big rectangle − missing rectangle. Ring with R=5, r=3: `π(25−9) = 16π ≈ 50.3`.
**Real-world use:** Flooring odd rooms, washers, running tracks, garden paths.
**Analogy:** Compound area is like weighing a sculpture by adding up its simple blocks.
**Method:** decompose → find each area → add or subtract as the picture demands.
**Misconception:** Using diameter for r in `πr²`. Corrective: r is the radius (half the diameter).
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the area of a circle radius 7 (use π ≈ 3.14). (2) *Find* an annulus area for R=6, r=4. (3) *Determine* the area of an L-shape you split into rectangles.

### Concept: Constructions (compass and ruler only)
**Definition:** Geometric constructions build triangles, angle bisectors and perpendiculars using only compass and straightedge.
**Worked example:** To bisect an angle: arc from the vertex cutting both arms, then equal arcs from those two points intersect; the vertex-to-intersection line bisects the angle.
**Real-world use:** Technical drawing, engineering, drafting without measuring.
**Analogy:** A compass guarantees equal distances the way a fixed string keeps a goat the same distance from its post.
**Method:** keep the compass width fixed for equal radii; intersecting arcs locate exact points.
**Misconception:** "Just estimate the halfway line." Corrective: construction gives an exact bisector, not an eyeballed one.
**Questions:** (1) *Describe* the steps to bisect an angle. (2) *Construct* a perpendicular bisector of a line segment. (3) *Explain* why equal compass radii guarantee accuracy.

## Chapter 3.4 — 3D Shapes

### Concept: Nets, surface area and volume of prisms and cylinders
**Definition:** A net is the 2D unfolding of a 3D solid. Surface area = total area of the net. Volume of a prism `= cross-sectional area × length`; cylinder volume `= πr²h`, surface area `= 2πr² + 2πrh`.
**Worked example:** Cuboid 3×4×5: volume `= 60`; surface area `= 2(3·4 + 4·5 + 3·5) = 2(12+20+15) = 94`. Cylinder r=2, h=10: volume `= π(4)(10) = 40π ≈ 125.7`.
**Real-world use:** Packaging design, tank capacities, paint quantities.
**Analogy:** A net is the "flattened cardboard box" you fold back into the solid.
**Method:** volume — area of base × height/length; surface area — add every face of the net.
**Misconception:** Confusing surface area with volume. Corrective: surface area is a 2D "wrapping" (units²); volume is 3D "filling" (units³).
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the volume of a cuboid 2×5×6. (2) *Find* the surface area of a cube of side 4. (3) *Explain* the difference between a solid's surface area and volume.

### Concept: 3D diagonals and Pythagoras in solids
**Definition:** The space diagonal of a cuboid uses Pythagoras twice: `d = √(l² + w² + h²)`.
**Worked example:** Cuboid 3×4×12: `d = √(9+16+144) = √169 = 13`.
**Real-world use:** Longest object fitting in a box, cable runs across a room.
**Analogy:** Reaching corner-to-corner through a room is a triangle standing on the floor's diagonal.
**Method:** find the base diagonal (Pythagoras) → combine with the height (Pythagoras again).
**Misconception:** Adding the three edges: `3+4+12 = 19`. Corrective: use the square-root formula → 13.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the space diagonal of a 2×3×6 cuboid. (2) *Find* the longest rod fitting in a 6×8×10 box. (3) *Explain* why the 3D diagonal uses Pythagoras twice.

## Chapter 3.5 — Pythagoras' Theorem

### Concept: Pythagoras' theorem and Pythagorean triples
**Definition:** In a right-angled triangle, `a² + b² = c²`, where c is the hypotenuse. Integer sets satisfying this (3,4,5; 5,12,13; 8,15,17) are Pythagorean triples.
**Worked example:** Legs 6 and 8: `c = √(36+64) = √100 = 10`. Given hypotenuse 13 and leg 5: other leg `= √(169−25) = √144 = 12`.
**Real-world use:** Checking a corner is square (3-4-5 method), ladder lengths, navigation.
**Analogy:** The square built on the longest side has exactly the same area as the other two squares combined.
**Method:** identify the hypotenuse (opposite the right angle) → add squares of legs for c, subtract for a leg.
**Misconception:** Treating any side as the hypotenuse. Corrective: c is always the longest side, opposite the 90° angle.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the hypotenuse for legs 9 and 12. (2) *Find* the missing leg if the hypotenuse is 25 and one leg is 7. (3) *Determine* whether 6, 8, 11 form a right triangle; justify.

## Chapter 3.6 — Coordinates

### Concept: The coordinate plane — plotting and reading points
**Definition:** The Cartesian plane uses an x-axis (horizontal) and y-axis (vertical) meeting at the origin (0,0). A point is `(x, y)`: across then up.
**Worked example:** (3, 2) is 3 right, 2 up; (−4, 1) is 4 left, 1 up; the point 5 left and 3 down of origin is (−5, −3).
**Real-world use:** Maps, GPS, screen pixels, graphs of data.
**Analogy:** Coordinates are like giving a seat as "row and column" in a cinema.
**Method:** draw and label axes → read x first, then y → plot across then up/down.
**Misconception:** Reading y before x. Corrective: always (x, y) — horizontal first.
**Questions:** (1) *State* the coordinates of a point 2 left, 4 up of the origin. (2) *Plot* (−3, −2) and name its quadrant. (3) *Explain* why (2, 5) and (5, 2) are different points.

## Chapter 4.1 — Probability

### Concept: Probability, its scale, and simple events
**Definition:** Probability measures how likely an event is, from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). `P(event) = favourable outcomes ÷ total outcomes` (theoretical). It can be written as a fraction, decimal or percentage.
**Worked example:** Rolling a 4 on a fair die: `P = 1/6 ≈ 0.17 = 17%`. P(even) `= 3/6 = 1/2`. Terms: 0 impossible, 0.5 even chance, 1 certain.
**Real-world use:** Weather forecasts, insurance, games, risk.
**Analogy:** The probability scale is a ruler from "no way" (0) to "for sure" (1).
**Method:** list the sample space → count favourable → divide by total → convert form as needed.
**Misconception:** "Probability can be 2 or 150%." Corrective: probabilities lie in 0–1 (0–100%); never above 1.
**Questions:** (1) *State* the probability of rolling a 7 on a normal die. (2) *Calculate* P(heart) from a 52-card deck. (3) *Explain* the difference between "unlikely" and "impossible".

### Concept: Sample space, trials, theoretical vs experimental probability
**Definition:** A trial is one performance of an experiment; the sample space is the set of all possible outcomes. Theoretical probability is predicted; experimental is observed (`frequency ÷ number of trials`).
**Worked example:** Sample space of one coin = {H, T}. Tossing 50 times gives 28 heads → experimental `P(H) = 28/50 = 0.56`, near the theoretical 0.5.
**Real-world use:** Testing whether a die or coin is fair; quality control.
**Analogy:** Theoretical is the recipe's promised servings; experimental is what you actually got when you cooked.
**Method:** theoretical from equally likely outcomes; experimental from recorded results; more trials → closer to theoretical.
**Misconception:** "Experimental must equal theoretical." Corrective: they only converge over many trials; short runs vary.
**Questions:** (1) *List* the sample space for rolling one die. (2) *Calculate* the experimental probability of a 6 seen 9 times in 60 rolls. (3) *Discuss* why experimental probability approaches theoretical as trials increase.

## Chapter 4.2 — Data Types and Tabulation

### Concept: Qualitative vs quantitative; discrete vs continuous; frequency tables
**Definition:** Qualitative data describes categories (colour); quantitative is numeric. Quantitative is discrete (counts, e.g. goals) or continuous (measured, e.g. height). Tally/frequency tables organise raw data; continuous data is grouped into classes.
**Worked example:** Eye colour = qualitative; number of siblings = discrete; mass in kg = continuous. Grouping heights: 150–159, 160–169, … with tallies and a cumulative frequency column.
**Real-world use:** Surveys, census, scientific measurement.
**Analogy:** Discrete data are whole steps on a staircase; continuous data is a ramp with every value in between.
**Method:** decide category vs number → if number, can it take in-between values? continuous : discrete → tabulate.
**Misconception:** "Shoe size is continuous." Corrective: shoe size comes in fixed steps → discrete; foot length is continuous.
**Questions:** (1) *Classify* "favourite sport" and "time to run 100 m". (2) *State* whether number of cars is discrete or continuous. (3) *Explain* why age can be treated as continuous.

## Chapter 4.3 — Representing and Summarising Data

### Concept: Data representations (tables, pictograms, bar charts, stem-and-leaf)
**Definition:** Raw data is processed into displays: frequency tables/tally, pictograms (a symbol = a number), bar charts (discrete), and stem-and-leaf (keeps original values while showing shape).
**Worked example:** Scores 21, 24, 24, 31, 35 → stem-and-leaf: 2 | 1 4 4  ;  3 | 1 5 (key 2|1 = 21). A pictogram where one star = 10 uses 3.5 stars for 35.
**Real-world use:** Reports, dashboards, newspapers.
**Analogy:** A stem-and-leaf is a bar chart that still remembers the exact numbers.
**Method:** pick the display for the data type → keep scales/keys consistent → label axes.
**Misconception:** Using a bar chart with gaps for continuous data. Corrective: continuous data uses a histogram (bars touch).
**Questions:** (1) *Draw* a stem-and-leaf for 12, 15, 15, 23, 29. (2) *State* what one symbol represents in a given pictogram. (3) *Explain* why the choice of scale can mislead a reader.

### Concept: Central tendency — mean, median, mode (raw & from frequency tables)
**Definition:** Mean = sum ÷ count; median = middle value when ordered; mode = most frequent. From a frequency table the mean `= Σ(fx) ÷ Σf`.

| Measure | Best when | Affected by outliers? |
|---------|-----------|-----------------------|
| Mean | data fairly symmetric | Yes |
| Median | skewed data / outliers present | No |
| Mode | categorical / most common | No |

**Worked example:** Data 4, 6, 6, 8, 11: mean `= 35/5 = 7`; median `= 6`; mode `= 6`. Frequency table x=1,2,3 with f=2,5,3 → mean `= (1·2+2·5+3·3)/10 = 21/10 = 2.1`.
**Real-world use:** Average income (median avoids distortion by billionaires), exam averages, best-selling size (mode).
**Analogy:** The mean is the "balance point" of the data; the median is the "middle of the queue".
**Method:** order the data → mean adds and divides; median finds the middle; mode counts frequency.
**Misconception:** "The median is the middle of the unordered list." Corrective: you must sort first.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the mean, median and mode of 3, 5, 5, 9, 13. (2) *Find* the mean from a given frequency table. (3) *Discuss* which average best describes house prices and why.

### Concept: Dispersion — range, mean deviation, standard deviation; histograms & ogives
**Definition:** Dispersion measures spread. Range `= max − min`. Mean deviation = average distance from the mean. Standard deviation measures typical spread about the mean (larger = more variable). A histogram shows grouped continuous frequencies (bars touch); an ogive is the cumulative-frequency curve.
**Worked example:** Data 4, 6, 8, 10, 12: mean 8, range `= 12−4 = 8`; deviations 4,2,0,2,4 → mean deviation `= 12/5 = 2.4`. An ogive lets you read the median at the cumulative frequency = n/2 point.
**Real-world use:** Comparing consistency (two players with the same mean but different spread), quality control, exam grade boundaries.
**Analogy:** Two classes can have the same average height but one is all similar and the other has giants and small children — spread tells them apart.
**Method:** range = max − min; mean deviation = mean of |x − mean|; ogive plots running totals, read median/quartiles from it.
**Misconception:** "Same mean means same data." Corrective: {5,5,5} and {0,5,10} both average 5 but have very different spreads.
**Questions:** (1) *Calculate* the range of 7, 3, 9, 2, 8. (2) *Find* the mean deviation of 2, 4, 6, 8. (3) *Compare* two data sets with equal means but different standard deviations and explain what the spread tells you.

## Common Misconceptions Master List (whole subject)

1. **Percentage reversal:** taking a percentage of the final value instead of dividing by the multiplier (a 20% discount → divide by 0.8, not ×1.2).
2. **Repeated percentages add:** treating 3 years of 10% as 30% instead of `1.1³ = 33.1%`.
3. **Dividing change by the wrong base:** using the final value rather than the original for percentage change.
4. **π = 22/7:** treating an approximation as exact; π is irrational.
5. **Ratio = fraction of total:** reading 2:3 as "2/3 of the whole" instead of 2/5.
6. **Area/volume conversions:** using the linear factor (1 m² ≠ 100 cm², it is 10000).
7. **Minutes in base 100:** adding times as decimals rather than base 60.
8. **`(a+b)² = a² + b²`:** dropping the `2ab` cross-term.
9. **Index product multiplies indices:** `a^m × a^n` gives `a^(m+n)`, not `a^(mn)`.
10. **`a^(1/2) = a ÷ 2`:** confusing a root with division.
11. **Cancelling terms not factors:** `(x+3)/3 ≠ x+1`.
12. **Adding fractions' denominators:** `x/2 + x/3 ≠ 2x/5`.
13. **Not flipping signs when transposing** across the equals sign.
14. **Quadrilateral angle sum = 180°:** it is 360°.
15. **A square is not a rectangle:** it is a special one.
16. **Exterior-angle sum varies with n:** it is always 360°.
17. **Slant side used as height** for parallelogram/triangle area.
18. **Using diameter as r** in `πr²`.
19. **Hypotenuse mis-identified:** c is always the longest side, opposite the right angle.
20. **Probability > 1** or experimental must equal theoretical.
21. **Median without sorting** the data first.
22. **Same mean ⇒ same data:** ignores spread/dispersion.

- **Physics (MYP Sciences):** Rates, unit conversions and the D=S×T triangle underpin speed, velocity and acceleration; Pythagoras resolves vector components; standard form (indices) expresses very large/small quantities; graphs and gradients link to motion graphs.
- **Chemistry (MYP Sciences):** Ratio and proportion drive mole ratios and concentration; percentage calculations give percentage yield, composition and purity; significant figures and rounding relate to measurement accuracy.
- **Geography / Individuals & Societies:** Time zones and coordinates support map-reading and global scheduling; percentage change models population growth, GDP and resource change; central tendency and dispersion summarise climate and demographic data; scale ratios convert map to real distance.
- **Digital Design / Computing:** The coordinate plane maps to screen pixels and game positions; probability underlies randomness and simulations; binary and storage sizes use powers/indices (2^10); data representation and averages feed dashboards and data-visualisation projects; algebraic logic mirrors conditional and formula-based programming.
