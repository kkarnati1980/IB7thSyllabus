## Subject Overview: Theatre in MYP Arts

Theatre belongs to the **MYP Arts** subject group. In the Arts, students act as **artists** — they investigate, develop skills, create/perform original work, and reflect on it. Theatre (drama) is a **performing art**: meaning is made live, in real time, through the body, voice, and space, in front of an audience.

### The Four MYP Arts Assessment Criteria

MYP Arts is assessed against four criteria, each out of **8 marks** (maximum level 8). Every criterion has strands described in level descriptors (1–2, 3–4, 5–6, 7–8).

| Criterion | Name | In Theatre, this means… |
|-----------|------|--------------------------|
| **A** | **Investigating** (Knowing & understanding) | Researching and understanding theatre forms, conventions, styles, practitioners and their contexts; using correct theatre vocabulary. |
| **B** | **Developing** | Developing skills and techniques; practising, exploring ideas, and planning a performance or creative intention. |
| **C** | **Creating / Performing** | Creating and/or performing theatre — realising a clear artistic intention on stage. |
| **D** | **Evaluating** | Reflecting on and evaluating one's own and others' work against the intention; appraising performances. |

> **Teacher note:** Different MYP Arts guides label Criterion A as "Investigating" (current framework) or "Knowing and understanding," and C as "Creating/Performing." Both refer to the same underlying skills. Assess with the descriptors your school has adopted for 2026–2027.

| Related Concept | Quick meaning in Theatre |

## Sub-topic (a) — Introduction to Theatre: Definition, Purpose & Vocabulary

### Concept: What Theatre *Is*
**Definition.** Theatre is a **live performing art** in which performers (actors) present a story or idea to an **audience**, usually in a shared space and time. The word comes from the Greek *theatron* — "a place for seeing." Its defining feature is **liveness**: performer and audience are present together, and the event happens now.

**Real example.** A class staging a short scene from Sophocles' *Antigone*; a touring production of the musical *The Lion King*; a street mime performing in a park.

**Analogy.** A film is like a painting you view later; theatre is like a conversation — it only fully exists while both sides are in the room together.

**Why it matters.** Understanding theatre as *live communication* explains every other idea in the unit: why the audience matters, why a "mistake" is handled differently on stage than on film, and why the same script feels different every night.

**Common misconception.** *"Theatre is just actors memorising and reciting lines."*
**Correction.** Lines are only one layer. Theatre also depends on movement, voice, timing, space, design, and — crucially — a live relationship with the audience. Acting is *reacting* and *doing*, not reciting.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* two features that make theatre "live."
- *Describe* the purpose of theatre in your own words.
- *Analyse* how being "live" changes the relationship between actor and audience.

---

### Concept: The Purpose of Theatre
**Definition.** Theatre exists to **entertain, move, teach, question, celebrate, and bring people together**. Across history it has served ritual and religious purposes (Greek festivals of Dionysus), civic and political debate, storytelling, and pure entertainment.

**Real example.** Greek tragedy explored justice and fate; Roman comedy entertained huge crowds; modern "Theatre in Education" teaches audiences about real issues.

**Why it matters.** Knowing *why* a piece was made helps us judge whether it succeeds (Criterion D — Evaluating).

**Common misconception.** *"Theatre is only for entertainment."*
**Correction.** Entertainment is one purpose among many — theatre can protest, mourn, worship, or educate.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* three different purposes theatre can serve.
- *Interpret* what a playwright might want an audience to feel or think.

### Concept: Essential Theatre Vocabulary

**Definition & importance.** A shared vocabulary lets performers, directors, and designers communicate quickly and precisely. Using correct terms is directly assessed under **Criterion A (Investigating / Knowing & understanding)**.

**Stage geography.** Stage directions are given **from the actor's point of view as they face the audience** — this is the single most common source of confusion.

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Stage** | The area where the performance happens. |
| **Upstage** | The part of the stage **furthest from the audience** (historically raked/higher). |
| **Downstage** | The part **closest to the audience**. |
| **Stage left / Stage right** | Left/right **from the actor's perspective** (so stage right is the audience's left). |
| **Centre stage** | The middle of the acting area. |
| **Wings** | The offstage areas at the sides, hidden from the audience, where actors wait to enter. |
| **Backstage** | Everything behind/around the stage the audience cannot see. |

**Key people and text terms.**

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Audience** | The people watching the performance. | The spectators in an amphitheatre. |
| **Dialogue** | Spoken exchange **between two or more characters**. | Two characters arguing in a scene. |
| **Monologue** | A **long speech by one character** to others or the audience. | A character revealing their plan aloud. |
| **Script** | The written text: dialogue plus stage directions. | The playscript of *Antigone*. |
| **Cast** | The group of actors performing the roles. | "The cast took a bow." |
| **Director** | The person who guides the artistic vision and shapes the performance. | Decides how a scene is staged. |
| **Props** | *Properties* — movable objects actors handle. | A sword, a letter, a cup. |
| **Set** | The constructed scenery showing the location. | A painted palace backdrop. |
| **Costume** | What the performers wear to show character/period. | A king's robe. |
| **Cue** | A signal (line, sound, or action) to do something next. | An actor's exit cue; a lighting cue. |
| **Blocking** | The planned movement and positioning of actors on stage. | "Cross downstage on that line." |

**Analogy for blocking.** Blocking is like choreography for a scene — a map of who moves where and when, so the stage picture stays clear.

**Analogy for a cue.** A cue is a relay-race baton: the moment you receive it, it's your turn to act.

**Common misconception.** *"Stage left is on my left as I sit in the audience."*
**Correction.** Stage left/right are always from the **actor's** viewpoint facing the audience — the opposite of the audience's left/right.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* the difference between a monologue and dialogue.
- *Describe* where the "wings" are and what they are used for.
- *Apply* correct stage directions to move an actor from upstage-right to downstage-centre.

### Concept: Kinds (Forms) of Performance
**Definition.** Theatre takes many **forms**, each with its own conventions.

| Form | What it is | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Play (spoken drama)** | A story told mainly through dialogue and action. | *Antigone*; a modern stage play. |
| **Musical** | Story told through song, dialogue, and dance. | *The Lion King*, *Matilda the Musical*. |
| **Mime** | Storytelling through movement and gesture, **no speech**. | A mime "trapped in a box." |
| **Puppetry** | Objects/puppets are animated to perform. | Shadow puppetry; *War Horse* puppets. |
| **Improvisation (improv)** | Performance created spontaneously, unscripted. | A "make-up-a-scene" game. |
| **Physical theatre** | Meaning made mainly through the body and movement. | Ensemble movement pieces. |
| **Comedy / Tragedy** | Genres by tone and outcome (see Unit 1c). | Aristophanes' comedies; Greek tragedies. |

**Why it matters.** Recognising forms lets students choose the right conventions for their own devised work (Criteria B and C).

**Common misconception.** *"Mime and improv are just messing about / warm-up games."*
**Correction.** Both are disciplined forms with real techniques — mime demands precise, controlled gesture; improv relies on skills like "accepting offers" (saying "yes, and…") and listening.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* three forms of theatre that do not rely on spoken dialogue.
- *Describe* one convention of musical theatre.
- *Evaluate* which form would best suit a story with no words.

## Sub-topic (b) — Elements of Drama

### Concept: The Elements of Drama (Overview)
**Definition.** The elements of drama are the **building blocks** every piece of theatre combines. Grade 7 focuses on the core three — **plot, character, theme** — and connects them to the classic Aristotelian set.

**Aristotle's six elements** (from *Poetics*, ~335 BCE) — age-appropriate awareness level:

| Aristotle's element | Simple meaning |
|---------------------|----------------|
| **Plot (mythos)** | The arrangement of events — the story's structure. |
| **Character (ethos)** | The people in the story and their qualities. |
| **Theme / Thought (dianoia)** | The ideas and meanings explored. |
| **Diction / Language (lexis)** | The words spoken — dialogue and how it's expressed. |
| **Music / Rhythm (melos)** | Sound, song, and the rhythm of speech and action. |
| **Spectacle (opsis)** | Everything seen — set, costume, movement, visual effect. |

**Analogy.** The elements are like ingredients in a recipe: the same ingredients in different amounts and orders make very different dishes.

**Why it matters.** Analysing a performance means noticing how these elements work **together**, not in isolation.

**Common misconception.** *"The elements are separate boxes you can study one at a time and that's enough."*
**Correction.** Their power comes from **combination** — a line of dialogue (language) delivered with a certain rhythm (music) while moving (spectacle) reveals character and advances plot all at once.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* Aristotle's six elements of drama.
- *Analyse* how two elements combine in a scene you have watched.

---

### Concept: Plot & Freytag's Pyramid
**Definition.** **Plot** is the ordered sequence of events — what happens and in what order. A classic way to map a plot's shape is **Freytag's Pyramid** (Gustav Freytag, 1863), which describes a five-part dramatic structure.

| Stage | What happens | Example (a simple story) |
|-------|--------------|--------------------------|
| **1. Exposition** | Sets up characters, setting, and situation. | We meet the hero and their ordinary world. |
| **2. Rising action** | Conflict builds; complications and tension grow. | A problem appears and gets harder. |
| **3. Climax** | The turning point of highest tension. | The hero faces the decisive moment. |
| **4. Falling action** | Events unwind as consequences play out. | The results of the climax unfold. |
| **5. Resolution (dénouement)** | Conflict is settled; the story closes. | The situation reaches its new normal. |

**Analogy.** A plot is like a roller-coaster: the slow climb (rising action) to the top (climax), then the rush down (falling action) to the station (resolution).

**Real example.** In *Antigone*, the exposition establishes Antigone's decision to bury her brother against the king's law; rising action is her defiance and arrest; the climax is Creon's fatal insistence; falling action and resolution bring the tragic consequences.

**Why it matters.** Structure controls tension and meaning — students use plot shape to build their own devised scenes (Criteria B and C).

**Common misconception.** *"Plot is the same as story / plot just means 'what it's about.'"*
**Correction.** **Story** = the raw events; **plot** = how those events are **selected and arranged** for effect. Rearranging the order changes the plot even if the story is identical.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* the five stages of Freytag's pyramid.
- *Describe* the climax of a play or film you know.
- *Analyse* how the rising action builds tension in a chosen scene.

### Concept: Character (Protagonist & Antagonist)
**Definition.** A **character** is a fictional person in the drama, with motives, objectives, and traits. Two key roles:
- **Protagonist** — the main character the story follows (not always "the good guy").
- **Antagonist** — the character or force that **opposes** the protagonist and creates conflict.

**Real example.** In *Antigone*, **Antigone** is the protagonist and **Creon** (the king) is the antagonist. In *Oedipus Rex* (Sophocles), Oedipus is the protagonist opposed by fate and his own past.

**Analogy.** Protagonist and antagonist are like two magnets pushing against each other — their opposition is the engine that drives the plot forward.

**Why it matters.** Conflict between characters is the fuel of drama; understanding objectives helps actors make strong, believable choices (Criterion C).

**Common misconception.** *"The antagonist is always evil, and the protagonist is always the hero."*
**Correction.** The antagonist is simply the **opposing force** — sometimes sympathetic, sometimes society, nature, or fate. Protagonists can be flawed or even villainous.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* the protagonist and antagonist in a chosen play.
- *Interpret* a character's main objective (what they want) in a scene.
- *Analyse* how conflict between two characters develops the plot.

---

### Concept: Theme
**Definition.** A **theme** is a central **idea, message, or question** a piece of theatre explores — e.g., justice, loyalty, power, fate, family, freedom.

**Real example.** *Antigone* explores the theme of **individual conscience vs. state law**; many comedies of Aristophanes explore the theme of politics and human folly.

**Analogy.** If plot is the "what happens," theme is the "so what?" — the idea left echoing after the story ends.

**Why it matters.** Theme gives a performance depth and helps an audience connect the story to their own lives.

**Common misconception.** *"The theme is just a one-word topic, like 'love.'"*
**Correction.** A topic is a word ("love"); a theme is a **statement or question** the play makes about it ("loyalty can demand painful sacrifice"). Theme is what the play *says* about the topic.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* one theme in a play you have studied.
- *Describe* how the plot reveals that theme.
- *Interpret* the message the playwright may be sending.

## Sub-topic (c) — Introduction to Theatre History

### Concept: Why Theatre History Matters
**Definition.** Studying theatre history means understanding **how theatre developed across time and place** — its forms, spaces, and conventions.

**Why it matters.** History gives us context: it explains where modern conventions (scripts, tragedy/comedy, the very idea of an "audience") came from, and it helps us appreciate and analyse today's theatre more deeply. This is the **Orientation in space and time** global context in action.

**Analogy.** Watching theatre without knowing its history is like hearing the end of a story with no beginning — you enjoy it, but you miss why it matters.

**Common misconception.** *"Old theatre is irrelevant to plays and shows today."*
**Correction.** Modern theatre is built on ancient foundations — Greek structure, genres, and stagecraft still shape musicals, films, and plays now.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Describe* one reason theatre history helps us appreciate modern performance.
- *Evaluate* whether knowing a play's history changes how we understand it.

### Concept: Ancient Greek Theatre (c. 6th–4th century BCE)
**Definition.** The earliest well-documented Western theatre, born in **Athens** from **religious festivals honouring Dionysus** (god of wine, fertility, and theatre), especially the **City Dionysia**. Plays were performed as **competitions**.

**Conventions and features.**
- **Performance space — the amphitheatre:** a large **open-air**, semi-circular theatre built into a hillside for excellent natural acoustics (e.g., the Theatre of Dionysus in Athens; the Theatre of Epidaurus). Key areas: the **orchestra** (round "dancing place" where the chorus performed), the **skene** (backdrop building — origin of the word "scene"), and the **theatron** (seating).
- **The chorus:** a **group of performers who sang, chanted, and moved together**, commenting on the action, representing the community, and guiding the audience's response. It was central, not decorative.
- **Masks:** actors wore large **masks** to show character type and emotion and to help project the voice; a few male actors played all roles, changing masks to switch characters.
- **Genres — tragedy and comedy:** **Tragedy** dealt with serious themes, fate, and downfall; **comedy** was humorous and often satirised politics and society.

**Major playwrights (accurate).**
| Playwright | Type | Notable works |
|------------|------|---------------|
| **Aeschylus** | Tragedy (earliest of the three great tragedians; added the **second actor**) | *The Oresteia*, *Prometheus Bound* |
| **Sophocles** | Tragedy (added the **third actor** and scene-painting) | *Antigone*, *Oedipus Rex*, *Electra* |
| **Euripides** | Tragedy (more realistic, questioning characters) | *Medea*, *The Bacchae* |
| **Aristophanes** | **Comedy** ("Old Comedy," political satire) | *Lysistrata*, *The Frogs*, *The Clouds* |

**Analogy.** The Greek chorus is like a live "commentary track" *and* the crowd's voice rolled into one — telling us how to feel and filling in the story.

**Why it matters.** Greek theatre gave us tragedy and comedy, the script, the actor, and much of dramatic structure.

**Common misconceptions.**
- *"Ancient Greek theatre had elaborate, realistic sets."* **Correction:** Staging was **minimal** — the *skene* and a few devices; meaning came from language, chorus, masks, and the audience's imagination, not detailed scenery.
- *"The chorus was just background decoration."* **Correction:** The chorus was a **central storytelling device** — it narrated, judged, questioned characters, and voiced the community.
- *"Women performed on the Greek stage."* **Correction:** In Athens, performers were **male**; men played female roles using masks.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* three features of an ancient Greek amphitheatre.
- *Describe* the role of the chorus in Greek theatre.
- *Analyse* why masks were useful in a large open-air theatre.
- *Interpret* why theatre was linked to the festivals of Dionysus.

### Concept: Ancient Roman Theatre (c. 3rd century BCE onward)
**Definition.** Roman theatre grew largely by **adapting Greek models** but shifted toward **broad entertainment and spectacle** for mass audiences.

**Conventions and features.**
- **Adaptation:** Romans borrowed Greek plots, characters, and forms, reworking them for Roman tastes and language (Latin).
- **Spectacle:** Emphasis on **entertainment** — comedy, farce, music, and, in the wider Roman world, large-scale spectacles. Roman theatres were often **free-standing stone buildings** (not dug into hillsides like Greek ones).
- **Playwrights:** **Plautus** (comedies full of stock characters, mistaken identity, and wordplay, e.g., *Miles Gloriosus*) and **Terence** (more refined comedies). **Seneca** wrote influential tragedies later admired in the Renaissance.

**Analogy.** If Greek theatre was a solemn civic-religious ceremony, Roman theatre leaned more toward a crowd-pleasing show — closer to popular entertainment.

**Why it matters.** Roman comedy's **stock characters** and comic devices influenced later European comedy (including *commedia dell'arte* and Shakespeare).

**Common misconception.** *"Roman theatre was completely original and unrelated to Greek theatre."*
**Correction.** Roman theatre was heavily **based on Greek theatre**, then adapted for spectacle and Roman audiences.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* one way Roman theatre differed from Greek theatre.
- *Describe* the kind of plays Plautus wrote.
- *Analyse* why "spectacle" became more important in Roman performance.

### Concept: The Theatre History Timeline (Overview)
**Definition.** A big-picture map of how Western theatre evolved. Grade 7 works at **overview level** — knowing the order and one or two features of each era.

| Era | Approx. period | Key features | A name to know |
|-----|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| **Ancient Greek** | c. 550–350 BCE | Festivals of Dionysus; amphitheatres; chorus; masks; tragedy & comedy | Sophocles |
| **Ancient Roman** | c. 240 BCE–500 CE | Adapted Greek forms; spectacle; stone theatres; stock-character comedy | Plautus |
| **Medieval** | c. 500–1500 CE | Religious drama — **mystery, miracle, and morality plays**; travelling players | Guild "cycle" plays |
| **Renaissance / Shakespeare** | c. 1500–1650 | Rebirth of classical ideas; professional playhouses (e.g., the **Globe**); rich language | **William Shakespeare** |
| **Modern** | c. 1850–present | Realism, musicals, experimental & physical theatre, world theatre traditions | Stanislavski (acting), modern musicals |

**Analogy.** The timeline is like a family tree — each era inherits traits from its ancestors and passes new ones on.

**Why it matters.** Placing a play in time helps explain its style, staging, and purpose.

**Common misconception.** *"Shakespeare invented theatre."*
**Correction.** Shakespeare (Renaissance, ~1564–1616) came **almost 2,000 years after** Greek theatre began. He built on a very long tradition.

**Command-term questions.**
- *Identify* the five eras of theatre in order.
- *Describe* one feature of medieval theatre.
- *Analyse* how Greek conventions can still be seen in later theatre.

## Common Misconceptions — Master List

1. **"Theatre is just memorising and reciting lines."** → Theatre is live communication through voice, body, space, design, and audience relationship — not recitation.
2. **"Stage left is on my left as an audience member."** → Stage directions are from the **actor's** viewpoint, opposite to the audience's.
3. **"Mime and improv are just games."** → Both are disciplined forms with real techniques.
4. **"Plot = story."** → Story is the events; plot is how they are **selected and arranged**.
5. **"The theme is a single topic word."** → A theme is a **statement or question** about a topic, not just the topic.
6. **"The antagonist is always evil / the protagonist is always heroic."** → The antagonist is the **opposing force**; both roles can be complex.
7. **"Ancient Greek theatre had elaborate realistic sets."** → Staging was **minimal**; imagination, language, chorus, and masks did the work.
8. **"The chorus was just background."** → The chorus was a **central storytelling and commentary device**.
9. **"Women acted on the ancient Greek stage."** → Performers were **male**; men played female roles.
10. **"Roman theatre was entirely original."** → It was largely **adapted from Greek theatre**, then made more spectacular.
11. **"Shakespeare invented theatre."** → He came ~2,000 years after Greek theatre and built on a long tradition.
12. **"The elements of drama work separately."** → Their power is in **combination**.

## Criteria / Command-Term Question Bank

Useful for lessons, formative checks, and modelling assessment language. Command terms: **identify, describe, analyse, interpret, perform, evaluate**.

1. **(A — Investigating) Identify** and label the main areas of a stage (upstage, downstage, stage left/right, wings, centre).
2. **(A — Investigating) Describe** the role of the chorus and masks in ancient Greek theatre.
3. **(A/D — Analyse)** the ways plot, character, and theme combine to communicate meaning in a scene you have watched.
4. **(A — Investigating) Interpret** why theatre began as part of the festivals of Dionysus, and what that tells us about its early purpose.
5. **(B/C — Perform)** a short improvised or scripted scene that clearly moves through the five stages of Freytag's pyramid.
6. **(C — Creating/Performing) Perform** a wordless mime moment that communicates one clear emotion to an audience.
7. **(D — Evaluating) Evaluate** whether a performance you watched successfully communicated its theme to the audience, using evidence.
8. **(D — Evaluating) Evaluate** the claim: *"You cannot fully appreciate modern theatre without knowing its history."* Argue with examples.

## Cross-Subject Connections

- **Individuals & Societies (History):** Ancient Greek and Roman theatre link directly to the study of **ancient civilisations** — democracy in Athens, religion (Dionysia), Roman society, architecture (amphitheatres), and daily life.
- **Language & Literature:** **Plot, character, and theme** are shared analytical tools; scripts are a literary form; Freytag's pyramid and protagonist/antagonist apply to novels and short stories too.
- **Music:** Aristotle's element of **music/rhythm**; the sung, chanted Greek chorus; the whole form of **musical theatre**; sound and rhythm in delivery.
- **Visual Art & Design:** **Spectacle** — set design, costume, masks, staging, and the layout/architecture of performance spaces; visual storytelling and stage pictures (blocking).
- **Physical Education / Dance:** Movement, physical theatre, mime, and the ensemble movement of the chorus.
