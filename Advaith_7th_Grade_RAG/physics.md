# Grade 7 Physics — IISH MYP Knowledge Base (2026-2027)

## Subject Overview

Physics at IISH is taught within the **MYP Sciences** subject group. Sciences in the MYP encourages students to investigate the physical world, develop scientific inquiry skills, and reflect on how science shapes and is shaped by society. Grade 7 sits in **MYP Year 2**, building the conceptual and practical foundations for later diploma-level study.

### The Four Sciences Assessment Criteria

Every unit is assessed against four equally weighted criteria, each scored out of 8:

| Criterion | Title | What it assesses |
|-----------|-------|------------------|
| **A** | Knowing and understanding | Recalling scientific knowledge; applying it to solve problems in familiar and unfamiliar situations; analysing and evaluating information to make judgements. |
| **B** | Inquiring and designing | Stating problems/questions; formulating testable hypotheses; identifying variables; designing a fair, logical, safe method. |
| **C** | Processing and evaluating | Presenting and organising data; interpreting data and drawing conclusions; evaluating validity of the method and improving the investigation. |
| **D** | Reflecting on the impacts of science | Explaining how science is applied to a problem/issue; discussing implications on society and the environment; evaluating scientific communication. |

### Sciences Related Concepts (used across the year)

Balance, Consequences, Development, Energy, Environment, Evidence, Form, Function, Interaction, Models, Movement, Patterns, Transfer, Transformation. Each unit below selects the related concepts most relevant to its content.

### How to read this file

The structure is **Unit → Sub-topic (chapter) → concept entries**. Each unit opens with its Key Concept, Related Concepts, Global Context, Statement of Inquiry, and three inquiry questions. Each concept entry gives a definition, a real-world example, an analogy, why it matters, a described diagram or worked example where useful, at least one misconception, and three command-term questions graded from recall to analyse/evaluate.

# Unit 1: Measurements and Motion

- **Key Concept:** Relationships
- **Related Concepts:** Movement, Patterns, Evidence
- **Global Context:** Scientific and technical innovation — how measurement and models let us describe and predict motion.
- **Statement of Inquiry:** Accurate measurement reveals patterns in movement that allow us to describe and predict how objects change position over time.
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What is the difference between distance and displacement, and between speed and velocity?
  - **Conceptual:** How does the slope of a graph reveal the relationship between two measured quantities?
  - **Debatable:** Is any measurement ever truly "accurate," or only accurate enough for its purpose?

## 1.1 Measurements

### Fundamental vs Derived Quantities

**Definition.** A *fundamental (base) quantity* cannot be defined in terms of other quantities; it is measured directly and forms the foundation of the SI system. A *derived quantity* is built by combining fundamental quantities through multiplication or division.

**Real-world example.** Length (fundamental) is measured directly with a ruler; speed (derived) is length divided by time.

**Analogy.** Fundamental quantities are like the primary colours; derived quantities are the shades you mix from them.

**Why it matters.** Every equation in physics is dimensionally consistent because derived units trace back to base units — this lets you check whether a formula is even possible.

| Type | Quantity | SI Unit | Symbol |
|------|----------|---------|--------|
| Fundamental | Length | metre | m |
| Fundamental | Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Fundamental | Time | second | s |
| Fundamental | Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Fundamental | Electric current | ampere | A |
| Derived | Area | square metre | m² |
| Derived | Volume | cubic metre | m³ |
| Derived | Speed / velocity | metre per second | m/s |
| Derived | Density | kilogram per cubic metre | kg/m³ |
| Derived | Acceleration | metre per second squared | m/s² |

**Misconception.** *"Weight is a fundamental quantity like mass."* Believed because both are measured in daily life. Wrong because weight is a force (derived: mass × acceleration, newtons); mass is the fundamental quantity in kilograms. Corrective: mass is base, weight is derived from mass and gravity.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* two fundamental quantities and their SI units. (recall)
2. *Show* that the unit of density can be written in terms of fundamental units only. (apply)
3. *Explain* why speed is classified as a derived quantity rather than a fundamental one. (analyse)

### Unit Conversions

**Definition.** Converting a measurement from one unit to another of the same quantity by multiplying by a conversion factor equal to 1.

**Real-world example.** A road sign gives 90 km/h; to use it in a physics formula you convert to 25 m/s.

**Analogy.** Like exchanging currency — the amount of "value" stays the same, only the label changes.

**Why it matters.** Mixing units (cm with m, minutes with seconds) is the most common source of wrong answers in numerical work.

**Worked example.** Convert 72 km/h to m/s.
72 km/h × (1000 m / 1 km) × (1 h / 3600 s) = 72 × 1000 / 3600 = **20 m/s**.
(Shortcut: divide km/h by 3.6.)

**Misconception.** *"To convert km/h to m/s you multiply by 1000."* Wrong because you must also convert hours to seconds. Corrective: divide by 3.6 (i.e. ×1000 ÷ 3600).

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Convert* 5 km into metres. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the speed 36 km/h in m/s. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a conversion factor must equal one for the physical quantity to stay unchanged. (analyse)

### Precision and Accuracy

**Definition.** *Accuracy* is how close a measurement is to the true value. *Precision* is how close repeated measurements are to each other (their spread), independent of the true value.

**Real-world example.** A bathroom scale that always reads 2 kg high is precise (consistent) but not accurate.

**Analogy.** A dartboard: accurate = darts near the bullseye; precise = darts clustered tightly, even if off-centre.

**Why it matters.** Good science needs both — data that are precise but inaccurate hide a systematic fault.

**Described diagram.** Picture four dartboards: (1) scattered and off-centre = neither; (2) tight cluster off-centre = precise not accurate; (3) spread around centre = accurate not precise; (4) tight cluster on centre = both.

**Misconception.** *"Precise and accurate mean the same thing."* Believed because both sound like "good measurement." Wrong because a stuck instrument can be perfectly repeatable yet always wrong. Corrective: precision = agreement of repeats; accuracy = closeness to truth.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* accuracy and precision. (recall)
2. *Identify* whether a scale reading consistently 0.5 kg too high is a precision or accuracy problem. (apply)
3. *Evaluate* a data set with a small spread but a large offset from the accepted value. (evaluate)

### The Scientific Method

**Definition.** A systematic process for investigating questions: observe → ask a question → form a hypothesis → design and perform an experiment → collect and analyse data → draw a conclusion → communicate and repeat.

**Real-world example.** Testing whether a heavier ball rolls down a ramp faster: predict, time several trials, average, conclude.

**Analogy.** A recipe you refine each time you cook — each attempt improves the next.

**Why it matters.** It makes findings testable, repeatable, and open to challenge — the heart of MYP Criteria B and C.

**Misconception.** *"A hypothesis is just a guess."* Wrong because a hypothesis is a *testable, reasoned* prediction, usually in an "if… then…" form. Corrective: it must be falsifiable by an experiment.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *List* the main steps of the scientific method in order. (recall)
2. *Formulate* a testable hypothesis for the ramp experiment. (apply)
3. *Explain* why repeating trials strengthens a conclusion. (analyse)

### Area and Volume (Regular and Irregular Objects)

**Definition.** *Area* is the amount of surface a shape covers (m²). *Volume* is the amount of space a 3-D object occupies (m³).

**Real-world example.** A rectangular tank's volume tells you how much water it holds; an oddly shaped stone's volume is found by displacement.

**Analogy.** Volume by displacement is like measuring your body by how much bathwater overflows when you get in.

**Why it matters.** Volume is needed for density, and density identifies materials and predicts floating/sinking.

**Method for regular objects.** Measure dimensions and apply formulas — rectangular block V = length × width × height; cylinder V = πr²h.

**Method for irregular objects (displacement).** Fill a measuring cylinder to a known volume, lower the object in, read the new volume; the rise equals the object's volume. For a stone: 50 mL → 68 mL means volume = 18 mL = 18 cm³.

**Misconception.** *"Bigger objects always have more volume than smaller ones."* Wrong because shape matters — a flat sheet can spread wide yet hold little volume. Corrective: volume depends on all three dimensions, not overall appearance.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the SI unit of volume. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the volume of a block 4 cm × 3 cm × 2 cm. (apply)
3. *Describe and justify* how you would find the volume of an irregular key using displacement, and why the method works. (analyse)

### Density (Solids)

**Definition.** Density is mass per unit volume: how much matter is packed into a given space. Formula: **density = mass ÷ volume** (ρ = m / V), unit kg/m³ or g/cm³.

**Real-world example.** A small lead fishing weight feels heavy for its size because lead is very dense; a large foam block feels light.

**Analogy.** A crowded lift (high density) versus a nearly empty one (low density) of the same size.

**Why it matters.** Density predicts whether something floats, and helps identify unknown materials.

**Described diagram.** A bar chart of densities: cork (~0.24), water (1.0), aluminium (2.7), iron (7.9), gold (19.3) g/cm³ — the taller the bar, the more mass squeezed into each cm³.

**Worked example.** A metal cube has mass 240 g and volume 30 cm³.
Density = mass ÷ volume = 240 ÷ 30 = **8 g/cm³** (likely iron/steel).

**Misconception.** *"Heavier objects are always denser."* Wrong because a huge low-density object can outweigh a tiny dense one. Corrective: density compares mass *for the same volume*, not total mass.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the formula for density and its unit. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the density of a 50 g object with volume 25 cm³. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a steel ship floats even though steel is denser than water. (analyse)

### Least Count

**Definition.** The least count of an instrument is the smallest value it can measure — the size of one smallest division on its scale.

**Real-world example.** A metre ruler marked in mm has a least count of 1 mm (0.1 cm); a stopwatch reading to 0.01 s has a least count of 0.01 s.

**Analogy.** Like the smallest coin in a currency — you cannot measure change finer than it.

**Why it matters.** The least count sets the limit of a measurement's precision and its uncertainty.

**Misconception.** *"You can read a ruler to any number of decimal places."* Wrong because you cannot report finer than half the least count. Corrective: precision is capped by the instrument's least count.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the least count of a millimetre ruler. (recall)
2. *Determine* the least count of a stopwatch that displays 00:00.00. (apply)
3. *Explain* how a smaller least count improves a measurement. (analyse)

### Common Errors and Parallax Error

**Definition.** *Errors* are differences between a measured and true value. Common length, mass and time errors include zero errors (instrument not reading zero at start), reaction-time delays with stopwatches, and *parallax error* — a reading error caused by viewing a scale from an angle rather than straight on.

**Real-world example.** Reading a thermometer or measuring cylinder from above or below the meniscus gives a wrong value; only eye-level reading is correct.

**Analogy.** A car's fuel gauge appears to point differently to the driver and the passenger — same needle, different angle, different reading.

**Why it matters.** Recognising and removing errors is central to MYP Criterion C (evaluating and improving a method).

**Described diagram.** A measuring cylinder with three eye positions: eye above reads too high, eye below reads too low, eye level with the meniscus reads correctly.

**Misconception.** *"A stopwatch gives the exact time."* Wrong because human reaction time (~0.2 s) adds error at start and stop. Corrective: repeat and average, or use light gates to reduce reaction error.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* parallax error. (recall)
2. *Identify* one source of error when timing a runner with a handheld stopwatch. (apply)
3. *Suggest and justify* two improvements to reduce errors when measuring the length of a table. (evaluate)

## 1.2 Motion

### Distance vs Displacement

**Definition.** *Distance* is the total path length travelled (a scalar — size only). *Displacement* is the straight-line distance from start to finish in a given direction (a vector — size and direction).

**Real-world example.** Walk 3 m east then 3 m west: distance = 6 m, displacement = 0 m (back at start).

**Analogy.** Distance is how far your car's odometer clocked up; displacement is how far you actually ended up from home "as the crow flies."

**Why it matters.** Confusing the two gives wrong velocities and misreads position-time graphs.

**Misconception.** *"Distance and displacement are always equal."* True only for straight-line, one-direction motion. Corrective: they differ whenever the path bends or reverses.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* displacement. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the distance and displacement of a person who walks 4 m north then 3 m north. (apply)
3. *Explain* how a runner completing one lap of a track can have zero displacement but large distance. (analyse)

### Speed vs Velocity (Velocity Definition)

**Definition.** *Speed* is the rate of change of distance (scalar). *Velocity* is the rate of change of displacement — speed in a stated direction (vector). Formula: velocity = displacement ÷ time.

> **Course note:** In Grade 7 the *direction* of velocity is used to distinguish it from speed conceptually, but numerical answers here do **not** require stating direction — treat magnitudes only.

**Real-world example.** A car's speedometer shows speed; a GPS that says "moving north at 20 m/s" gives velocity.

**Analogy.** Speed is "how fast"; velocity is "how fast *and which way*."

**Why it matters.** Velocity, not speed, connects to displacement-time graphs and to acceleration.

**Worked example.** A cyclist covers 100 m in 20 s. Average speed = distance ÷ time = 100 ÷ 20 = **5 m/s**.

**Misconception.** *"Speed and velocity are the same word for the same thing."* Wrong because velocity carries direction and can be zero even at high speed (round trip). Corrective: speed is magnitude only; velocity includes direction.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* velocity. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the average speed of a train travelling 240 m in 30 s. (apply)
3. *Distinguish* between speed and velocity using an example of circular motion. (analyse)

### Acceleration

**Definition.** Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity with time — how quickly speed increases or decreases. Formula: **a = (v − u) ÷ t**, unit m/s². (u = initial velocity, v = final velocity.)

**Real-world example.** A car speeding up from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 s accelerates at 4 m/s²; braking is negative acceleration (deceleration).

**Analogy.** Acceleration is how hard the "push in the back" feels when a car pulls away — the faster the velocity changes, the stronger the push.

**Why it matters.** Acceleration links force to motion (later: F = ma) and shapes velocity-time graphs.

**Worked example.** A car goes from 10 m/s to 30 m/s in 4 s.
a = (v − u) ÷ t = (30 − 10) ÷ 4 = 20 ÷ 4 = **5 m/s²**.

**Misconception.** *"An object moving fast must be accelerating."* Wrong because constant high speed means zero acceleration; acceleration is about *changing* velocity. Corrective: no change in velocity → no acceleration, however fast.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the unit of acceleration. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the acceleration of a bike that slows from 12 m/s to 0 in 3 s. (apply)
3. *Explain* how an object can have a high speed but zero acceleration. (analyse)

### Distance–Time / Position–Time / Displacement–Time Graphs

**Definition.** A distance-time (or position/displacement-time) graph plots how far an object is from a start point against time. The **slope (gradient) = speed/velocity**.

**Real-world example.** A GPS trace of a school bus stopping and starting — flat sections are stops, steep sections are fast stretches.

**Analogy.** Reading the graph is like reading a story: rising line = moving away, flat line = resting, steeper = faster.

**Why it matters.** Graphs turn a table of numbers into an instantly readable picture of motion and give speed from the slope.

**Described diagram.** Time on the x-axis, distance on the y-axis. A straight sloped line = constant speed; a horizontal line = at rest; a steeper line = higher speed; a curve that gets steeper = speeding up.

**Worked example (slope).** A line rises from 0 m to 40 m between t = 0 s and t = 8 s.
Slope = rise ÷ run = 40 ÷ 8 = **5 m/s**.

**Misconception.** *"A horizontal line on a distance-time graph means the object moves at constant speed."* Wrong — a flat line means distance isn't changing, so the object is *stationary*. Corrective: constant speed is a *straight sloped* line; flat = at rest.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* what the slope of a distance-time graph represents. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the speed from a line rising 60 m over 12 s. (apply)
3. *Interpret* a distance-time graph that is steep, then flat, then steep again. (analyse)

### Instantaneous vs Average Velocity

**Definition.** *Average velocity* = total displacement ÷ total time (over a whole journey). *Instantaneous velocity* is the velocity at a single instant — the reading a speedometer would show at that moment. (Definition only at this level.)

**Real-world example.** A 60 km trip in 1 hour gives an average of 60 km/h even though the speedometer showed 0, 80, and 40 km/h at different instants.

**Analogy.** Average velocity is your overall school-report grade; instantaneous velocity is your score on one particular question.

**Why it matters.** Explains why "average" hides the ups and downs of a real journey.

**Misconception.** *"Average velocity is the average of the highest and lowest speeds."* Wrong because it is total displacement over total time, which weights how long you spend at each speed. Corrective: use displacement ÷ time, not a simple mean of speeds.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* instantaneous velocity. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the average velocity of a car covering 150 m in 30 s. (apply)
3. *Distinguish* between average and instantaneous velocity using a journey with traffic. (analyse)

### Velocity–Time Graphs

**Definition.** A velocity-time graph plots velocity against time. The **slope = acceleration**, and the **area under the line = distance travelled**.

**Real-world example.** A train pulling out: velocity rises (accelerating), holds steady (cruising), then falls (braking).

**Analogy.** The area under the graph is like counting the tiles beneath the line — each tile is a small chunk of distance, and they add up to the total.

**Why it matters.** One graph gives both acceleration (slope) and distance (area) — two answers from one picture.

**Described diagram.** Time on x-axis, velocity on y-axis. A rising straight line = constant acceleration; a horizontal line = constant velocity; a falling line = deceleration. To find distance while cruising at 10 m/s for 6 s, the area is a rectangle: 10 × 6 = 60 m. For a triangle (acceleration from rest), area = ½ × base × height.

**Worked example (area = distance).** An object accelerates from 0 to 12 m/s over 4 s (triangle), then holds 12 m/s for 3 s (rectangle).
Triangle area = ½ × 4 × 12 = 24 m. Rectangle area = 12 × 3 = 36 m. **Total distance = 60 m.**

**Misconception.** *"A horizontal line on a velocity-time graph means the object is stationary."* Wrong — a flat non-zero line means *constant velocity*; the object keeps moving. Only a line on the time axis (v = 0) means at rest. Corrective: on a v-t graph, flat = steady speed, not stopped.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* what the area under a velocity-time graph represents. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the distance travelled by an object moving at a constant 8 m/s for 5 s from its v-t graph. (apply)
3. *Analyse* a v-t graph with a rising line, a flat line, and a falling line, describing the motion in each stage. (analyse)

### Formulae and Numerical Problems

**Key relationships used in this unit:**
- Average speed = total distance ÷ total time
- Average velocity = total displacement ÷ total time
- Acceleration: a = (v − u) ÷ t
- Slope of distance-time graph = speed
- Slope of velocity-time graph = acceleration; area under v-t graph = distance
- **v = u + at**
- **s = ut + ½at²**

**Worked example (v = u + at).** A car starts at u = 5 m/s and accelerates at a = 2 m/s² for t = 4 s.
v = u + at = 5 + (2 × 4) = 5 + 8 = **13 m/s**.

**Worked example (s = ut + ½at²).** Same car: distance in those 4 s.
s = ut + ½at² = (5 × 4) + ½ × 2 × 4² = 20 + ½ × 2 × 16 = 20 + 16 = **36 m**.

**Misconception.** *"You can use v = u + at even when the acceleration keeps changing."* Wrong because these equations assume *constant* acceleration. Corrective: only apply them to uniform acceleration; otherwise use graphs.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the equation linking final velocity, initial velocity, acceleration and time. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the final velocity of an object starting from rest and accelerating at 3 m/s² for 6 s. (apply)
3. *Solve* for the distance travelled and *comment* on whether the constant-acceleration assumption is reasonable for a real car. (evaluate)

# Unit 2: Forces and Energy

- **Key Concept:** Systems
- **Related Concepts:** Energy, Transformation, Interaction, Balance
- **Global Context:** Globalization and sustainability — how we harness, transform and conserve energy responsibly.
- **Statement of Inquiry:** Within any system, forces interact and energy transforms while the total is conserved, shaping how we design machines and use resources sustainably.
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What are the main types of force and forms of energy?
  - **Conceptual:** How can energy change form yet the total stay the same?
  - **Debatable:** Should society prioritise renewable energy even when it costs more today?

## 2.1 Forces

### Definition of Force

**Definition.** A force is a push or a pull that can change the shape, size, speed, or direction of an object. Measured in newtons (N).

**Real-world example.** Kicking a ball (push) changes its motion; stretching a spring (pull) changes its shape.

**Analogy.** A force is an "invisible hand" nudging or tugging objects.

**Why it matters.** Forces explain every change in motion and shape around us — from walking to bridges standing.

**Misconception.** *"A moving object always has a force pushing it forward."* Wrong — a moving object continues without a forward force; force is needed to *change* motion, not maintain it (in the absence of friction). Corrective: forces cause changes in motion, not motion itself.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* force and state its unit. (recall)
2. *Identify* the force changing the shape of a squeezed sponge. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a force is needed to stop a rolling ball. (analyse)

### Types of Force and Contact vs Non-Contact

**Definition.** A *contact force* acts only when objects touch; a *non-contact force* acts across a distance without touching.

| Force | Type | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Gravity | Non-contact | Attraction between masses; pulls objects toward Earth |
| Magnetic | Non-contact | Attraction/repulsion between magnetic materials |
| Electrostatic | Non-contact | Attraction/repulsion between electric charges |
| Nuclear | Non-contact | Very short-range force holding nuclei together |
| Friction | Contact | Opposes motion between touching surfaces |
| Push | Contact | Applied force away from the source |
| Pull | Contact | Applied force toward the source |
| Tension | Contact | Pulling force along a rope, string or cable |
| Drag / air resistance | Contact | Friction from a fluid (air/water) opposing motion |
| Upthrust (buoyancy) | Contact | Upward push from a fluid on an immersed object |

**Real-world example.** A dropped apple falls by gravity (non-contact); a book slides to a stop by friction (contact).

**Analogy.** Contact forces are like a handshake (you must touch); non-contact forces are like feeling the warmth of a fire from across the room.

**Why it matters.** Classifying forces helps predict how objects interact and which forces to include in a diagram.

**Misconception.** *"Magnets must touch to affect each other."* Wrong — magnetism is non-contact and acts through a field across empty space. Corrective: magnetic, gravitational and electrostatic forces act at a distance.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *List* two contact and two non-contact forces. (recall)
2. *Classify* upthrust and gravity as contact or non-contact. (apply)
3. *Explain* why air resistance is a contact force even though air seems empty. (analyse)

### Effects of Forces

**Definition.** Forces can change an object's shape, size, speed, or direction of motion.

**Real-world example.** Squeezing clay changes its shape; a tennis racket changes a ball's direction.

**Analogy.** A force is like an editor's pen — it can reshape, resize, or redirect.

**Why it matters.** Every deformation and change of motion in engineering traces back to a force's effect.

**Misconception.** *"Forces only make things move."* Wrong — forces also stretch, compress and bend objects that stay still (a stretched rubber band). Corrective: forces can change shape/size without any movement of the whole object.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* three effects a force can have. (recall)
2. *Identify* the effect of a force on a stretched spring. (apply)
3. *Explain* how a single kick can change both the speed and direction of a football. (analyse)

### Balanced vs Unbalanced Forces & Free Body Diagrams

**Definition.** Forces are *balanced* when they cancel out (net force zero) — the object stays at rest or moves at constant velocity. Forces are *unbalanced* when one direction wins — the object accelerates. A *free body diagram* shows a single object with labelled arrows for each force; arrow length shows size, arrow direction shows the force's direction. (Linear/one-line-of-action only at this level.)

**Real-world example.** A book resting on a table: weight down is balanced by the normal (reaction) force up. A floating boat: weight down balanced by upthrust up. A lamp hanging from a thread: weight down balanced by tension up.

**Analogy.** Balanced forces are a tug-of-war where both teams pull equally — the rope doesn't move.

**Why it matters.** Recognising balance explains why resting, floating and hanging objects don't accelerate, and free body diagrams are the standard tool for analysing forces.

**Described diagram.** For a resting book, draw a box with one arrow pointing down labelled "weight" and an equal-length arrow pointing up labelled "normal force." Equal arrows = balanced = no acceleration.

**Which force balances weight:**
- Resting on a surface → **normal (reaction) force** balances weight.
- Floating in a fluid → **upthrust** balances weight.
- Suspended by a thread → **tension** balances weight.

**Misconception.** *"If forces are balanced, the object must be still."* Wrong — balanced forces also allow *constant velocity* motion. Corrective: balanced = zero net force = no *change* in motion (rest OR steady speed).

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* what happens to an object when forces are balanced. (recall)
2. *Draw* a free body diagram for a box floating on water. (apply)
3. *Explain* which force balances the weight of a hanging lamp and why the lamp does not accelerate. (analyse)

### Mass vs Weight

**Definition.** *Mass* is the amount of matter in an object (kilograms), the same everywhere. *Weight* is the gravitational force on that mass (newtons): **weight = mass × gravitational field strength (W = mg)**, where g ≈ 10 N/kg on Earth.

**Real-world example.** An astronaut with mass 60 kg weighs about 600 N on Earth but only about 100 N on the Moon — same mass, less weight.

**Analogy.** Mass is how much "stuff" you're made of; weight is how hard that stuff is being pulled down.

**Why it matters.** Confusing mass and weight leads to errors in force calculations and misunderstanding space travel.

**Worked example.** A 5 kg bag on Earth: W = mg = 5 × 10 = **50 N**.

**Misconception.** *"Mass and weight are the same thing."* Believed because scales in shops show "kg." Wrong because mass (kg) is constant while weight (N) changes with gravity. Corrective: mass is matter in kg; weight is a force in N that depends on g.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the units of mass and weight. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the weight of a 12 kg object on Earth (g = 10 N/kg). (apply)
3. *Explain* why an object's weight is less on the Moon but its mass is unchanged. (analyse)

### Pressure in Fluids

**Definition.** Pressure is force spread over an area: **pressure = force ÷ area** (P = F / A), unit pascal (Pa = N/m²). In fluids, pressure increases with depth and acts in all directions.

**Real-world example.** A dam is built thicker at the bottom because water pressure is greater at depth; sharp knives cut easily because a small area gives high pressure.

**Analogy.** Standing on snow in boots (small area, sink in) versus snowshoes (large area, stay on top) — same weight, different pressure.

**Why it matters.** Pressure explains diving, hydraulics, and why area matters for the same force.

**Worked example.** A force of 200 N acts on an area of 4 m².
P = F ÷ A = 200 ÷ 4 = **50 Pa**.

**Misconception.** *"A heavier object always exerts more pressure."* Wrong because pressure also depends on area — spreading the same force over more area lowers pressure. Corrective: pressure = force ÷ area, so area matters as much as force.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the formula and unit for pressure. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the pressure when 500 N acts on 2 m². (apply)
3. *Explain* why water pressure on a dam increases with depth. (analyse)

### Hydraulic Press & Pascal's Law

**Definition.** *Pascal's Law:* pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions. A *hydraulic press* uses this: a small force on a small piston creates pressure that acts on a large piston, producing a large force.

**Real-world example.** Car brakes and hydraulic car jacks lift heavy loads with a light push.

**Analogy.** Squeezing one end of a water-filled balloon pushes out everywhere — the pressure travels through the whole fluid.

**Why it matters.** Hydraulics multiply force, letting small inputs move heavy machinery.

**Described diagram.** Two connected cylinders share a fluid. A small piston (small area) is pushed down; the same pressure acts on the large piston (large area), so its force is much bigger (F = P × A). The larger the area, the larger the output force.

**Misconception.** *"A hydraulic press creates extra energy from nothing."* Wrong — you gain force but the large piston moves a smaller distance, so work in ≈ work out (energy conserved). Corrective: force is multiplied, not energy.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* Pascal's Law. (recall)
2. *Describe* how a hydraulic press multiplies force. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a hydraulic press does not break the law of conservation of energy. (evaluate)

### Law of Conservation of Energy (in machines)

**Definition.** Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another; the total energy in a closed system stays constant.

**Real-world example.** In a simple machine, the energy you put in equals the useful energy out plus energy "lost" to friction as heat.

**Analogy.** Like money moved between bank accounts — the total never changes even as it shifts around.

**Why it matters.** It sets the limit on what any machine can do — you never get more energy out than you put in.

**Misconception.** *"A machine can produce more energy than it uses."* Wrong — a perpetual motion machine is impossible; some energy always becomes waste heat. Corrective: output energy ≤ input energy, always.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the law of conservation of energy. (recall)
2. *Apply* the law to explain why a machine warms up during use. (apply)
3. *Evaluate* the claim that a machine is "100% efficient." (evaluate)

### Mechanical Advantage & Efficiency of Simple Machines

**Definition.** *Mechanical advantage (MA)* is how many times a machine multiplies your input force: MA = load ÷ effort. *Efficiency* is the percentage of input energy that becomes useful output: efficiency = (useful output ÷ total input) × 100%.

**Real-world example.** A crowbar lets a small effort lift a heavy rock (high MA); some effort is still lost to friction, so efficiency is below 100%.

**Analogy.** Mechanical advantage is like a lever letting a child lift an adult on a see-saw by sitting further out.

**Why it matters.** These measures let engineers compare and improve tools and machines.

**Worked example.** A machine lifts a 300 N load with an effort of 100 N.
MA = load ÷ effort = 300 ÷ 100 = **3** (the machine triples the force).

**Misconception.** *"A machine with a high mechanical advantage is always efficient."* Wrong — MA is about force multiplication, efficiency about energy loss; a rusty pulley can have high MA but low efficiency. Corrective: MA and efficiency are separate ideas.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* mechanical advantage. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the MA of a lever lifting a 400 N load with 80 N effort. (apply)
3. *Explain* why the efficiency of a real machine is always less than 100%. (analyse)

## 2.2 Work, Power and Energy

### Types of Energy & Energy Transformation

**Definition.** Energy is the capacity to do work; it exists in many forms and can transform between them.

| Energy type | Description | Example transformation |
|-------------|-------------|------------------------|
| Kinetic | Energy of a moving object | Chemical → kinetic (a running person) |
| Potential (gravitational) | Stored energy due to height | Gravitational PE → kinetic (falling ball) |
| Elastic potential | Stored in stretched/compressed objects | Elastic PE → kinetic (released catapult) |
| Chemical | Stored in bonds of fuel/food/batteries | Chemical → heat + light (burning fuel) |
| Atomic (nuclear) | Stored in atomic nuclei | Nuclear → heat (reactor) |
| Heat (thermal) | Energy from particle motion/temperature | Electrical → heat (heater) |
| Mechanical | Sum of kinetic + potential energy | Mechanical → electrical (dynamo) |

**Real-world example.** A torch: chemical energy in the battery → electrical → light + heat.

**Analogy.** Energy forms are like different currencies; transformation is exchanging one for another without changing the total value.

**Why it matters.** Nearly every device works by transforming energy from one useful form to another.

**Misconception.** *"Energy gets used up and disappears."* Wrong — it transforms, often into less useful heat, but the total is conserved. Corrective: energy is transferred/transformed, never destroyed.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *List* four types of energy. (recall)
2. *Describe* the energy transformations in a swinging pendulum. (apply)
3. *Explain* why the light energy from a bulb is less than the electrical energy supplied. (analyse)

### Law of Conservation of Energy (conceptual)

**Definition.** The total energy in a closed system is constant; energy only changes form. (Conceptual here — no calculations.)

**Real-world example.** A roller coaster: at the top, mostly gravitational PE; at the bottom, mostly kinetic — the total (minus friction losses) stays the same.

**Analogy.** Pouring water between cups — you can move it around, but no water appears or vanishes.

**Why it matters.** It underpins all energy analysis and rules out "free energy" devices.

**Misconception.** *"A roller coaster loses energy and that energy is gone."* Wrong — it converts to heat and sound in the rails and air. Corrective: "lost" energy is transformed, not destroyed.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the law of conservation of energy. (recall)
2. *Describe* how energy is conserved on a roller coaster. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a pendulum eventually stops even though energy is conserved. (analyse)

### Kinetic Energy

**Definition.** Kinetic energy is the energy of a moving object: **Ek = ½ m v²**, unit joules (J).

**Real-world example.** A fast car has far more kinetic energy than a slow one — and doubling speed quadruples it, which is why high speeds are dangerous.

**Analogy.** Kinetic energy is the "punch" a moving object can deliver — heavier and faster means a bigger punch.

**Why it matters.** Explains braking distances, collisions and the danger of speed.

**Worked example.** A 2 kg ball moves at 3 m/s.
Ek = ½ m v² = ½ × 2 × 3² = ½ × 2 × 9 = **9 J**.

**Misconception.** *"Doubling the speed doubles the kinetic energy."* Wrong — because of the v² term, doubling speed *quadruples* Ek. Corrective: Ek depends on speed squared.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the formula for kinetic energy. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the kinetic energy of a 4 kg object moving at 5 m/s. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a small increase in a car's speed greatly increases its stopping energy. (analyse)

### Gravitational Potential Energy

**Definition.** Gravitational PE is energy stored due to an object's height in a gravitational field: **ΔEp = m g Δh**, unit joules (J), with g ≈ 10 N/kg.

**Real-world example.** Water stored high in a reservoir has gravitational PE that becomes kinetic energy as it falls to drive turbines.

**Analogy.** Lifting a book to a shelf is like "charging it up" with stored energy ready to be released if it falls.

**Why it matters.** Explains hydroelectric power, roller coasters and why falls from height are dangerous.

**Worked example.** A 3 kg object is lifted 2 m (g = 10 N/kg).
ΔEp = m g Δh = 3 × 10 × 2 = **60 J**.

**Misconception.** *"Heavier objects always have more potential energy."* Wrong — PE also depends on height; a light object placed very high can have more PE than a heavy one on the floor. Corrective: PE depends on both mass and height.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the formula for gravitational potential energy. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the PE gained by a 2 kg book lifted 1.5 m. (apply)
3. *Explain* how a hydroelectric dam converts gravitational PE into electricity. (analyse)

### Elastic Potential Energy

**Definition.** Elastic potential energy is energy stored in a stretched or compressed object such as a spring or rubber band. (Mentioned as a type at this level.)

**Real-world example.** A drawn bow stores elastic PE that becomes the arrow's kinetic energy on release.

**Analogy.** A loaded spring is like a coiled snake, holding energy ready to spring out.

**Why it matters.** Explains catapults, trampolines, and clockwork mechanisms.

**Misconception.** *"Only metal springs store elastic energy."* Wrong — any elastic material (rubber, bungee cords) stores it when deformed. Corrective: elastic PE is stored in any stretched or compressed elastic object.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Define* elastic potential energy. (recall)
2. *Identify* the energy stored in a stretched catapult. (apply)
3. *Explain* the energy transformation when a compressed spring is released. (analyse)

### Work Done

**Definition.** Work is done when a force moves an object in the direction of the force: **W = force × displacement (in the same direction)**, unit joules (J).

**Real-world example.** Pushing a trolley 5 m across a shop does work; holding a heavy bag still does *no* physics work (no movement).

**Analogy.** Work is the "effort that actually got the job moved along" — straining without movement doesn't count.

**Why it matters.** Work is the link between force and energy transfer.

**Worked example.** A force of 20 N pushes a box 4 m in the direction of the force.
W = F × displacement = 20 × 4 = **80 J**.

**Misconception.** *"You do work just by pushing hard on a wall."* Wrong — if the wall doesn't move, displacement is zero, so work done is zero, however tiring. Corrective: no movement in the force's direction = no work done.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the formula for work done. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the work done lifting a box with a 50 N force through 3 m. (apply)
3. *Explain* why holding a heavy weight still does no physics work. (analyse)

### Power

**Definition.** Power is the rate of doing work (or transferring energy): **power = work done ÷ time**, unit watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.

**Real-world example.** Two cranes lift the same load, but the more powerful one does it faster.

**Analogy.** Power is like how fast you can eat a pizza — same pizza (work), but a faster eater has more "power."

**Why it matters.** Power rates appliances and machines, telling us how quickly they deliver energy.

**Worked example.** A motor does 600 J of work in 4 s.
Power = work ÷ time = 600 ÷ 4 = **150 W**.

**Misconception.** *"A more powerful machine always does more total work."* Wrong — power is about *speed* of doing work; two machines can do equal work, the more powerful one just does it faster. Corrective: power = work per unit time, not total work.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the unit of power. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the power of a device doing 900 J in 3 s. (apply)
3. *Explain* the difference between two engines with the same work output but different power. (analyse)

### Efficiency & Sankey Diagrams

**Definition.** Efficiency is the fraction of input energy transformed into useful output, as a percentage: **efficiency = (useful energy output ÷ total energy input) × 100%**. A *Sankey diagram* shows energy flow as arrows whose widths are proportional to the energy — the useful output continues forward and wasted energy branches off.

**Real-world example.** An LED bulb turning most electrical energy into light is more efficient than an old filament bulb that wastes most as heat.

**Analogy.** A Sankey diagram is like a river splitting into a main channel (useful energy) and side streams (wasted energy) — the widths add up to the source.

**Why it matters.** Efficiency guides energy-saving design and reduces waste.

**Described diagram.** For a bulb: a wide arrow of "100 J electrical" enters; a narrow "20 J light" arrow continues (useful) and an "80 J heat" arrow branches downward (wasted). Widths are drawn to scale.

**Worked example.** A lamp takes in 100 J and gives out 20 J of light.
Efficiency = (20 ÷ 100) × 100 = **20%**.

**Misconception.** *"Wasted energy in a Sankey diagram just disappears."* Wrong — it is still counted (often as heat) so total in = total out; the arrows must balance. Corrective: input width = useful width + wasted width (energy conserved).

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the formula for efficiency. (recall)
2. *Calculate* the efficiency of a motor using 400 J to produce 300 J of useful output. (apply)
3. *Interpret* a Sankey diagram to identify where energy is wasted and suggest an improvement. (evaluate)

### Energy Resources: Renewable & Non-Renewable

**Definition.** *Renewable* resources are naturally replenished on a human timescale (solar, wind, hydro, tidal); *non-renewable* resources (coal, oil, gas, nuclear fuel) exist in finite amounts and run out. The **Sun is the ultimate source** of most of Earth's energy (driving wind, waves, hydro cycle, and stored in fossil fuels).

| Resource | Type | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|----------|------|-----------|--------------|
| Solar | Renewable | Clean, unlimited sunlight | Depends on weather/daylight |
| Wind | Renewable | No fuel cost, low emissions | Intermittent, needs wind |
| Hydro | Renewable | Reliable, large scale | Dams disrupt ecosystems |
| Tidal | Renewable | Predictable | High build cost, few sites |
| Fossil fuels | Non-renewable | High energy density, reliable | Pollution, CO₂, finite |

**Real-world example.** Solar farms and wind turbines increasingly replace coal power stations to cut carbon emissions.

**Analogy.** Renewable energy is like a well that refills; non-renewable is a tank that empties for good.

**Why it matters.** The choice of energy resources affects climate, economy and sustainability — central to MYP Criterion D.

**Implications & latest discoveries.** Advances include higher-efficiency perovskite solar cells, floating offshore wind farms, large grid-scale batteries for storing renewable energy, green hydrogen produced from renewable electricity, and progress toward nuclear fusion for clean, high-output power. These aim to reduce fossil-fuel dependence and greenhouse emissions.

**Misconception.** *"Renewable energy produces no pollution at all."* Wrong — manufacturing panels/turbines and building dams have environmental costs, even if running them is clean. Corrective: renewables are far cleaner in operation but not entirely impact-free.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *List* three renewable energy resources. (recall)
2. *Classify* natural gas and tidal power as renewable or non-renewable. (apply)
3. *Evaluate* whether a coastal town should invest in tidal power, considering advantages, disadvantages and implications. (evaluate)

## 2.3 Magnetism

### Properties of Magnets

**Definition.** A magnet has two poles — north (N) and south (S). Poles always occur in pairs; like poles repel, unlike poles attract. The force is strongest at the poles.

**Real-world example.** A compass needle (a small magnet) turns so its north pole points toward Earth's magnetic north.

**Analogy.** Like poles are like two people who dislike each other backing away; unlike poles are like friends drawn together.

**Why it matters.** These rules underlie compasses, motors, generators and data storage.

**Misconception.** *"You can get a single north pole by cutting a magnet in half."* Wrong — each piece becomes a new complete magnet with both N and S poles. Corrective: poles always exist in pairs (no magnetic monopole).

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the rule for like and unlike magnetic poles. (recall)
2. *Predict* what happens when the north poles of two magnets are brought together. (apply)
3. *Explain* why breaking a bar magnet does not isolate a single pole. (analyse)

### Magnetic vs Non-Magnetic Materials

**Definition.** *Magnetic materials* (iron, nickel, cobalt and steel) are attracted to magnets; *non-magnetic materials* (plastic, wood, copper, aluminium, glass) are not.

**Real-world example.** A magnet picks up steel paper clips but not plastic or aluminium ones.

**Analogy.** Magnetic materials "answer the call" of a magnet; non-magnetic ones ignore it.

**Why it matters.** Sorting scrap metal, magnetic separation and design of devices depend on knowing which materials respond.

**Misconception.** *"All metals are magnetic."* Wrong — copper, gold and aluminium are metals but not magnetic. Corrective: only certain metals (iron, nickel, cobalt and their alloys) are magnetic.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *List* two magnetic and two non-magnetic materials. (recall)
2. *Identify* whether an aluminium can is attracted to a magnet. (apply)
3. *Explain* how a magnet could be used to separate iron filings from sand. (analyse)

### Magnetic Field Lines

**Definition.** A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where its force acts. *Field lines* are drawn from N to S outside the magnet; they never cross, and where they are closer together the field is stronger.

**Real-world example.** Iron filings sprinkled around a bar magnet arrange themselves along the field lines, revealing the pattern.

**Analogy.** Field lines are like invisible "wind currents" flowing out of the north pole and into the south pole.

**Why it matters.** Field lines let us visualise an invisible force and predict how magnets and compasses behave.

**Described diagram (bar magnet field lines).** Curved lines leave the N pole, loop around the outside, and enter the S pole; lines are densest and most tightly packed near the poles (strongest field) and more spread out at the sides (weaker field). Arrows point from N to S outside the magnet.

**Using a compass.** Place a plotting compass near the magnet; the needle aligns with the field. Mark the direction at many points and join them to trace the field lines.

**Misconception.** *"Magnetic field lines can cross each other."* Wrong — crossing would mean two field directions at one point, which is impossible. Corrective: field lines never intersect.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* the direction field lines point outside a magnet. (recall)
2. *Describe* how iron filings reveal a magnet's field. (apply)
3. *Explain* what the spacing of field lines tells you about field strength. (analyse)

### Magnetizing, Demagnetizing & Induced Magnetism

**Definition.** A material can be *magnetized* by stroking it with a magnet in one direction, placing it in a strong field, or passing electric current through a coil around it. It can be *demagnetized* by heating, hammering, or applying an alternating current — all of which jumble the aligned domains. *Induced magnetism* is when a magnetic material becomes a temporary magnet while near a magnet.

**Real-world example.** A steel needle stroked with a magnet becomes a compass; dropping or heating a magnet weakens it.

**Analogy.** Magnetizing lines up tiny "compass" domains to point the same way; demagnetizing shakes them back into disorder, like tidying then scattering a row of pencils.

**Why it matters.** Explains how magnets are made, why they weaken, and how magnetic devices work.

**Misconception.** *"A magnet stays magnetic forever no matter what."* Wrong — heating, hammering or strong opposing fields can demagnetize it. Corrective: magnetism can be lost when domains are disrupted.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* one way to magnetize a steel bar. (recall)
2. *Describe* what happens to the domains when a magnet is heated strongly. (apply)
3. *Explain* how a paper clip becomes magnetic when held near a magnet (induced magnetism). (analyse)

### Soft Iron vs Steel; Permanent vs Electromagnets

**Definition.** *Soft magnetic materials* (soft iron) magnetize and demagnetize easily — good for temporary magnets. *Hard magnetic materials* (steel) are harder to magnetize but keep their magnetism — good for permanent magnets. A *permanent magnet* keeps its magnetism; an *electromagnet* is a coil of wire around a soft iron core that is magnetic only while current flows.

**Real-world example.** Electromagnets in scrapyard cranes lift cars and drop them by switching the current off; fridge magnets are permanent.

**Analogy.** Soft iron is like a light switch magnet — on when powered, off when not; steel is like a permanent tattoo of magnetism.

**Why it matters.** Choosing the right material and magnet type is key to motors, cranes, speakers, doorbells and data storage.

**Applications.** Electromagnets: cranes, electric bells, relays, MRI machines, electric motors. Permanent magnets: compasses, fridge magnets, loudspeakers, simple generators.

**Misconception.** *"An electromagnet stays magnetic after you switch off the current."* Wrong — a soft-iron-core electromagnet loses almost all magnetism when the current stops. Corrective: electromagnet magnetism depends on current flowing.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* one use of an electromagnet. (recall)
2. *Compare* the magnetic behaviour of soft iron and steel. (apply)
3. *Explain* why an electromagnet is more suitable than a permanent magnet for a scrapyard crane. (evaluate)

## 3.1 Models of the Universe

### Big Bang Theory & Timeline

**Definition.** The Big Bang theory states that the universe began about 13.8 billion years ago from an extremely hot, dense point and has been expanding and cooling ever since. Introductory timeline: Big Bang → formation of the first atoms → first stars and galaxies → formation of the solar system → present day.

**Real-world example.** The observed *redshift* of distant galaxies (moving away from us) and the cosmic microwave background radiation are evidence the universe is expanding from a hot origin.

**Analogy.** The expanding universe is like dots on a balloon's surface moving apart as the balloon inflates — every dot sees the others receding.

**Why it matters.** It is the leading scientific model of cosmic origins and a prime example of how models rest on evidence.

**Misconception.** *"The Big Bang was an explosion in empty space."* Wrong — space itself expanded; there was no pre-existing space for it to explode *into*. Corrective: the Big Bang is the expansion *of* space, not an explosion within it.

**Command-term questions.**

3. *Explain* why galaxy redshift suggests an expanding universe. (analyse)

### Heliocentric vs Geocentric Models

**Definition.** The *geocentric* model places Earth at the centre with everything orbiting it. The *heliocentric* model (Copernicus) places the Sun at the centre with planets, including Earth, orbiting it.

**Real-world example.** The apparent daily motion of the Sun across the sky once seemed to support geocentrism, but telescope observations (e.g. Galileo's moons of Jupiter) supported heliocentrism.

**Analogy.** Geocentric thinking is like believing the scenery moves past a parked car; heliocentric is realising the car (Earth) is the one moving.

**Why it matters.** Shows how evidence overturns long-held models — a landmark in the history of science.

**Misconception.** *"The Sun orbits the Earth because it rises and sets."* Wrong — the Sun appears to move because Earth *rotates*. Corrective: Earth's rotation, not the Sun's motion, causes day and night.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* what is at the centre of the heliocentric model. (recall)
2. *Distinguish* between geocentric and heliocentric models. (apply)
3. *Explain* how new evidence led scientists to reject the geocentric model. (evaluate)

### The Solar System: Planets & Asteroid Belt

**Definition.** The solar system is the Sun and everything bound to it by gravity: eight planets, their moons, and smaller bodies. In order from the Sun: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, (asteroid belt), Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune. The *asteroid belt* is a ring of rocky bodies between Mars and Jupiter.

**Real-world example.** The four inner planets are small and rocky; the four outer planets are large gas/ice giants — a pattern set by their formation.

**Analogy.** The Sun is like a campfire and the planets are people sitting at different distances — the closest feels the most heat.

**Why it matters.** Understanding our solar system frames Earth's place and conditions for life.

**Misconception.** *"The asteroid belt is a densely packed field of rocks like in films."* Wrong — asteroids are spread over vast distances; spacecraft pass through easily. Corrective: the belt is mostly empty space with widely separated objects.

**Command-term questions.**

2. *Identify* where the asteroid belt lies. (apply)
3. *Explain* why the inner and outer planets differ in size and composition. (analyse)

### Galaxies — Types

**Definition.** A galaxy is a huge system of stars, gas, dust and dark matter held together by gravity. Main types: *spiral* (arms winding out, like the Milky Way), *elliptical* (rounded/oval, older stars), and *irregular* (no clear shape).

**Real-world example.** We live in the Milky Way, a spiral galaxy of over 100 billion stars.

**Analogy.** Galaxy types are like city layouts — spirals with orderly avenues, ellipticals a rounded blob, irregulars a sprawling unplanned town.

**Why it matters.** Galaxy types reveal how star systems form and evolve over cosmic time.

**Misconception.** *"The Milky Way is the whole universe."* Wrong — there are billions of galaxies beyond ours. Corrective: the Milky Way is just one galaxy among an estimated two trillion.

**Command-term questions.**

### Eclipses

**Definition.** An eclipse occurs when one body's shadow falls on another. A *solar eclipse* happens when the Moon passes between the Sun and Earth, blocking sunlight. A *lunar eclipse* happens when Earth passes between the Sun and Moon, casting Earth's shadow on the Moon.

**Real-world example.** During a total solar eclipse, day briefly turns to twilight along a narrow path on Earth.

**Analogy.** A solar eclipse is like someone holding a coin close to your eye to block a distant lamp; a lunar eclipse is the coin (Moon) falling into a bigger shadow.

**Why it matters.** Eclipses demonstrate the geometry and motion of the Sun-Earth-Moon system.

**Described diagram.** Solar eclipse: Sun — Moon — Earth in a line, Moon's shadow hitting Earth. Lunar eclipse: Sun — Earth — Moon in a line, Earth's shadow hitting the Moon.

**Misconception.** *"Eclipses happen every month at full and new moon."* Wrong — the Moon's orbit is tilted, so alignment is exact only occasionally. Corrective: eclipses need the three bodies to line up precisely, which is rare.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* what lies between the Sun and Earth during a solar eclipse. (recall)
2. *Describe* the arrangement of bodies during a lunar eclipse. (apply)
3. *Explain* why eclipses do not occur every month. (analyse)

### Life Cycle of a Star

**Definition.** A star forms from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust (nebula), shines steadily during its *main sequence* while fusing hydrogen, then swells into a red giant. Low-mass stars end as a white dwarf; massive stars explode as a supernova, leaving a neutron star or black hole.

**Real-world example.** Our Sun is a middle-aged main-sequence star that will become a red giant, then a white dwarf, in billions of years.

**Analogy.** A star's life is like a human's — born, long adult life, then old age and death — but scaled to billions of years.

**Why it matters.** Star life cycles explain where chemical elements (including those in our bodies) come from.

**Described diagram.** A flow: Nebula → Protostar → Main-sequence star → Red giant → (low mass) White dwarf, or (high mass) Supernova → Neutron star / Black hole.

**Misconception.** *"Stars live forever."* Wrong — stars have finite fuel and eventually die. Corrective: every star has a life cycle ending when its fuel runs out.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* what a star forms from. (recall)
2. *Describe* the stages in the life cycle of a low-mass star like the Sun. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a massive star ends differently from a low-mass star. (analyse)

### Celestial Bodies: Comets, Asteroids, Meteors, Meteorites

**Definition.**
- *Comet:* icy body that grows a glowing tail of gas and dust when near the Sun.
- *Asteroid:* rocky body, mostly in the asteroid belt.
- *Meteoroid:* small rock/debris in space.
- *Meteor:* a meteoroid burning up in Earth's atmosphere — a "shooting star."
- *Meteorite:* a meteor that survives and lands on Earth's surface.

**Real-world example.** Halley's Comet returns about every 76 years; meteor showers occur when Earth passes through comet debris.

**Analogy.** A meteoroid is a stone in space, a meteor is that stone glowing as it "skids" through the air, and a meteorite is the stone that hits the ground.

**Why it matters.** These bodies carry clues about the solar system's formation and pose occasional impact hazards.

**Misconception.** *"A shooting star is a real star falling."* Wrong — it is a small rock burning up in the atmosphere. Corrective: meteors are tiny debris, not stars.

**Command-term questions.**

2. *Distinguish* between a meteor and a meteorite. (apply)
3. *Explain* why a comet develops a tail as it nears the Sun. (analyse)

### Satellites & Types

**Definition.** A satellite is any object that orbits a larger body. *Natural satellites* are moons; *artificial satellites* are human-made and include communication, weather, GPS/navigation, and scientific/observation satellites.

**Real-world example.** The Moon is Earth's natural satellite; GPS satellites let phones pinpoint location.

**Analogy.** A satellite orbiting is like a ball whirled on a string — gravity is the "string" keeping it circling.

**Why it matters.** Satellites underpin modern communication, navigation, weather forecasting and Earth monitoring.

**Misconception.** *"Satellites stay up because there is no gravity in space."* Wrong — gravity is what holds them in orbit; they are constantly falling *around* Earth. Corrective: orbit is continuous free-fall balanced by forward speed, not the absence of gravity.

**Command-term questions.**

2. *List* two uses of artificial satellites. (apply)
3. *Explain* how gravity keeps a satellite in orbit rather than letting it fly off. (analyse)

### Phases of the Moon

**Definition.** The Moon's phases are the changing shapes of its sunlit side as seen from Earth as it orbits us: new moon → waxing crescent → first quarter → waxing gibbous → full moon → waning gibbous → last quarter → waning crescent, over about 29.5 days.

**Real-world example.** A full moon rises around sunset; a new moon is invisible because its sunlit side faces away from Earth.

**Analogy.** Shine a torch on a ball and walk around it — you see different amounts lit, just as we see different Moon phases.

**Why it matters.** Phases show the geometry of the Sun-Earth-Moon system and drive tides and calendars.

**Described diagram.** The Moon orbits Earth; half the Moon is always sunlit, but from Earth we see varying fractions of that lit half, producing the cycle of phases.

**Misconception.** *"Moon phases are caused by Earth's shadow on the Moon."* Wrong — that is a lunar eclipse. Phases come from which part of the sunlit Moon faces us. Corrective: phases result from the Moon's orbital position, not Earth's shadow.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *Name* the phase when the Moon is fully lit. (recall)
2. *Describe* why we cannot see the Moon at new moon. (apply)
3. *Explain* why Moon phases are not caused by Earth's shadow. (analyse)

### Implications of Space Research

**Definition.** Space research is the scientific study and exploration of space; its findings and technologies affect society, the economy and the environment.

**Real-world example.** Spin-off technologies from space programmes include satellite communications, GPS, weather prediction, water-purification systems, and medical imaging improvements.

**Analogy.** Space research is like planting seeds whose benefits (technology, knowledge) sprout unexpectedly across daily life.

**Why it matters.** It drives innovation and global cooperation but raises debates about cost, priorities and space debris — central to MYP Criterion D.

**Implications.** Benefits include navigation, communication, disaster monitoring, and scientific knowledge; concerns include high costs, space debris, and whether resources should address Earthly needs first.

**Misconception.** *"Space research only benefits astronauts and has no use on Earth."* Wrong — countless everyday technologies came from it. Corrective: space research yields wide practical benefits for everyday life.

**Command-term questions.**
1. *State* one everyday technology that came from space research. (recall)
2. *Describe* one benefit and one drawback of space exploration. (apply)
3. *Evaluate* whether governments should continue funding space research given needs on Earth. (evaluate)

# Common Misconceptions Master List

1. **"Heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones."** In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall with the same acceleration (~10 m/s²); mass does not affect fall rate. A feather falls slowly only because of air resistance, not its lightness.
2. **"Mass and weight are the same."** Mass (kg) is the amount of matter and is constant; weight (N) is the gravitational force on that mass and changes with location.
3. **"A moving object needs a constant force to keep moving."** Force is required to *change* motion, not to maintain it; without friction, motion continues on its own.
4. **"If forces are balanced, the object must be at rest."** Balanced forces can also mean constant-velocity motion; zero net force means no change in motion, not necessarily no motion.
5. **"Precision and accuracy mean the same thing."** Precision is repeatability; accuracy is closeness to the true value — a measurement can be one without the other.
6. **"Doubling speed doubles kinetic energy."** Ek depends on v², so doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy.
7. **"Energy gets used up and disappears."** Energy is transformed, often into waste heat, but the total is always conserved.
8. **"Cutting a magnet gives a single pole."** Every piece keeps both a north and a south pole; no magnetic monopole exists.
9. **"All metals are magnetic."** Only iron, nickel, cobalt and their alloys are magnetic; copper, aluminium and gold are not.
10. **"A shooting star is a falling star."** It is a small meteoroid burning up in the atmosphere, not a star.
11. **"Moon phases are caused by Earth's shadow."** Phases come from the Moon's orbital position and which part of its sunlit side faces Earth; Earth's shadow causes lunar eclipses only.
12. **"The Big Bang was an explosion in space."** Space itself expanded; there was no surrounding space to explode into.
13. **"A pushing force on a wall does work even if it doesn't move."** No displacement means zero work done in physics.
14. **"Satellites stay up because there is no gravity in space."** Gravity provides the centripetal pull that keeps them orbiting; they are in continual free-fall around Earth.

# Cross-Subject Connections

- **Mathematics:** Motion graphs use gradient (slope) and area calculations; unit conversion, rearranging formulas (v = u + at, ρ = m/V), ratios and percentages (efficiency, mechanical advantage), and plotting/interpreting linear graphs all draw directly on maths skills. Scientific notation and significant figures link to number work.
- **Chemistry:** Density and states of matter connect to particle theory and material properties; energy resources and combustion of fossil fuels link to chemical reactions and chemical energy stored in bonds; the Big Bang and stellar life cycles explain the origin of chemical elements.
- **Geography:** Renewable and non-renewable energy resources, their advantages, disadvantages and environmental implications, tie to human and physical geography, climate change, and sustainability; tides (linked to the Moon) and the water cycle (driven by the Sun) connect astronomy and forces to Earth systems.
