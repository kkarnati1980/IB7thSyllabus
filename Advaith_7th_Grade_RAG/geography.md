## Subject Overview

### Where this subject sits
Geography in the IISH MYP belongs to the **Individuals and Societies (I&S)** subject group. I&S encourages students to study human and physical environments, and to appreciate that people, places, resources and societies are deeply connected. Grade 7 Geography focuses on **environmental regions and societies, resources and the environment, and the policy and governance of development.**

### MYP Individuals & Societies Assessment Criteria
Each criterion is marked out of 8 (levels 1–8) using published rubrics.

| Criterion | Title | What it assesses |
|-----------|-------|------------------|
| **A** | Knowing and understanding | Use terminology in context; demonstrate knowledge and understanding of content and concepts through descriptions, explanations and examples. |
| **B** | Investigating | Formulate a clear research question; develop and follow an action plan; collect and record information; reflect on the process. |
| **C** | Communicating | Communicate information and ideas with a clear structure; organise information appropriately; document sources correctly. |
| **D** | Thinking critically | Analyse concepts, issues, models and arguments; interpret and evaluate sources (OPVL); recognise different perspectives and their implications; synthesise information to make arguments. |

### I&S Related Concepts (used throughout the year)
- **Causality** (cause and effect) — why biomes, poverty or scarcity occur.
- **Processes** — soil formation, nutrient cycling, the water cycle, development.
- **Sustainability** — meeting present needs without harming the future.
- **Resources** — natural, human and man-made means of meeting needs.
- **Disparity / equity** — the North–South divide, inequality, uneven development.
- **Perspective** — different stakeholders view resource use and development differently.
- **Globalization, power, culture, cooperation** — supporting concepts used in Units 2–3.

### MYP Key Concepts most used this year
**Systems, Global interactions, Change,** and **Time, place and space.**

# UNIT 1: Environmental Regions and Societies

**Key Concept:** Systems
**Related Concepts:** Processes, Causality, Sustainability
**Global Context:** *Orientation in space and time* (natural landscapes and the relationships between people and the environments they inhabit).
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Climatic and physical processes create distinct environmental systems whose fragile balance shapes how societies adapt, survive and must conserve them.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- **Factual:** What are the world's major biomes and where are they located?
- **Conceptual:** How do climate processes and pressure belts cause the distribution of biomes?
- **Debatable:** Should the survival of indigenous cultures matter more than the economic development of the biomes they live in?

## Chapter 1.1 — Global Distribution of Biomes and Climate Zones

### Concept: Biome
**Definition:** A biome is a large-scale community of plants and animals adapted to a particular climate, spread across large regions of the Earth.
**Real-world example:** The Amazon in Brazil is part of the tropical rainforest biome; the Sahara in North Africa is part of the hot desert biome.
**Analogy:** A biome is like a "neighbourhood" of the natural world — every home (species) in it is built to suit the same climate "weather rules."
**Why it matters:** Biomes determine what can grow, what animals live there, how people make a living, and how vulnerable a region is to climate change.
**Key facts/data:**
- Biomes are defined mainly by **climate (temperature + precipitation)** and **vegetation**, not by country borders.
- Major biomes: tropical rainforest, taiga/coniferous forest, savanna, temperate grassland, hot desert, tundra, Mediterranean, temperate deciduous forest.
- Biomes generally form in **latitudinal bands** because temperature and rainfall change with latitude.
**Misconception:** *"A biome is the same as an ecosystem."* Believed because both involve plants, animals and environment. Wrong because an **ecosystem** is a smaller local unit (a single pond or forest patch) while a **biome** is a global-scale grouping of many similar ecosystems. Corrective: think biome = global category, ecosystem = local example within it.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Define** the term "biome." 
2. (Apply) **Describe** how latitude influences the location of two named biomes.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Analyse** why biome boundaries rarely match political (country) boundaries.

### Concept: Global Pressure Belt System and Prevailing Winds
**Definition:** A pattern of alternating high- and low-pressure zones circling the Earth that controls where air rises (wet) and sinks (dry), driving the location of biomes; prevailing winds are the dominant direction winds blow in a region.
**Real-world example:** Rising air at the Equator (low pressure) produces the rain that feeds the Congo rainforest; sinking air around 30°N (high pressure) creates the Sahara Desert.
**Analogy:** The pressure belts are like a global conveyor belt of air — where the belt lifts air it "wrings out" rain; where it presses air down it "dries the towel."
**Why it matters:** Explains *why* deserts sit near 30° latitude and rainforests near the Equator — connecting physics to ecology.
**Key facts/data:**
- **Equatorial low (0°):** rising air, heavy rain → rainforests.
- **Subtropical high (~30° N/S):** sinking air, dry → hot deserts.
- **Subpolar low (~60°):** rising air, moderate rain → temperate forests/taiga.
- **Polar high (~90°):** sinking cold air, very dry → tundra/ice.
- Prevailing winds (e.g. **trade winds** near the tropics, **westerlies** in mid-latitudes) carry moisture and shape rainfall.
**Misconception:** *"It rains most at the poles because they are cold."* Believed because cold feels wet. Wrong: cold air holds little moisture and polar highs make air sink and dry — poles are technically "cold deserts." Corrective: rainfall depends on rising air and warmth, not coldness.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **State** what happens to air at the equatorial low-pressure belt.
2. (Apply) **Explain** how the subtropical high-pressure belt helps create hot deserts.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the claim that pressure belts are the single most important cause of biome distribution.

### Climate Zones — Comparison Table
| Climate zone | Temperature | Rainfall | Location example | Linked biome |
|--------------|-------------|----------|------------------|--------------|
| Equatorial | Hot all year (~27°C) | Very high, all year (>2000 mm) | Amazon Basin, Brazil | Tropical rainforest |
| Tropical monsoon | Hot | Very high in wet season, dry season present | India, SE Asia | Monsoon forest |
| Hot desert | Very hot days, cold nights | Very low (<250 mm) | Sahara, Africa | Hot desert |
| Savanna (tropical grassland) | Hot | Distinct wet & dry seasons | Serengeti, Tanzania | Savanna grassland |
| Temperate grassland | Warm summers, cold winters | Moderate | Prairies, USA; Steppes, Ukraine | Temperate grassland |
| Warm temperate (Mediterranean) | Hot dry summers, mild wet winters | Moderate, winter rain | Southern Spain, California | Mediterranean scrub |
| Cool temperate (Taiga) | Cold winters, short cool summers | Low–moderate | Siberia, Russia; Canada | Coniferous forest/taiga |
| Arctic (Tundra) | Freezing most of year | Very low | Northern Alaska, N. Canada | Tundra |

**Relationship between climate zones and biomes:** Each climate zone produces a matching biome because vegetation and animals evolve to survive that specific temperature and rainfall pattern. Climate is the *cause*; the biome is the *result*.

## Chapter 1.2 — Wet Forests

### Biome Profile: Tropical Rainforest
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Around the Equator — Amazon (Brazil), Congo Basin (DR Congo), Southeast Asia (Indonesia, Malaysia). |
| **Climate** | Hot (~27°C) and wet all year (>2000 mm); no real seasons. |
| **Soil & nutrient cycling** | Soils (latosols) are surprisingly **poor/infertile**; nutrients are stored in the *vegetation*, not the soil. Fast **nutrient cycling**: dead leaves decompose rapidly in heat and humidity and are taken straight back up by plants. |
| **Plant adaptations** | Buttress roots for support in thin soil; drip-tip leaves to shed water; lianas and epiphytes climb to reach light; layered canopy. |
| **Animal adaptations** | Camouflage, prehensile tails, bright warning colours, nocturnal habits; many species living at different canopy levels. |
| **Indigenous lifestyle** | Peoples such as the **Yanomami (Amazon)** practise shifting cultivation, hunting, fishing and gathering — using the forest sustainably without permanently clearing it. |
| **Threats** | Deforestation for cattle ranching, soya, palm oil, logging, roads and mining. |
| **Global importance** | "Lungs of the Earth" — produces oxygen, absorbs CO₂, stores carbon, holds ~50% of the world's biodiversity, drives the water cycle. |
| **Conservation** | Protected reserves/national parks, ecotourism, selective logging, agroforestry, debt-for-nature swaps, indigenous land rights, REDD+ carbon schemes. |

**Analogy:** The rainforest soil is like a bank account with almost no cash saved — all the "money" (nutrients) is constantly circulating in the "economy" of living plants.
**Why it matters:** Rainforests regulate global climate; losing them accelerates climate change and biodiversity loss.
**Misconception:** *"Rainforests have rich, fertile soil because they are so green and full of life."* Believed because lush vegetation suggests rich ground. Wrong: the soil is nutrient-poor; the richness is stored in the fast-cycling living biomass. Corrective: cut the forest down and the fertility disappears within a few years.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **List** three plant adaptations of the tropical rainforest.
2. (Apply) **Explain** why rainforest soils become infertile soon after clearing.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Discuss** whether conservation of the Amazon should override national economic development. *(Related SDGs: 13 Climate Action, 15 Life on Land.)*

### Biome Profile: Coniferous Forest / Taiga (Boreal Forest)
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | High northern latitudes (~50–60°N) — Siberia (Russia), Canada, Scandinavia. |
| **Climate** | Long, very cold winters; short, cool summers; low–moderate precipitation (much as snow). |
| **Soil** | Thin, acidic, nutrient-poor **podzols**; slow decomposition due to cold; needle litter adds acidity. |
| **Plant/animal adaptations** | Conifers (pine, spruce, fir) with needle leaves (reduce water loss, shed snow), conical shape, evergreen for quick growth in short summer; animals (bears, moose, lynx) with thick fur, hibernation, migration. |
| **Indigenous lifestyle** | Peoples such as the **Sami (Scandinavia)** herd reindeer; others hunt and trap, moving with the seasons. |
| **Threats** | Commercial logging, paper/pulp industry, mining, oil & gas, wildfires, acid rain, climate warming. |
| **Role in global climate** | Vast carbon store; the largest land biome; influences global oxygen and carbon balance. |
| **Conservation** | Sustainable forestry, replanting, protected areas, certification (FSC), fire management. |

**Analogy:** Conifer needles are like drinking straws with wax coats — they hold onto water through the frozen months when the ground water is locked as ice.
**Misconception:** *"Evergreen trees don't lose their leaves."* Wrong: they shed needles gradually all year, just not all at once. Corrective: "evergreen" means never fully bare, not never dropping needles.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Identify** two adaptations of coniferous trees.
2. (Apply) **Describe** how the Sami adapt their lifestyle to the taiga climate.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the importance of the taiga as a global carbon store. *(SDG 13, 15.)*

## Chapter 1.3 — Grasslands

### Biome Profile: Tropical Grassland / Savanna
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Between rainforests and hot deserts (~5–15° N/S) — Serengeti (Tanzania/Kenya), Sahel, northern Australia. |
| **Climate & seasonal change** | Hot all year; a distinct **wet season and dry season** — the defining feature. |
| **Soil** | Fairly fertile in places; dry-season fires return nutrients; can bake hard and crack. |
| **Adaptations** | Grasses grow fast in the wet season and lie dormant/deep-rooted in the dry; acacia trees have thorns and small waxy leaves; animals migrate to find water (wildebeest) or store fat. |
| **Threats** | Overgrazing, farming expansion, poaching, desertification, climate change. |
| **Ecological importance** | Supports the world's largest herds and iconic megafauna; grazing/fire cycle maintains grassland. |
| **Conservation** | National parks (Serengeti NP), anti-poaching, controlled grazing, community conservancies, ecotourism. |

**Analogy:** Savanna grass is like a phone on low-power mode in the dry season — it shuts down above ground and stores energy in the roots until the "recharge" of the rains.
**Misconception:** *"Bushfires in the savanna are always disasters."* Wrong: periodic fire is natural and *necessary* — it recycles nutrients and stops forest taking over. Corrective: the savanna is a fire-adapted biome; suppressing all fire can harm it.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **State** the two seasons of the savanna.
2. (Apply) **Explain** how wildebeest are adapted to seasonal savanna change.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Discuss** the role of fire in maintaining the savanna. *(SDG 15.)*

### Biome Profile: Temperate Grassland
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Mid-latitude continental interiors — Prairies (North America), Pampas (Argentina), Steppes (Ukraine/Central Asia), Veld (S. Africa). |
| **Climate** | Warm summers, cold winters; moderate rainfall (mostly in summer); no trees over much of the area. |
| **Soil & agriculture** | Deep, dark, very **fertile chernozem** soils rich in humus — among the best farmland on Earth ("breadbaskets"). |
| **Adaptations** | Grasses with deep, dense root mats; grazing animals (bison, pronghorn) with speed and herding; burrowing animals (prairie dogs). |
| **Indigenous lifestyle** | Plains peoples (e.g. **Native American Plains nations**) historically followed bison herds; Mongolian steppe herders are nomadic pastoralists. |
| **Threats** | Ploughing for cereals, overgrazing, soil erosion (Dust Bowl), urban spread. |
| **Economic importance** | Grow the world's wheat, maize and support cattle ranching — vital to global food supply. |
| **Conservation** | Contour ploughing, crop rotation, shelter belts, protected prairie reserves, no-till farming. |

**Analogy:** Prairie roots form a mat like a woven carpet holding the soil — rip up the carpet (plough badly) and the wind blows the "floor" away, as in the 1930s Dust Bowl.
**Misconception:** *"Grasslands are empty, unused land."* Wrong: they are the planet's most productive farmland and rich ecosystems. Corrective: "few trees" does not mean "unimportant."
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Name** two temperate grasslands and their continents.
2. (Apply) **Explain** why chernozem soils make grasslands important for agriculture.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Analyse** the causes and consequences of the 1930s Dust Bowl. *(SDG 2, 15.)*

## Chapter 1.4 — Extreme Environments

### Biome Profile: Hot Desert
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Around 30° N/S under subtropical high pressure — Sahara (Africa), Arabian, Thar (India), Australian outback. |
| **Climate** | Very hot days, cold nights; extreme temperature range; rainfall <250 mm/year, very unreliable. |
| **Soil** | Thin, sandy/stony, low organic matter, often salty; little vegetation to enrich it. |
| **Adaptations** | Plants: cacti store water, spines reduce loss and deter grazers, deep or wide roots, waxy skins. Animals: camels store fat, nocturnal habits, water conservation, light coloured fur. |
| **Indigenous lifestyle** | Nomads such as the **Tuareg (Sahara)** and **Bedouin (Arabia)** travel between water sources, herd camels/goats, use tents and light clothing. |
| **Threats & uniqueness** | Mining, oil extraction, over-abstraction of water, tourism, desertification at edges; unique adaptations found nowhere else. |
| **Conservation** | Protected reserves, sustainable water use, solar-energy projects, controlled grazing, anti-desertification tree planting (e.g. Great Green Wall). |

**Analogy:** A cactus is like a living water bottle with a spiky lid — it hoards every drop and defends it.
**Misconception:** *"Deserts are just lifeless seas of sand."* Wrong: most deserts are rocky, not sandy, and teem with specially adapted life. Corrective: deserts are defined by low rainfall, not by sand or by being lifeless.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Define** a hot desert by its rainfall.
2. (Apply) **Describe** two adaptations of the camel to desert life.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the benefits and drawbacks of large-scale solar projects in deserts. *(SDG 7, 15.)*

### Biome Profile: Tundra
| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Far north (Arctic) — northern Alaska, northern Canada, northern Russia; also alpine tundra on high mountains. |
| **Climate** | Freezing for most of the year; short cool summer; very low precipitation ("cold desert"). |
| **Soil & permafrost** | Thin, waterlogged soil over **permafrost** (permanently frozen subsoil) that blocks drainage and root growth. |
| **Adaptations** | Plants: low-growing cushions and mosses, grow fast in short summer, hairy stems. Animals: thick fur/blubber, white winter camouflage, migration (caribou), hibernation. |
| **Vulnerability to climate change** | Warming thaws permafrost, releasing stored methane/CO₂ (a feedback loop); very fragile, slow to recover. |
| **Indigenous lifestyle** | The **Inuit** hunt seals, caribou and fish; historically use skins, sleds and detailed knowledge of ice and seasons. |
| **Threats** | Oil & gas extraction, pipelines, mining, climate warming, pollution. |
| **Conservation** | Protected wilderness, strict extraction limits, cutting global emissions, supporting indigenous stewardship. |

**Analogy:** Permafrost is like a frozen freezer floor beneath a thin thawed rug — water can't drain through it, so the surface stays soggy and boggy in summer.
**Misconception:** *"Nothing lives in the tundra."* Wrong: it hosts caribou, arctic foxes, migrating birds and hardy plants. Corrective: life is sparse and specialised, not absent.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Define** "permafrost."
2. (Apply) **Explain** how the Inuit adapt their lifestyle to the tundra.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Analyse** why the tundra is especially vulnerable to climate change. *(SDG 13, 15.)*

# UNIT 2: Resources and Environment

**Key Concept:** Change
**Related Concepts:** Resources, Sustainability, Processes
**Global Context:** *Globalization and sustainability* (how the consumption of finite resources connects communities and affects the future of the planet).
**Statement of Inquiry:** *As populations consume resources ever faster, only sustainable and circular use can prevent depletion and preserve the environment for future generations.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- **Factual:** What types of resources exist and how are they classified?
- **Conceptual:** How do consumption and population growth cause resource depletion?
- **Debatable:** Can a growing global economy ever be truly sustainable?

---

## Chapter 2.1 — Classification of Resources

### Concept: Resource
**Definition:** A resource is anything useful to humans that helps satisfy a need or want.
**Real-world example:** Crude oil in Saudi Arabia, fertile soil in Ukraine, or the skills of software engineers in Bangalore are all resources.
**Analogy:** Resources are the "ingredients" a society cooks with — some are in the cupboard already (natural), some you make (man-made), some are the cook's skill (human).
**Why it matters:** How wisely resources are classified and managed decides a country's wealth and its environmental future.

### Resource Classification Table
| Basis of classification | Types | Meaning | Example |
|--------------------------|-------|---------|---------|
| **Origin** | Natural | Provided by nature | Water, minerals, forests |
| | Human | People's skills, labour, knowledge | Doctors, engineers |
| | Man-made (capital) | Made by people from natural resources | Buildings, machines, roads |
| **Usage/technology** | Conventional | Long-used, traditional | Coal, oil, firewood |
| | Non-conventional | Newer/alternative | Solar, wind, tidal, geothermal |
| **Renewability** | Renewable | Replaced naturally in a human lifetime | Solar, wind, water, forests |
| | Non-renewable | Finite; take millions of years to form | Coal, oil, gas, minerals |

### Renewable vs Non-renewable — Advantages/Disadvantages
| | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|-----------|---------------|
| **Renewable** | Won't run out; low/zero emissions; cleaner | Can be intermittent (no sun/wind); high set-up cost; land use |
| **Non-renewable** | Reliable, high energy density; established technology | Finite; pollution & CO₂; price volatility; depletion |

### Concept: Resource Depletion
**Definition:** Using up a resource faster than it can be replaced, so its available amount shrinks.
**Real-world example:** Overfishing collapsed the Grand Banks cod stocks off Canada in the 1990s.
**Analogy:** Spending from a savings account without paying in — eventually the balance hits zero.
**Why it matters:** Depletion threatens future supply, raises prices and damages ecosystems.
**Misconception:** *"Renewable resources can never run out."* Wrong: renewables like forests, fish and groundwater *can* be depleted if used faster than they regenerate. Corrective: "renewable" means it *can* replace itself — but only if we don't overuse it.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Distinguish** between renewable and non-renewable resources.
2. (Apply) **Classify** solar power, coal, and a doctor's skills by resource type.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the claim that non-renewable resources should be abandoned immediately.

## Chapter 2.2 — Soil as a Resource

### Concept: Soil
**Definition:** Soil is the thin, living top layer of the Earth made of weathered rock, minerals, water, air and decayed organic matter (humus).
**Why soil is a resource:** It grows almost all our food, supports ecosystems, stores water and carbon, and filters water.
**Real-world example:** The fertile chernozem of Ukraine feeds much of the world's wheat supply; degraded soil in the Sahel worsens food shortages.
**Analogy:** Soil is like a slow-baked cake — it takes centuries of "ingredients" and time to form a few centimetres, but minutes to ruin.
**Soil formation process:** Rock is broken down by **weathering** (physical, chemical, biological); over hundreds–thousands of years, plants, decomposers and climate add humus and structure to create soil layers (horizons). Forming ~1 cm can take 100+ years.
**Importance for agriculture & ecosystems:** Provides nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium), anchors roots, holds water, hosts billions of organisms.
**Causes of soil erosion & degradation:** Deforestation, overgrazing, over-cultivation, wind and water erosion, chemical overuse, salinisation, urbanisation.
**Soil conservation measures:** Contour ploughing, terracing, crop rotation, cover crops, shelter belts/windbreaks, afforestation, adding organic matter, no-till farming.
**Why it matters:** Roughly a third of the world's soil is already degraded; without conservation, food security collapses.
**Misconception:** *"Soil is just dead dirt."* Wrong: healthy soil is teeming with living organisms and is essentially non-renewable on a human timescale. Corrective: soil is a living, slowly-formed resource we must protect.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **List** three causes of soil erosion.
2. (Apply) **Describe** how contour ploughing reduces erosion.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Discuss** why soil should be treated as a non-renewable resource. *(SDG 2, 15.)*

## Chapter 2.3 — Resource Consumption and Depletion

### Concept: Depletion (causes and consequences)
**Definition:** Depletion is the reduction of a resource's stock through consumption until it becomes scarce or exhausted.
**Real-world example:** Groundwater depletion in Punjab (India) and the near-drying of the Aral Sea from over-abstraction for cotton farming.
**Analogy:** Draining a bathtub faster than the tap fills it — soon it's empty.
**Causes:**
- **Overconsumption** — high per-person use, especially in wealthy nations.
- **Population growth** — more people needing food, water, energy and materials.
- Waste, inefficiency and the throwaway (linear) economy.
**Consequences:**
- **On society:** rising prices, conflict over resources ("water wars"), poverty, migration, unemployment.
- **On environment:** habitat loss, pollution, biodiversity decline, climate change, desertification.
**Why it matters:** Understanding causes lets societies act before resources run out.
**Misconception:** *"Only poor, overpopulated countries cause resource depletion."* Wrong: wealthy nations with smaller populations often have far higher per-capita consumption and ecological footprints. Corrective: depletion is driven by *how much each person consumes* as much as by *how many people* there are.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Define** resource depletion.
2. (Apply) **Explain** how population growth contributes to depletion.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** whether overconsumption or population growth is the bigger driver of depletion.

## Chapter 2.4 — Conservation of Resources & Sustainable Development

### Concept: Conservation
**Definition:** The careful management and protection of resources to prevent waste and depletion so they last for the future.
**Real-world example:** Germany's recycling system (Green Dot) recovers a large share of packaging waste.
**Analogy:** Conservation is like budgeting pocket money so it lasts the whole month instead of the first day.
**Strategies — the 3 Rs:** **Reduce** (use less), **Reuse** (use again), **Recycle** (make into new products). Also **energy efficiency** (LED lighting, insulation, efficient appliances) and **alternative/renewable energy** (solar, wind, hydro).
**Why it matters:** Conservation slows depletion, cuts pollution and saves money.

### Concept: Linear vs Circular Economy
**Definition:** A **linear economy** follows "take → make → use → dispose." A **circular economy** keeps resources in use as long as possible by reusing, repairing, remanufacturing and recycling — designing out waste.
| | Linear economy | Circular economy |
|---|---------------|------------------|
| Model | Take–make–dispose | Reduce–reuse–recycle–regenerate |
| Waste | High | Minimised (waste = new input) |
| Resource use | One-way, wasteful | Looped, efficient |
| Sustainability | Low | High |
**Benefits of circular economy:** Less waste and pollution, resource security, new "green" jobs, lower emissions, cost savings.
**Case study (circular economy):** The **Netherlands** aims to be fully circular by 2050; the **Ellen MacArthur Foundation** promotes circular design; **Patagonia's** "Worn Wear" repairs and resells clothing so garments loop back into use instead of landfill.
**Analogy:** Linear economy is a one-use paper plate; circular economy is a washable plate used again and again.
**Misconception:** *"Recycling alone makes an economy circular."* Wrong: recycling is only one loop and still uses energy; true circularity prioritises reducing and reusing first. Corrective: recycling is the last resort *after* reduce and reuse.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **State** the three Rs of conservation.
2. (Apply) **Compare** a linear and a circular economy.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** how a circular economy supports sustainable development.

### Concept: Sustainable Development
**Definition:** Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs (Brundtland, 1987).
**Real-world example:** Costa Rica generates almost all its electricity from renewables while protecting its rainforests and growing ecotourism.
**Analogy:** Living off the interest of a bank account, not the capital — so the balance keeps producing forever.
**Key milestones:**
- **Earth Summit (Rio de Janeiro, 1992)** — a landmark UN conference on environment and development.
- **Agenda 21** — its global action plan for sustainable development in the 21st century.
**Three pillars of sustainability:**
| Pillar | Focus | Example |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Social | People, equity, health, education | Fair access to clean water |
| Economic | Jobs, growth, prosperity | Green industries |
| Environmental | Protecting ecosystems & resources | Cutting emissions, conserving forests |
**Balancing human needs with environmental protection:** Sustainable development seeks a middle path where the economy grows, people's lives improve, *and* the environment is protected — none sacrificed for the others.
**Why it matters:** It is the guiding framework (and basis of the UN SDGs) for global policy today.
**Misconception:** *"Sustainable development means stopping all growth and development."* Wrong: it means developing *differently* — smarter, cleaner and fairer — not halting progress. Corrective: it balances growth with protection rather than ending growth.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Define** sustainable development.
2. (Apply) **Describe** the three pillars of sustainability with an example of each.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Discuss** the difficulty of balancing economic growth with environmental protection. *(Underpins all SDGs.)*

# UNIT 3: Policy and Governance

**Key Concept:** Global interactions
**Related Concepts:** Disparity, Power, Cooperation, Sustainability
**Global Context:** *Fairness and development* (rights and responsibilities; the relationship between resources, governance and inequality worldwide).
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Because development is uneven, cooperation between governments and international bodies is needed to manage resources fairly and reduce global disparities.*

**Inquiry Questions**

## Chapter 3.1 — Understanding Development

### Concept: Development (growth vs development)
**Definition:** **Development** is the improvement in people's quality of life — including economic wealth, social wellbeing and cultural opportunity. **Growth** is only the increase in the *size* of the economy (more output/income).
**Real-world example:** A country's GDP may grow from an oil boom (growth), but if hospitals, schools and equality don't improve, real development hasn't happened.
**Analogy:** Growth is a plant getting taller; development is the plant getting healthier — stronger roots, fruit and flowers, not just height.
**Aspects of development:**
- **Economic** — income, jobs, industry, infrastructure.
- **Social** — health, education, life expectancy, clean water.
- **Cultural** — freedom, rights, arts, identity, participation.
**Why it matters:** Governments aim for development, not just growth, to genuinely improve lives.
**Misconception:** *"A rich country is automatically a developed country."* Wrong: wealth can be unequally shared; a country can be rich yet have poor health, education or rights. Corrective: development is about wellbeing for all, not just total money.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Distinguish** between growth and development.
2. (Apply) **Describe** the economic, social and cultural aspects of development.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** whether economic growth always leads to development.

## Chapter 3.2 — Factors Affecting Development

### Concept: Physical Factors
**Definition:** Natural conditions of a place that help or hinder development.
- **Climate** — extreme climates (deserts, tropics with disease) can hinder farming and health; temperate climates often help.
- **Natural resources** — oil, minerals, fertile soil and water can boost development (e.g. oil wealth in the Gulf states).
**Analogy:** Physical factors are the "hand of cards" a country is dealt by nature.

### Concept: Human-made Factors
**Definition:** Human and economic conditions that shape development.
- **Population** — size, growth rate and structure. A very **high population** growth can strain jobs, food and services; too **low/ageing** a population can shrink the workforce. A balanced, educated population aids development.
- **Education** — a skilled workforce raises productivity and innovation.
- **Jobs** — stable employment reduces poverty and boosts income.
- **Trade** — **exports** (goods sold abroad), **imports** (goods bought in), and the **balance of trade** (exports minus imports). A trade surplus (exports > imports) brings in money; a deficit can create debt.
**How factors interact:** Factors combine and reinforce each other — e.g. a good climate → productive farming → income → schools → educated workers → industry → trade. A weakness in one (e.g. conflict or drought) can undermine the whole chain.
**Why it matters:** Development is never caused by one factor alone; understanding interaction guides better policy.
**Misconception:** *"Countries with lots of natural resources are always developed."* Wrong — the "resource curse": some resource-rich countries suffer conflict, corruption and inequality (e.g. DR Congo's minerals). Corrective: resources help only when governance, education and fair trade turn them into wellbeing.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Identify** two physical and two human factors affecting development.
2. (Apply) **Explain** how the balance of trade can affect a country's development.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Analyse** how physical and human factors interact to influence development.

## Chapter 3.3 — Measures of Development

### Concept: Development Indicators
**Definition:** Statistics used to compare the level of development between places.
### Development Indicators Table
| Indicator | Full name | What it measures | Limitation |
|-----------|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| **GDP (per capita)** | Gross Domestic Product | Total value of goods & services ÷ population | Ignores inequality, informal economy, wellbeing |
| **HDI** | Human Development Index | Combines income, life expectancy & education (0–1) | Doesn't measure freedom, environment or inequality |
| **Birth/death rate, life expectancy, literacy** | — | Social wellbeing | Single measures give a partial picture |

**Real-world example:** Norway has a very high HDI (~0.96); Niger has a low HDI (~0.4).
**Analogy:** Using one indicator is like judging a student on one test; HDI is like a report card combining several subjects.

### Concept: North–South Divide and the Brandt Line
**Definition:** The **Brandt Line** (1980) is an imaginary line dividing the wealthier "Global North" from the poorer "Global South."
**Purpose:** A simple visual to show global inequality in development.
**Limitations:** It is outdated and oversimplified — fast-growing economies like China, India and Brazil are in the "South," and there is poverty within the "North." Development is not neatly split by a line.
**Country categories (case studies):**
- **LIC (Low-Income Country):** e.g. **Malawi** — low GDP, reliant on subsistence farming.
- **HIC (High-Income Country):** e.g. **Japan / Germany** — high GDP & HDI, service/industry economy.
- **NEE (Newly Emerging Economy):** e.g. **India / Brazil** — rapidly industrialising, rising incomes, but still inequality.
**Why it matters:** Categories and indicators help target aid and policy, but must be used carefully.
**Misconception:** *"All countries in the Global South are equally poor."* Wrong: the "South" includes wealthy oil states and booming NEEs. Corrective: development is a spectrum, not two boxes.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **Define** GDP and HDI.
2. (Apply) **Explain** why HDI is often preferred to GDP as a development measure.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the usefulness of the Brandt Line today.

## Chapter 3.4 — Issues Related to Development

### Concept: Poverty
**Definition:** A state where people lack the money and resources to meet basic needs (food, shelter, health, education).
**Real-world example:** Rural poverty in Malawi, where drought and lack of schooling trap families.
**Causes:** Lack of education, conflict/war, inequality, poor health, unemployment, debt.
**Cycle of poverty:** Low income → poor education/health → low-skilled work → low income again — a self-repeating trap passed to the next generation.
**Impact:** Poor health, child labour, hunger, limited opportunity, social unrest.
**Analogy:** The cycle of poverty is like a wheel stuck in mud — without an outside push (education, aid, jobs) it keeps spinning in place.
**Misconception:** *"Poor people are poor because they don't work hard."* Wrong: poverty is largely structural — caused by lack of education, conflict and inequality beyond individual control. Corrective: breaking the cycle needs systemic change, not just effort.
**IB questions:** (Recall) **List** three causes of poverty. (Apply) **Explain** the cycle of poverty. (Analyse) **Discuss** how education can break the cycle of poverty. *(SDG 1 No Poverty.)*

### Concept: Food Security
**Definition:** When all people, at all times, have physical and economic access to enough safe, nutritious food for an active, healthy life.
**Real-world example:** Yemen faces severe food insecurity due to conflict and drought.
**Factors affecting supply:** **Climate** (drought, floods), **technology** (irrigation, machinery, GM crops), **population** (more mouths to feed), conflict, poverty, trade.
**Environmental impacts of food production:** Deforestation, water use, soil degradation, greenhouse gases, pollution from fertilisers.
**Concept/challenges:** Unequal distribution, food waste, price shocks, climate change.
**Strategies — local:** Community gardens, improved seeds, irrigation, storage, education. **Global:** Fair trade, food aid, technology transfer, reducing waste, the Green Revolution.
**Undernutrition vs overnutrition:** **Undernutrition** = too little/insufficient nutrients (hunger, stunting); **overnutrition** = too much/unbalanced food (obesity, diet-related disease). Both are forms of malnutrition.
**Analogy:** Food security is like a household pantry that is reliably stocked, affordable and healthy — not just full one week and empty the next.
**Misconception:** *"There isn't enough food in the world to feed everyone."* Wrong: the world produces enough food; the problem is unequal distribution, waste and poverty. Corrective: hunger is mainly an access and distribution problem, not total shortage.
**IB questions:** (Recall) **Define** food security. (Apply) **Explain** how climate affects food supply. (Analyse) **Evaluate** local vs global strategies to improve food security. *(SDG 2 Zero Hunger.)*

### Concept: Water Scarcity
**Definition:** When the demand for safe water exceeds the available supply.
**Types:**
- **Physical scarcity** — not enough water naturally exists (e.g. Middle East, North Africa).
- **Economic scarcity** — water exists but people lack the money/infrastructure to access it (e.g. parts of Sub-Saharan Africa).
**Real-world example:** Cape Town, South Africa nearly reached "Day Zero" (taps off) in 2018.
**Water-stressed regions:** Middle East, North Africa, parts of India, southwestern USA.
**Impacts:** Disease, crop failure, conflict, migration, lost economic productivity, hardship for women/children who fetch water.
**Conservation strategies:** Drip irrigation, rainwater harvesting, fixing leaks, desalination, wastewater recycling, dams, education.
**Case studies:** **Cape Town** (physical/management crisis); **India** (groundwater over-abstraction and rainwater harvesting schemes).
**Analogy:** Physical scarcity is an empty well; economic scarcity is a full well with no bucket or rope.
**Misconception:** *"Water scarcity only happens in deserts."* Wrong: economic scarcity can affect water-rich regions that lack pipes, treatment and money. Corrective: scarcity is about *access* as well as physical availability.
**IB questions:** (Recall) **Distinguish** physical and economic water scarcity. (Apply) **Describe** two water conservation strategies. (Analyse) **Discuss** the impacts of water scarcity on a named region. *(SDG 6 Clean Water and Sanitation.)*

### Concept: Energy Security
**Definition:** Reliable, affordable access to enough energy to meet a country's needs.
**Real-world example:** Europe's 2022 energy crisis after gas supply disruption showed the risk of depending on imported energy.
**Importance of energy conservation:** Reduces costs, cuts emissions, extends resource life, improves security.
**Strategies to reduce consumption:** Efficient appliances, insulation, public transport, LED lighting, smart grids, behaviour change.
**Role of technology:** Renewables (solar, wind), battery storage, smart meters, electric vehicles, efficient design.
**Challenges of clean-energy transition:** High upfront cost, intermittency (sun/wind not constant), storage limits, need for new infrastructure, jobs lost in fossil-fuel industries.
**Analogy:** Energy security is like a phone that's always charged and never suddenly dies mid-call.
**Misconception:** *"Switching to renewable energy is quick and easy."* Wrong: transition needs huge investment, storage solutions and grid changes over decades. Corrective: it is essential but gradual and challenging.
**IB questions:** (Recall) **Define** energy security. (Apply) **Explain** two strategies to reduce energy consumption. (Analyse) **Evaluate** the challenges of moving to clean energy. *(SDG 7 Affordable and Clean Energy.)*

## Chapter 3.5 — International Aid, Agreements and Governance

### Concept: International Aid
**Definition:** Help (money, goods, expertise) given by one country or organisation to another to support development or respond to crises.
**Real-world example:** Emergency aid after the 2010 Haiti earthquake (short-term); long-term development aid building schools and clinics in Rwanda.
**Purpose of aid:** Reduce poverty, respond to disasters, improve health/education, support development and stability.
**Types of aid:**
| Type | Timescale | Purpose | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Short-term / emergency** | Immediate | Save lives after disaster | Food, water, shelter, medics after an earthquake |
| **Long-term / development** | Years | Build lasting capacity | Schools, clean water systems, training, infrastructure |
**Role of international organizations:** The **UN**, **World Bank**, **IMF**, **WHO**, and NGOs (Oxfam, Red Cross) fund, coordinate and deliver aid and set development goals (the SDGs).
**Analogy:** Emergency aid is a plaster for a wound; development aid is physiotherapy that helps the patient walk again.
**Misconception:** *"Aid always helps the receiving country."* Wrong: poorly planned aid can create dependency, debt or benefit donors more than recipients. Corrective: aid works best when it is long-term, locally led and builds self-reliance.

### Concept: International Agreements and the Role of Governments
**Definition:** Countries cooperate through treaties and agreements to manage shared resources and problems that cross borders.
**Real-world example:** The **Paris Agreement (2015)** on climate change; the **Kyoto Protocol**; **Agenda 21** and the **UN Sustainable Development Goals (2015)**.
**Role of governments:** Make laws, set targets, fund conservation, sign international agreements, regulate industry, provide services, and balance economic growth with sustainability.
**Why it matters:** Resource problems (climate, oceans, rivers) don't stop at borders, so governance must be cooperative and global.
**Misconception:** *"One country acting alone can solve global resource problems."* Wrong: issues like climate change need cooperation because emissions and pollution cross borders. Corrective: global problems require global governance and shared responsibility.
**IB command-term questions:**
1. (Recall) **State** the difference between emergency and development aid.
2. (Apply) **Explain** the role of one international organisation in resource management.
3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the claim that international aid does more harm than good.

# Subject-Wide Reference

## Common Misconceptions Master List
1. **Biome = ecosystem.** No — biome is global-scale; ecosystem is local.
2. **Rainforest soil is rich.** No — it is nutrient-poor; richness is in the living plants.
3. **Poles are the rainiest places because they are cold.** No — cold, sinking air is dry; poles are "cold deserts."
4. **Evergreen trees never lose leaves.** No — they shed needles gradually all year.
5. **Deserts are lifeless seas of sand.** No — mostly rocky and full of adapted life; defined by low rainfall.
6. **Savanna fires are always disasters.** No — periodic fire is natural and needed.
7. **Grasslands are empty, unused land.** No — the world's richest farmland.
8. **Nothing lives in the tundra.** No — caribou, foxes, birds and hardy plants thrive.
9. **Renewable resources can never run out.** No — forests, fish and groundwater can be depleted.
10. **Soil is just dead dirt.** No — living and effectively non-renewable on human timescales.
11. **Only poor/overpopulated countries cause depletion.** No — high per-capita consumption matters as much.
12. **Recycling alone makes an economy circular.** No — reduce and reuse come first.
13. **Sustainable development means stopping growth.** No — it means growing cleaner and fairer.
14. **A rich country is automatically developed.** No — wealth can be unequally shared.
15. **Resource-rich countries are always developed.** No — the "resource curse."
16. **All Global South countries are equally poor.** No — development is a spectrum.
17. **Poverty is caused by laziness.** No — it is largely structural.
18. **There isn't enough food for everyone.** No — it's a distribution and waste problem.
19. **Water scarcity only happens in deserts.** No — economic scarcity affects water-rich regions.
20. **Renewable transition is quick and easy.** No — costly, gradual and challenging.
21. **Aid always helps.** No — poorly planned aid can create dependency.
