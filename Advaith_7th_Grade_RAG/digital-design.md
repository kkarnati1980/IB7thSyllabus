# Grade 7 Digital Design (MYP Design) — IISH MYP Knowledge Base (2026–2027)

## Subject Overview

Digital Design at IISH sits in the **MYP Design** subject group. Grade 7 students learn to solve real-life problems by moving through the **MYP Design Cycle**, a structured, iterative process for turning a need into a working product. The course is delivered through two application units across the year:

- **Unit 1 — Toy and Game Design** (July–December)
- **Unit 2 — Robotics (Maker Bot)** (January–May)

For each unit students produce an **e-Portfolio**: a digital document (Google Doc/Slides, PDF, or website) that captures their thinking and evidence at every stage of the Design Cycle for a real-life scenario. The e-Portfolio *is* the assessment — it shows the journey, not just the finished product.

### The Four MYP Design Criteria

MYP Design is assessed against four criteria, each scored out of 8. In Grade 7 these are taught in order but revisited iteratively.

| Criterion | Name | Core question | Grade 7 focus |
|-----------|------|---------------|---------------|
| **A** | Inquiring and Analysing | *What is the problem and what already exists?* | Identify a problem, research it, analyse existing products |
| **B** | Developing Ideas | *What could the solution look like?* | Success criteria, design ideas/diagrams, flowchart |
| **C** | Creating the Solution | *Can I build it?* | Logical plan, make the product, record changes |
| **D** | Evaluating | *Does it work and what is its impact?* | Test cases, testing against success criteria, improvements, client impact |

### Design-Related Concepts

Related concepts are the "lenses" that sharpen inquiry in Design. Students should be able to name and use these:

- **Function** — what the product does and how well.
- **Form** — the shape, look and structure of the product.
- **Innovation** — new or improved ideas that add value.
- **Evaluation** — judging quality against criteria.
- **Ergonomics** — designing to fit the human user comfortably and safely.
- **Adaptation** — adjusting a design for a new context or user.
- **Collaboration** — working with clients, users and teammates.
- **Markets and trends** — what users want and what is popular now.
- **Resources** — the materials, tools, time and skills available.
- **Sustainability** — designing with care for people and the planet.
- **Perspective** — considering different viewpoints and needs.

### The Design Cycle (Iterative, Not Linear)

The Design Cycle is a **loop**, not a straight line. Students inquire (A), develop ideas (B), create (C) and evaluate (D) — and the evaluation feeds back into new inquiry. Testing in D often sends a designer *back* to B or C to improve the product. Good designers circle around the cycle several times.

| Stage | Criterion | Key output in the e-Portfolio |
|-------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Inquiring & Analysing | A | Problem statement, research, product analysis, SWOT |
| Developing Ideas | B | Success criteria, design diagrams, chosen design, flowchart |
| Creating the Solution | C | Logical plan, the built product, list of changes |
| Evaluating | D | Test cases, test results, improvements, client impact |

### The e-Portfolio Requirement

- One e-Portfolio per unit, organised by criterion (A → B → C → D).
- Every claim needs **evidence**: screenshots, photos, survey data, diagrams, links.
- Written in the student's own words, with a **bibliography** for sources.
- Shows iteration — including mistakes, changes and improvements. Struggles that are documented and fixed score higher than a polished product with no visible process.

# CRITERION A — Inquiring and Analysing

Criterion A is about **understanding the problem before building anything**. Students identify a real problem, prove it is worth solving, learn about the user, and study what already exists.

## Problem Identification

**Definition:** Clearly stating a real-life problem or need that a design can solve, and justifying *why* a solution is required.

**How to do it (checklist):**
1. Describe the situation or scenario.
2. Name who is affected (the client/user).
3. State the problem in one clear sentence.
4. Justify *why* it matters — what happens if it is not solved?
5. Explain why a design solution (a product) is the right response.

**Worked mini-example (Toy/Game):** *"Younger students at IISH (ages 5–7) in after-school care often wait with nothing to do. A problem is that there is no simple, safe indoor game they can play in small groups without adult setup. A solution is needed because boredom leads to restlessness and conflict; a well-designed board or card game would keep them engaged and build turn-taking skills."*

**Why it matters:** A weak problem statement produces a solution nobody needs. Everything downstream (research, criteria, testing) points back to the problem.

**Common misconception:** *"The problem is just the product I want to make."* Correction: the problem is the **need**, stated independently of any solution. "We need a robot" is not a problem; "elderly residents forget to water their plants" is.

**Command-term questions:**
- **State** the problem your design will solve. (1 sentence)
- **Explain** why a solution to this problem is needed. (justify with a consequence)

## Target Audience

**Definition:** The specific group of people the product is designed for.

**How to do it:**
1. Name the group (age, role, context).
2. Describe their needs, abilities and limits.
3. Keep it **specific** — "children" is too broad; "IISH students aged 5–7 in after-school care" is usable.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics):** Target audience = *"Home gardeners aged 30–55 who travel for work and own 4–6 potted plants indoors."* This tells us the Maker Bot must be simple, safe near soil/water, and usable without coding knowledge.

**Why it matters:** The audience determines the success criteria and the testing. You cannot judge "easy to use" without knowing *for whom*.

**Common misconception:** *"Everyone is my audience."* Correction: designing for everyone means designing for no one. Narrow the audience so decisions become clear.

**Command-term question:** **Identify** your target audience and **describe** two of their needs.

## Primary vs Secondary Research

**Definition:**
- **Primary research** — data you collect yourself, first-hand (surveys, interviews, observations, your own testing).
- **Secondary research** — information gathered by others that you find (websites, books, articles, videos, existing product reviews).

**How to do it (checklist):**
1. Plan *what* you need to know.
2. Choose primary methods to hear directly from users.
3. Choose secondary methods to understand the wider context and existing solutions.
4. Record the **source** of every piece of information.

| Type | Examples | Strength | Limit |
|------|----------|----------|-------|
| Primary | Survey of classmates, interview with a teacher, observation | Directly about *your* users | Takes time; small sample |
| Secondary | Website on board-game design, robot sensor datasheet | Fast, broad, expert | May be outdated or not about your users |

**Worked mini-example (Toy/Game):** Primary = a survey asking 20 younger students which game types they enjoy. Secondary = an article on age-appropriate game mechanics for 5–7 year-olds.

**Why it matters:** Good design balances *what real users say* (primary) with *what is already known* (secondary).

**Common misconception:** *"Anything on the internet is primary research because I found it."* Correction: if someone else produced it, it is **secondary**, no matter how you accessed it.

**Command-term question:** **Distinguish** between primary and secondary research using an example from your project.

## Surveys, Data Collection and Data Analysis

**Definition:** Gathering numerical or descriptive data from users (often via a survey/form) and making sense of it.

**How to do it:**
1. Write clear, unbiased questions (avoid leading questions).
2. Use a mix of closed (multiple-choice, rating) and open questions.
3. Collect responses (Google Forms is ideal — it charts results automatically).
4. **Analyse:** summarise with charts, find the most common answers, and state what the data *means* for your design.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics):** Survey question: *"How confident are you using technology at home? (1–5)"* Result: 70% answered 1–2. **Analysis:** most users are low-confidence, so the Maker Bot must have very simple controls and clear instructions.

**Why it matters:** Data turns opinions into evidence. A design decision backed by data is defensible.

**Common misconception:** *"Showing the chart is the analysis."* Correction: a chart is data *display*. **Analysis** explains what the numbers tell you and how they change your design.

**Command-term question:** **Analyse** your survey results and **explain** one design decision they support.

## Bibliography and Citation

**Definition:** A list of every source used, formatted so others can find it. Citing gives credit and avoids plagiarism.

**How to do it (checklist):**
1. Record author, title, website/publisher, date, and URL as you research.
2. List sources alphabetically.
3. Use a consistent style (MLA is common in MYP).
4. Cite images and videos too, not just text.

**Worked mini-example:** *Smith, J. "Designing Games for Young Children." GameDesignHub, 2024, www.gamedesignhub.com/kids.* 

**Why it matters:** Academic honesty is an IB core value. Uncited work is plagiarism even if unintentional.

**Common misconception:** *"I put it in my own words, so I don't need to cite it."* Correction: **ideas and facts** need citing even when reworded — only the wording is yours, not the information.

**Command-term question:** **List** three sources you used in correct bibliographic format.

## Product Analysis

**Definition:** Studying existing products similar to your intended solution to learn what works and what does not.

**How to do it:**
1. Choose 2–3 existing products related to your problem.
2. Examine function, form, materials, cost, ergonomics, audience.
3. Note strengths and weaknesses of each.
4. Draw conclusions that inform your own design.

**Worked mini-example (Toy/Game):** Analysing *Snakes and Ladders* (simple, luck-based, no skill) vs *Uno* (needs colour/number recognition) reveals that a game for 5–7 year-olds should use bright colours, simple rules and short play time.

**Why it matters:** You rarely design from zero. Existing products are free lessons in what users already accept.

**Common misconception:** *"Product analysis means describing the product."* Correction: description is not analysis — you must **judge** what is good/bad and say why it matters for *your* design.

**Command-term question:** **Analyse** one existing product and **explain** what you will borrow or avoid.

## SWOT Analysis

**Definition:** A structured tool for analysing a product using four lenses: **S**trengths, **W**eaknesses, **O**pportunities, **T**hreats. Strengths/Weaknesses are *internal* (about the product itself); Opportunities/Threats are *external* (about the market/context).

**SWOT template:**

| | Helpful | Harmful |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Internal (the product)** | **Strengths** — what it does well | **Weaknesses** — what it lacks |
| **External (the world)** | **Opportunities** — trends/gaps to exploit | **Threats** — competitors, risks |

**How to do it:**
1. Pick a product (usually an existing one).
2. Fill each quadrant with specific points.
3. Use SWOT of several products to **compare** and find a gap for your design.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics — analysing an existing plant-watering gadget):**
- **S:** Waters automatically on a timer.
- **W:** No sensor — waters even when soil is wet.
- **O:** Users increasingly want "smart" eco-friendly gadgets.
- **T:** Cheaper competitor products already exist.
Conclusion: our Maker Bot should add a **moisture sensor** (fixing the weakness and seizing the opportunity).

**Why it matters:** SWOT forces you to look beyond the product itself to the market and risks — the same thinking real companies use.

**Common misconception:** *"SWOT is just a list of features."* Correction: SWOT separates *internal vs external* and *helpful vs harmful*. Opportunities and Threats are about the **outside world**, not the product's parts.

**Command-term question:** **Construct** a SWOT analysis of an existing product related to your problem.

## Comparing Products with SWOT & Summarising Research

**Definition:** Using SWOT across multiple products to compare them, then summarising all research to define the solution.

**How to do it:**
1. SWOT two or three competitor products.
2. Compare quadrants side by side — where do they all fall short?
3. Write a research summary: *"Based on my primary and secondary research and product analysis, the solution should…"*
4. This summary becomes the bridge into Criterion B.

**Worked mini-example:** After comparing three toys, all lack replay value. Summary: *"My game must remain fun over many plays, so it will include variable outcomes."*

**Why it matters:** The summary links evidence to the design brief, proving your solution is research-driven, not guesswork.

**Common misconception:** *"The summary is a recap of everything I did."* Correction: it is a **focused conclusion** stating what the solution needs, drawn from the evidence.

# CRITERION B — Developing Ideas

Criterion B turns understanding into **plans and designs**. Students set success criteria, generate and compare design ideas, and map the product's logic with a flowchart.

## Success Criteria (Design Specification)

**Definition:** A list of clear, measurable statements that a successful solution **must** meet. Together they form the **design specification**. They are the "test list" you will use in Criterion D.

**How to do it (checklist):**
1. Write 5–8 criteria drawn from your research.
2. Make each **specific and testable** (measurable or clearly checkable).
3. Cover function, audience needs, form, safety, resources.
4. Phrase as "The solution must…".

**Worked mini-example (Toy/Game):**
- The game must be playable by 2–4 children aged 5–7.
- The game must last no longer than 15 minutes.
- The rules must fit on one card and use pictures.
- The game must use only safe, non-toxic, non-choking materials.

**Why it matters:** Success criteria connect A → B → D. You design *to* them (B) and test *against* them (D).

**Common misconception:** *"Success criteria = a list of features."* Correction: criteria are **measurable standards** ("must last under 15 minutes"), not just parts ("has cards"). A feature answers *what*; a criterion answers *how well / how much*, so it can be tested pass/fail.

**Command-term question:** **List** five success criteria and **explain** how each is measurable.

## Design Diagrams and Annotation

**Definition:** Visual drawings of possible solutions, **annotated** (labelled) to explain each part, made in tools such as **draw.io** or **Canva**.

**How to do it:**
1. Sketch **at least two or three** different design ideas.
2. Label every important part (annotation) — material, size, function, colour.
3. Keep ideas genuinely different so there is a real choice.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics):** Two annotated draw.io diagrams of the Maker Bot — Design 1 with wheels + moisture sensor; Design 2 with a fixed base + timer. Annotations note the sensor position, battery, and controller.

**Why it matters:** Diagrams make ideas visible and communicable before you commit resources to building.

**Common misconception:** *"One design idea is enough."* Correction: MYP Design requires **multiple** ideas so you can *compare and justify* a choice.

**Command-term question:** **Present** two annotated design ideas for your solution.

## Analysing and Selecting the Best Design

**Definition:** Comparing your design ideas against the success criteria to justify which one to build.

**How to do it:**
1. Make a table: designs as columns, success criteria as rows.
2. Mark how well each design meets each criterion.
3. Choose the best-fitting design and **explain why** with reference to the criteria.

**Worked mini-example:** Design 1 (with sensor) meets the "saves water / eco-friendly" criterion; Design 2 does not. Selected Design 1 because it satisfies more success criteria, especially sustainability.

**Why it matters:** Justified selection shows the choice is evidence-based, not personal preference.

**Common misconception:** *"I pick the one I like best."* Correction: selection must be **justified against the success criteria**, not taste.

## Flowcharts

**Definition:** A diagram that shows the **step-by-step logic** of how the product (or a user's interaction, or a program) works, using standard symbols.

**Flowchart symbols:**

| Symbol shape | Name | Meaning |
|--------------|------|---------|
| Rounded rectangle / oval | Terminator | Start or End |
| Parallelogram | Input/Output | Get input or show output |
| Rectangle | Process | An action or step |
| Diamond | Decision | A yes/no question that branches |
| Arrow | Flow line | Direction / order of steps |

**How to do it:**
1. Start with a **Start** terminator.
2. Add processes and inputs/outputs in order.
3. Use a diamond wherever the product must *decide* (branch).
4. End with a **Stop** terminator. Follow arrows top-to-bottom.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics — watering logic):**
Start → Read soil moisture (input) → *Is soil dry?* (decision) → if **Yes**: Turn on pump (process) → Wait 5 s → Stop pump; if **No**: do nothing → End.

**Why it matters:** Flowcharts force logical thinking and reveal gaps before building. They are the bridge between an idea and an algorithm.

**Common misconception:** *"A flowchart is just a list of steps in boxes."* Correction: a true flowchart shows **decisions and branches** (diamonds), not only a straight sequence.

**Command-term question:** **Design** a flowchart showing how your solution works, using correct symbols.

# CRITERION C — Creating the Solution

Criterion C is about **making the product** by following a plan and demonstrating technical skill.

## Logical Plan

**Definition:** A step-by-step plan for building the solution, in order, listing tasks, tools/resources and time.

**How to do it (checklist):**
1. Break the build into ordered steps.
2. For each step list the resources/tools needed.
3. Estimate time for each step.
4. Present as a table or numbered list (a Gantt-style plan is ideal).

**Worked mini-example (Toy/Game):**
1. Design board layout in Canva (1 lesson, laptop).
2. Print and laminate (1 lesson, printer).
3. Make game cards (1 lesson, card + markers).
4. Playtest and adjust (1 lesson).

**Why it matters:** A plan keeps the build on track and is evidence of organisation — assessed under Criterion C.

**Common misconception:** *"I can just start building and figure it out."* Correction: an unplanned build wastes resources; the **plan itself is assessed**.

## Creating the Solution & Recording Changes

**Definition:** Building the product by following the plan, showing technical skills, and **documenting every change** made along the way.

**How to do it:**
1. Follow the logical plan; photograph/screenshot each stage.
2. Show the skills you used (making, coding, wiring, designing).
3. When something doesn't work, record **what you changed and why**.
4. Present the **final solution** with clear evidence.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics):** *"Planned to use two wheels but the bot tipped over, so I changed to a wider four-wheel base."* — recorded with before/after photos.

**Why it matters:** The e-Portfolio must show the *journey*. Documented changes show iteration and problem-solving — highly valued in MYP.

**Common misconception:** *"Only the finished product counts."* Correction: **process evidence and recorded changes** are assessed, often more than polish.

**Command-term question:** **Create** your solution and **outline** two changes you made during building and why.

# CRITERION D — Evaluating

Criterion D judges **whether the solution works** and what **impact** it has on the client.

## Testing and Test Cases

**Definition:** A **test case** is a planned check that verifies whether the product does what it should. Testing means running these checks and recording results.

**Test-case template:**

| Test # | What is tested (linked to criterion) | Steps to test | Expected result | Actual result | Pass/Fail |
|--------|--------------------------------------|---------------|-----------------|----------------|-----------|
| 1 | Game lasts under 15 min | Time a full 4-player game | Under 15 min | 12 min | Pass |
| 2 | Rules understood by age 5–7 | Ask a 6-year-old to explain rules | Explains correctly | Needed help | Fail |

**How to do it:**
1. Write one test per success criterion.
2. State expected result *before* testing.
3. Run the test with real users where possible.
4. Record actual results honestly, including failures.

**Worked mini-example (Robotics):** Test: place bot in dry soil → expect pump to run 5 s → actual: pump ran 8 s → **Fail** → improvement: adjust timer.

**Why it matters:** Testing against criteria is objective proof the design works (or doesn't). It closes the loop back to Criterion A/B.

**Common misconception:** *"Testing means checking it turns on / looks nice."* Correction: testing checks **each success criterion** with a defined expected result, not a general impression.

**Command-term question:** **Design** three test cases that check your solution against its success criteria.

## Evaluation Against Success Criteria

**Definition:** Judging the finished product criterion by criterion, using test evidence.

**How to do it:**
1. Go through each success criterion.
2. State whether it was met, partly met, or not met — with evidence.
3. Be honest; unmet criteria are learning, not failure.

**Why it matters:** This is the heart of Criterion D — evaluation must be **evidence-based**, tied to the criteria set in B.

**Common misconception:** *"My product is good because I like it."* Correction: evaluation uses **test data against the criteria**, not opinion.

## Improvements and Client Impact

**Definition:** Explaining what could be improved and how, and describing the **impact** of the solution on the client/user.

**How to do it:**
1. For each weakness found in testing, suggest a specific improvement.
2. Describe how the product affects the client — benefits, and any drawbacks.
3. Consider wider impact: sustainability, safety, cost.

**Worked mini-example (Toy/Game):** *Improvement:* add picture symbols to rules so 5-year-olds don't need help. *Client impact:* after-school staff report fewer conflicts because children can start the game independently.

**Why it matters:** Design exists to serve people. Explaining impact shows you understand *why* the product matters, closing the Design Cycle.

**Common misconception:** *"Impact means whether I finished it."* Correction: impact is about the **effect on the client and world**, not your completion.

**Command-term question:** **Evaluate** the impact of your solution on the client and **suggest** two improvements.

# UNIT 2 — Robotics (Maker Bot) (Jan–May)

**Key Concept:** Systems.
**Related Concepts:** Function, Innovation, Adaptation.
**Global Context:** Scientific and technical innovation.
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Robotic systems combine inputs, processing and outputs to perform functions that adapt to human needs.*

**Inquiry questions**
- *Factual:* What are the parts of a robot and what does each do?
- *Conceptual:* How does the input–process–output model let a robot respond to its environment?
- *Debatable:* Should robots replace humans in everyday household tasks?

## Robotics Basics for Grade 7

### What is a Robot?
A robot is a machine that can **sense** its environment, **process** information, and **act** on the world, often to do a task automatically. Unlike a simple machine, a robot can respond to what is happening around it.

### Input – Process – Output (IPO)
Every robot (and computer system) follows this model:
- **Input** — information comes in from **sensors** (e.g., moisture, light, distance, button press).
- **Process** — the **controller** (the "brain," e.g., a micro:bit or Arduino) decides what to do using an algorithm.
- **Output** — **actuators** act on the world (e.g., a motor spins, a pump runs, an LED lights, a buzzer sounds).

**Example:** Moisture sensor (input) → controller checks "is it dry?" (process) → pump turns on (output).

### Sensors, Actuators, Controllers

| Part | Role | Examples |
|------|------|----------|
| **Sensor** | Detects the environment (input) | Moisture, light, ultrasonic distance, temperature, button |
| **Controller** | Processes input, runs the program (brain) | micro:bit, Arduino, Maker board |
| **Actuator** | Produces movement/action (output) | DC motor, servo, pump, LED, buzzer |

### Simple Algorithm & Flowchart Thinking
An **algorithm** is a set of ordered steps to solve a problem. Robots run algorithms. Students plan algorithms as **flowcharts** (see Criterion B) before coding, using decisions (diamonds) for "if this, then that" logic.

**Example algorithm (Maker Bot obstacle avoider):**
1. Read distance sensor.
2. Is an object closer than 10 cm? 
3. If **Yes** → stop and turn. If **No** → keep moving forward.
4. Repeat.

### Iteration and Testing in Robotics
Robots almost never work perfectly first time. Students **test, observe, adjust, and re-test** — this is iteration, the same loop as the Design Cycle. Record each change (Criterion C) and test result (Criterion D).

**Application context:** Students build a "Maker Bot" that solves a real problem (e.g., automatic plant waterer, obstacle-avoiding rover, line follower). They complete the full e-Portfolio, with special attention to flowcharts (logic), the logical plan (build order), and test cases (does the bot behave correctly?).

**Age-appropriate note:** Grade 7 robotics stays at the level of connecting sensors/actuators to a beginner controller and using simple block or lightly-typed code — the emphasis is on **logic, IPO, and testing**, not advanced programming.

# Common Misconceptions Master List

1. **"The Design Cycle is linear."** — It is **iterative**; evaluation loops back to inquiry and development.
2. **"The problem is the product I want to make."** — The problem is the **user need**, stated without a solution.
3. **"Everyone is my target audience."** — Audiences must be **specific**; designing for all serves none.
4. **"Anything I find online is primary research."** — If someone else made it, it is **secondary**.
5. **"Showing a chart is analysis."** — Analysis **explains what the data means** for the design.
6. **"I reworded it, so I don't need to cite it."** — **Ideas and facts** need citing, not just exact words.
7. **"Product analysis = describing the product."** — You must **judge** strengths/weaknesses and their relevance.
8. **"SWOT is just a list."** — SWOT separates **internal vs external** and **helpful vs harmful**.
9. **"Success criteria = a list of features."** — Criteria are **measurable, testable standards**.
10. **"One design idea is enough."** — Multiple ideas are needed to **compare and justify** a choice.
11. **"A flowchart is a list of boxes."** — It must show **decisions and branches** (diamonds).
12. **"I pick the design I like best."** — Selection must be **justified against success criteria**.
13. **"Only the finished product counts."** — **Process and recorded changes** are assessed.
14. **"Testing means it turns on / looks nice."** — Test **each success criterion** with an expected result.
15. **"My product is good because I like it."** — Evaluation is **evidence-based** against criteria.

# Command-Term & Criteria Question Bank

1. **(A)** **State** the problem your design solves and **explain** why a solution is required.
2. **(A)** **Distinguish** between primary and secondary research, giving an example of each from your project.
3. **(A)** **Construct** a SWOT analysis of an existing product and **explain** what your design will do differently.
4. **(B)** **List** five measurable success criteria for your solution and **justify** each with research.
5. **(B)** **Design** a flowchart, using correct symbols, that shows how your solution works.
6. **(B)** **Analyse** two design ideas against your success criteria and **justify** which you selected.
7. **(C)** **Outline** your logical plan and **describe** two changes you made while building, and why.
8. **(D)** **Design** three test cases and **evaluate** your solution's impact on the client.

# Cross-Subject Connections

- **Mathematics** — data handling for surveys (averages, percentages, charts); logical reasoning behind flowcharts, algorithms and decision branches; measurement for design specifications.
- **Physics / Sciences** — robotics forces and motion (wheels, gears, balance, friction); electricity and circuits for sensors and actuators; how energy is used and conserved (sustainability).
- **Language & Literature / Language Acquisition** — clear writing for the e-Portfolio; structuring an argument to justify decisions; correct citation and bibliography; writing precise instructions and rules.
- **Visual Art** — aesthetics, colour theory and composition for game boards, packaging and product form; using design tools (Canva) to make products appealing and ergonomic for the audience.
