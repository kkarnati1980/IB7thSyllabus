# IB MYP Framework Reference — IISH Grade 7 (2026–2027)

> **Purpose of this file.** This is the shared IB scaffolding that every subject file in this knowledge base draws on. When the teaching assistant needs to attach a Key Concept, Related Concept, Global Context, Statement of Inquiry, ATL skill, Learner Profile attribute, or IB command term to a lesson, the authoritative definitions live here. Subject files reference these terms; this file explains them.

---

## 1. What the MYP is (in one paragraph)

The Middle Years Programme (MYP, ages 11–16 / Grades 6–10) is a concept-driven, inquiry-based curriculum framework from the International Baccalaureate. It does **not** prescribe content the way a national board does; instead it organises learning around **big transferable concepts**, frames each unit with a **Statement of Inquiry** and **inquiry questions**, situates learning in real-world **Global Contexts**, and assesses students against published **criteria (A–D)** rather than only right/wrong answers. Grade 7 sits in **MYP Year 2**.

---

## 2. The 16 MYP Key Concepts (broad, cross-disciplinary "big ideas")

A Key Concept is a large, powerful idea that reaches across subjects and years. Every MYP unit is built on **one or more** Key Concepts.

| Key Concept | Plain-language meaning | Typical subjects |
|---|---|---|
| **Aesthetics** | Beauty, taste, and how we perceive art and design | Arts, Language & Literature |
| **Change** | How things become different over time | Sciences, History, Geography |
| **Communication** | Exchange/transfer of information, meaning, messages | Languages, Arts |
| **Communities** | Groups connected by shared features or purpose | Individuals & Societies |
| **Connections** | Links and relationships between things | All subjects |
| **Creativity** | Generating new ideas and considering things in new ways | Arts, Design |
| **Culture** | Learned, shared beliefs, values, and practices of a group | Languages, History |
| **Development** | Growth, progress, and evolution over time | Geography, PHE |
| **Form** | The features/structure of something — what it is | Sciences, Arts, Maths |
| **Global interactions** | How humans, environments, and systems connect worldwide | Geography, History |
| **Identity** | What makes a person or thing itself | Language & Lit, PHE |
| **Logic** | Reasoning, proof, valid steps | Mathematics, Sciences |
| **Perspective** | Point of view; the position from which something is seen | History, Arts, Language |
| **Relationships** | How things affect/connect to one another | Sciences, Maths |
| **Systems** | Sets of interacting/interdependent parts forming a whole | Sciences, Geography |
| **Time, place and space** | The where and when; absolute and relative location | History, Geography, Maths |

**How to use:** Pick the *one* Key Concept that a unit is truly about. Don't stack all sixteen. In this KB each unit names its Key Concept explicitly.

## 3. Related Concepts (subject-specific lenses)

Related Concepts are narrower ideas that live inside a subject and sharpen a Key Concept. Examples used across this KB:

- **Language & Literature:** audience imperatives, character, context, genres, point of view, purpose, self-expression, setting, structure, style, theme, voice.
- **Mathematics:** change, equivalence, generalization, justification, measurement, model, pattern, quantity, representation, simplification, space, systems.
- **Sciences (Biology/Chemistry/Physics):** balance, consequences, energy, environment, evidence, form, function, interaction, models, movement, patterns, transfer, transformation.
- **Individuals & Societies (Geography/History):** causality, culture, disparity, equity, globalization, identity, innovation & revolution, perspective, power, processes, resources, sustainability.
- **Arts (Visual Art/Music/Theatre):** audience, boundaries, composition, expression, genre, innovation, interpretation, narrative, play, presentation, representation, role, structure, style, visual culture.
- **Design (Digital Design):** adaptation, collaboration, ergonomics, evaluation, form, function, innovation, invention, markets & trends, perspective, resources, sustainability.
- **PHE (PAHE):** adaptation, balance, choice, energy, environment, function, interaction, movement, perspective, refinement, space, systems.

## 4. The 6 Global Contexts (why the learning matters in the world)

A Global Context answers "why are we learning this?" by connecting the unit to real human concerns.

1. **Identities and relationships** — who am I? who are we? health, relationships, what it means to be human.
2. **Orientation in space and time** — journeys, turning points, discoveries, histories, migration.
3. **Personal and cultural expression** — how we express ideas, feelings, culture, creativity, beauty.
4. **Scientific and technical innovation** — how science/technology affect us and the world; the natural world.
5. **Globalization and sustainability** — interconnectedness; how human decisions affect the planet and each other.
6. **Fairness and development** — rights, justice, equality, access to opportunity, and the consequences of decisions.

## 5. Statement of Inquiry (SOI)

A **Statement of Inquiry** is one sentence that fuses **Key Concept + Related Concept(s) + Global Context** into a transferable "so what" the whole unit explores. It reads as a truth claim students test, not a topic.

- **Formula:** *[Key Concept] + [Related Concept] examined through [Global Context] = a statement about how the world works.*
- **Example (Biology, Digestion):** "Systems of interacting parts transform matter and energy to sustain life" (Key: Systems; Related: transformation, function; Context: Scientific & technical innovation).
- Each unit in this KB provides a ready-to-use SOI plus 3 inquiry questions (factual, conceptual, debatable).

**The three kinds of inquiry question:**
- **Factual** — answerable with knowledge/recall ("What are the four biomolecules?").
- **Conceptual** — explores transferable understanding ("How does structure determine function?").
- **Debatable** — invites argument, multiple defensible positions ("Should water be fluoridated?").

## 6. Approaches to Learning (ATL) — 5 skill categories, 10 clusters

ATL skills are *how* students learn — the transferable skills that make them independent.

1. **Communication** — exchanging information (reading, writing, listening, presenting).
2. **Social** — collaboration, managing conflict, sharing responsibility.
3. **Self-management** — organization (deadlines, materials) and affective skills (focus, resilience, mindfulness).
4. **Research** — information literacy (finding, evaluating, citing sources) and media literacy.
5. **Thinking** — critical thinking, creative thinking, and transfer (applying skills in new contexts).

**Prompts that build ATL (use these when teaching):**
- Research: "Where could you find reliable evidence? How would you check the source?"
- Thinking (critical): "What is the assumption here? What is the counter-example?"
- Thinking (creative): "What if the opposite were true? Can you combine two ideas?"
- Self-management: "How will you break this into steps and check your progress?"
- Communication: "How would you explain this to someone three years younger?"

## 7. The 10 IB Learner Profile Attributes (the kind of person we grow)

Inquirers · Knowledgeable · Thinkers · Communicators · Principled · Open-minded · Caring · Risk-takers (Courageous) · Balanced · Reflective.

Name an attribute only when a task genuinely develops it (e.g., designing a fair experiment → *Principled* and *Inquirers*; presenting a viewpoint you disagree with → *Open-minded*).

## 8. IB Command Terms (the verbs that tell students what thinking is required)

Grouped by cognitive demand. Command terms drive MYP question design — teach students to read the verb first.

**Lower demand (recall/understand):**
- **State / Identify / List / Label / Define** — give a specific name, value, or brief answer; no explanation.
- **Outline** — give a brief account or summary.
- **Describe** — give a detailed account without reasons.

**Middle demand (apply/analyse):**
- **Explain** — give a detailed account **including reasons or causes**.
- **Apply** — use knowledge in a new situation.
- **Analyse** — break down to identify parts and relationships; interpret.
- **Distinguish / Compare / Contrast** — show differences and/or similarities.
- **Calculate / Solve / Determine** — obtain a numerical/algebraic answer showing working.

**Higher demand (evaluate/create):**
- **Evaluate** — weigh strengths and limitations to reach a judgement.
- **Justify** — give valid reasons/evidence to support a conclusion.
- **Discuss** — balanced review including a range of arguments/factors.
- **To what extent** — consider merits/arguments and reach a supported judgement.
- **Design / Create / Construct** — produce a plan, model, or solution.
- **Predict** — give an expected result of an action.
- **Reflect** — think about and give a considered response to experience/learning.

## 9. The MYP Assessment Criteria by subject group (A–D, each /8)

Each subject is marked against **four criteria**, each on a 0–8 scale, using published rubrics ("achievement level descriptors"). Knowing the criterion tells students what *kind* of work earns marks.

| Subject group | Criterion A | Criterion B | Criterion C | Criterion D |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Language & Literature** | Analysing | Organizing | Producing text | Using language |
| **Language Acquisition** (Spanish/French/Hindi/Telugu) | Listening (Comprehending spoken & visual text) | Reading (Comprehending written & visual text) | Speaking (Communicating) | Writing (Using language) |
| **Sciences** (Bio/Chem/Phys) | Knowing & understanding | Inquiring & designing | Processing & evaluating | Reflecting on the impacts of science |
| **Mathematics** | Knowing & understanding | Investigating patterns | Communicating | Applying maths in real-life contexts |
| **Individuals & Societies** (Geo/Hist) | Knowing & understanding | Investigating | Communicating | Thinking critically |
| **Arts** (Visual Art/Music/Theatre) | Investigating (Knowing & understanding) | Developing | Creating/Performing | Evaluating |
| **Design** (Digital Design) | Inquiring & analysing | Developing ideas | Creating the solution | Evaluating |
| **Physical & Health Ed (PAHE)** | Knowing & understanding | Planning for performance | Applying & performing | Reflecting & improving performance |

## 10. The teaching scaffold used in every concept entry

Each concept in the subject files follows this 5-layer progressive-explanation model plus supports. When the teaching assistant explains a concept, it can walk up this ladder as far as the learner needs:

- **Level 1 — Age 10:** simplest true version, everyday words.
- **Level 2 — Classroom:** standard clear explanation.
- **Level 3 — MYP:** proper terminology + the concept lens.
- **Level 4 — Cross-subject connection:** where else this idea appears.
- **Level 5 — Advanced / exceptions:** deeper reasoning, edge cases, limits.

**Every concept entry also supplies:** a simple definition · a real-world example · an analogy · why it matters · at least one common misconception (with why it's wrong + a corrective example) · IB command-term questions at three mastery levels (recall → apply → analyse/evaluate).

**Mastery checkpoint rule:** a learner should demonstrate ~75% mastery at recall + application before moving on. If weak, reteach differently, practise, reassess.
