## 1. Subject Overview

### 1.1 The MYP Language Acquisition Group
Spanish, French and Hindi at Grade 7 sit in the **IB MYP Language Acquisition** subject group. The purpose of Language Acquisition is to enable students to develop the ability to communicate in a target language (an additional language beyond the language of instruction/mother tongue) and to gain insight into and respect for other cultures and world views. Learning is organised into **six phases** (Emergent phases 1–2, Capable phases 3–4, Proficient phases 5–6). Grade 7 learners at IISH work primarily in the **Emergent phase (phases 1–2)**.

**What "Emergent" means.** Emergent-phase students are early language learners. They:
- comprehend and respond to simple, familiar spoken, written and visual texts;
- interact in basic, rehearsed exchanges about everyday topics (self, family, school, routine);
- produce short, simple texts using a limited but growing range of vocabulary, high-frequency structures and memorised chunks;
- rely on visual support, cognates, gestures and context to construct meaning;
- make frequent errors that do not usually prevent basic communication.
Teaching therefore prioritises **high-frequency vocabulary, present tense, simple sentence patterns, modelled text types, and heavy scaffolding** (word banks, sentence starters, visuals, bilingual glossaries).

### 1.2 The Four MYP Language Acquisition Criteria
All three Emergent languages are assessed against the same four criteria, each out of **8 marks** (band descriptors 1–2, 3–4, 5–6, 7–8):

| Criterion | Title | What it assesses |
|-----------|-------|------------------|
| **A** | Comprehending spoken and visual text | Listening: understanding spoken and audio-visual texts, identifying information, drawing conclusions, recognising conventions. |
| **B** | Comprehending written and visual text | Reading: understanding written and visual texts, identifying information, interpreting, recognising conventions. |
| **C** | Communicating in response to spoken, written and visual text | Speaking/interaction: responding, communicating information and ideas, interacting, using phases-appropriate language. |
| **D** | Using language in spoken and written form | Producing: writing and speaking accurately, organising information, using vocabulary/grammar/conventions appropriate to the phase. |

> **Note on labels.** Some teachers shorthand these as A = Listening, B = Reading, C = Speaking, D = Writing. The official focus is comprehension (A, B) and production/interaction (C, D). Criterion C is interactive/communicative; D is about accuracy and range of language use.

### 1.3 Telugu — a Language & Literature-style frame
Telugu at IISH is taught as a **mother-tongue / Language & Literature-style** course (based on **"Telugu Vennela 2"**), not as an acquisition course. Rather than the four Acquisition criteria, it is framed around the MYP **Language & Literature** competencies — analysing, organising, producing text, and using language — delivered through prose, poetry and value-based lessons. Its emphasis is reading comprehension, vocabulary enrichment, grammar, and speaking/writing for students who already speak Telugu at home. It is detailed in Section 5.

### 1.4 Related Concepts for Language Acquisition
These recur across all units and languages and should be woven into inquiry:
**audience, context, conventions, form, function, meaning, message, purpose, word choice, structure** (and, in Acquisition, also *idiom, patterns, point of view*).

## 2. Shared "Language Acquisition (Emergent)" Unit Framework
*(Applies identically to Spanish, French and Hindi. Each unit is presented once here; language-specific vocabulary, grammar and text samples follow in Sections 3–4.)*

The four **Key Concepts** most used in Language Acquisition are **Communication, Connections, Creativity and Culture**. Each unit below selects a Key Concept, related concepts, a Global Context, a Statement of Inquiry (SOI) and three inquiry questions.

---

### Unit 1 — Education and the World of Work
**Topics:** Daily routine · School life · Text types · Formal/Informal email & letter

- **Key Concept:** Communication
- **Related Concepts:** Audience, Purpose, Conventions, Context
- **Global Context:** *Fairness and development* — exploring access to education and the world of work (also justifiable as *Identities and relationships* through daily routine and school identity). Fairness and development fits because school life and work introduce ideas of opportunity, rights and responsibilities.
- **Statement of Inquiry:** *Effective communicators adapt purpose, audience and conventions when they share information about their education and daily lives.*
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What vocabulary and structures describe a daily routine and a school day? What are the conventions of a formal versus an informal email?
  - **Conceptual:** How does the audience change the words, tone and conventions we choose?
  - **Debatable:** Is a formal register always more effective than an informal one when writing about school or work?

---

### Unit 2 — Technology and the Media
**Topics:** Mobile technology · Social media · Text types · Social media post

- **Key Concept:** Connections
- **Related Concepts:** Message, Function, Word choice, Audience
- **Global Context:** *Scientific and technical innovation* (with a strong link to *Globalization and sustainability*). Mobile technology and social media are innovations that connect people; the context invites students to examine how technology shapes communication.
- **Statement of Inquiry:** *Innovations in technology create new forms and functions of communication that connect people across the world.*
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What vocabulary describes devices, apps and online activities? What are the features of a social media post?
  - **Conceptual:** How does the medium (a post, a message, a comment) change the message and its audience?
  - **Debatable:** Does social media connect us more than it separates us?

---

### Unit 3 — The World We Live In
**Topics:** The weather · The environment · Text types · Article

- **Key Concept:** Creativity (secondary: Connections)
- **Related Concepts:** Purpose, Meaning, Structure, Context
- **Global Context:** *Globalization and sustainability* — the environment and weather connect local experience to global systems and shared responsibility.
- **Statement of Inquiry:** *People use structured, purposeful writing to raise awareness of the environment and inspire others to act sustainably.*
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What vocabulary describes weather, seasons and the environment? How is an informative article structured?
  - **Conceptual:** How do writers structure an article to inform and persuade a reader?
  - **Debatable:** Can an individual's choices really make a difference to the environment?

---

### Unit 4 — Identity and Culture
**Topics:** Food and drinks · Health and well-being · Text type · Brochure

- **Key Concept:** Culture (secondary: Communication)
- **Related Concepts:** Context, Meaning, Audience, Form
- **Global Context:** *Identities and relationships* (with a strong link to *Personal and cultural expression*). Food, health and well-being express who we are; the context lets students explore identity, culture and healthy lifestyles.
- **Statement of Inquiry:** *Food and lifestyle choices express cultural identity, and we communicate them through forms designed for a specific audience.*
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What vocabulary describes food, drink, meals and healthy habits? What are the features of a brochure?
  - **Conceptual:** How does food connect to cultural identity and well-being?
  - **Debatable:** Should traditional food culture change to promote better health?

## 3. Cross-Language Teaching Toolkit (per Unit)
*(Applies across Spanish, French and Hindi. Grammar is calibrated to the Emergent phase: master the present tense and simple structures; introduce past/future only as recognisable chunks.)*

### Unit 1 — Education and the World of Work
- **Core vocabulary fields:** days of the week; times & telling the time; daily routine verbs (get up, wash, eat, go, study, sleep); school subjects; classroom objects; school places (library, canteen, gym); jobs/professions; family members (as context for routines).
- **Key grammar/structures:** present tense of high-frequency and regular verbs; **reflexive verbs** for routine (get up, wash, get dressed); **question words** (what, when, where, who, why, how, how many); time expressions and sequencers (first, then, after that, finally); **connectors** (and, but, because, also).
- **Target text type — Email/Letter (formal vs informal):**
  - *Informal:* friendly greeting (Hi/Dear + first name), relaxed tone, contractions/colloquial phrases, personal questions, casual sign-off (See you / Bye + name).
  - *Formal:* formal salutation (Dear Sir/Madam / Dear Mr/Ms + surname), clear purpose statement, polite register, no slang, formal closing (Yours sincerely/faithfully + full name).
  - Shared structure: greeting → opening → body (purpose/details) → closing → sign-off.
- **Communicative functions:** greetings & introductions; describing a daily routine; telling the time; asking and answering questions; expressing likes/dislikes about subjects.
- **Command-term tasks:** *Identify* the times in an audio timetable (A); *Respond* to questions about your school day (C); *Describe* your daily routine orally (C/D); *Write* an informal email to a pen-pal about school and a formal email to a teacher (D).

### Unit 2 — Technology and the Media
- **Core vocabulary fields:** devices (phone, tablet, laptop, computer); apps & platforms; online actions (post, share, like, comment, chat, download, watch, play); frequency adverbs (always, often, sometimes, never); opinion phrases; time online.
- **Key grammar/structures:** present tense with frequency adverbs; **negation**; verbs of liking/preferring + noun/infinitive; **opinion structures** (I think that…, In my opinion…, I agree/disagree); connectors for contrast (however, on the other hand).
- **Target text type — Social media post:** short and punchy; hook/first line; informal register and emojis; hashtags; a call to interact (like/share/comment); optional image caption. Teach appropriate register and digital-citizenship conventions.
- **Communicative functions:** expressing opinions; agreeing/disagreeing; describing habits & frequency; recommending an app or activity.
- **Command-term tasks:** *Identify* opinions for/against in a listening clip (A); *Describe* how you use technology (D); *Respond* to a friend's post (C); *Write* a social media post about screen-time or a favourite app (D).

### Unit 3 — The World We Live In
- **Core vocabulary fields:** weather expressions; seasons; temperature; nature & landscape (mountain, river, forest, beach); environmental problems (pollution, waste, plastic, climate change); eco-actions (recycle, save water/energy, reuse, plant trees).
- **Key grammar/structures:** weather expressions (impersonal "it is / there is" forms); **near future** to talk about plans/predictions (introduced as a chunk: "going to…"); imperatives for advice/appeals (Recycle! Save water!); comparatives (more/less than); connectors of cause & effect (because, so, therefore).
- **Target text type — Article:** headline; introduction (hook + topic); body organised in paragraphs (problem → cause → solution); facts/examples; conclusion or call to action; may include subheadings. Register is informative and semi-formal.
- **Communicative functions:** describing weather; expressing concern; giving advice/making appeals; presenting facts.
- **Command-term tasks:** *Identify* weather details in a forecast (A); *Present* an environmental problem and solution (C); *Describe* the weather across seasons (D); *Write* a short article encouraging eco-friendly habits (D).

### Unit 4 — Identity and Culture
- **Core vocabulary fields:** foods; drinks; meals (breakfast, lunch, dinner); quantities & containers; flavours & opinions (delicious, sweet, spicy, healthy, unhealthy); body & health (tired, ill, healthy); healthy-habit verbs (eat, drink, sleep, exercise, rest).
- **Key grammar/structures:** likes/dislikes + food nouns; **partitive / quantity expressions** (some, a lot of); **giving advice** with "should" / imperatives (You should eat…, Drink more water!); adjective agreement with food nouns; frequency for habits.
- **Target text type — Brochure:** catchy title; short informative sections with subheadings; bullet points and tips; imperatives and direct address ("you"); persuasive, positive tone; images/layout; contact or call-to-action panel.
- **Communicative functions:** expressing food preferences; ordering/offering food; giving health advice; describing a healthy lifestyle.
- **Command-term tasks:** *Identify* foods and opinions in a dialogue (A); *Respond* in a role-play at a café/market (C); *Describe* your diet and healthy habits (D); *Write* a brochure promoting healthy eating and well-being (D).

### 4.1 SPANISH (Emergent)

#### Alphabet & script
Latin alphabet (27 letters — includes **ñ**). Once digraphs, historically "ch" and "ll" were separate letters; now alphabetised within c and l. Written accents (á, é, í, ó, ú), the tilde (ñ) and diaeresis (ü) matter and change meaning/pronunciation. Inverted opening marks **¿ … ?** and **¡ … !** frame questions and exclamations.

#### Pronunciation basics
Largely phonetic — words are pronounced as written. Five pure vowel sounds (a, e, i, o, u). **h** is silent; **j** and **g** (before e/i) are a throaty /x/; **ñ** = "ny"; **ll**/**y** ≈ "y"; **rr** is trilled; **v** ≈ **b**; **z**/soft **c** = "s" in Latin America. Stress usually falls on the second-to-last syllable unless an accent marks otherwise.

#### Key grammar peculiarity
- **Gender & articles:** every noun is masculine or feminine; articles **el/la (los/las)** and **un/una** must agree. Adjectives agree in gender and number (*el chico alto → la chica alta → los chicos altos*).
- **Ser vs estar** (two verbs for "to be"): **ser** for permanent identity/characteristics/origin/time (*Soy estudiante; Son las tres*); **estar** for location, temporary states and feelings (*Estoy cansado; El libro está en la mesa*).
- **Regular verbs in three families — -ar, -er, -ir.** Present tense endings:

| Subject | hablar (-ar) | comer (-er) | vivir (-ir) |
|---|---|---|---|
| yo | hablo | como | vivo |
| tú | hablas | comes | vives |
| él/ella/usted | habla | come | vive |
| nosotros/as | hablamos | comemos | vivimos |
| vosotros/as | habláis | coméis | vivís |
| ellos/ellas/ustedes | hablan | comen | viven |

- **Reflexive verbs** for routine: *me levanto, te lavas, se acuesta.*

#### Sample text type (Unit 1 — informal email)
> **Asunto:** ¡Hola desde mi cole!
> Hola Marco:
> ¿Qué tal? Me llamo Ana y tengo doce años. Cada día me levanto a las siete y desayuno con mi familia. Voy al colegio a las ocho. Mi asignatura favorita es el arte porque es divertido. Por la tarde hago los deberes y juego al fútbol. ¿Cómo es tu día? Escríbeme pronto.
> Un abrazo,
> Ana

#### Common misconceptions / errors (Spanish)
- **False cognates:** *embarazada* = "pregnant" (not "embarrassed"); *éxito* = "success" (not "exit"); *librería* = "bookshop" (not "library" = *biblioteca*); *sensible* = "sensitive". Teach carefully.
- **Ser/estar confusion:** *Soy cansado* ✗ → *Estoy cansado* ✓ (feeling = estar).
- **Gender errors:** *el problema* is masculine despite ending in -a (as are *el día, el mapa*); *la mano* is feminine.
- **Adjective agreement dropped:** *la casa blanco* ✗ → *la casa blanca* ✓.
- **Omitting inverted ¿ ¡** at the start of questions/exclamations.

### 4.2 FRENCH (Emergent)

#### Alphabet & script
Latin alphabet (26 letters) plus diacritics: accents **é (aigu), è/à/ù (grave), â/ê/î/ô/û (circumflex)**, the **cédille (ç)** and the **tréma (ë, ï, ü)**. Accents affect pronunciation and meaning (*a* vs *à*, *ou* vs *où*). Ligature **œ** appears in some words (*sœur, cœur*).

#### Pronunciation basics
Not fully phonetic. Many **silent final consonants** (the -s, -t, -d, -x are usually silent). **Nasal vowels** (an/en, in/im, on, un). **Liaison** links a normally-silent final consonant to a following vowel (*les_amis* → "lay-zami"). The French **r** is guttural; **u** is a rounded front vowel with no English equivalent; **h** is silent (mute or aspirate). Stress is even, on the last syllable of a group.

#### Key grammar peculiarity
- **Gender & articles:** nouns are masculine or feminine. Definite **le/la/l'/les**, indefinite **un/une/des**, and **partitive du/de la/de l'/des** ("some"). The partitive is essential for food: *Je mange du pain, de la confiture, des fruits.*
- **Être and avoir** (the two core auxiliary/irregular verbs):

| | être (to be) | avoir (to have) |
|---|---|---|
| je | suis | ai |
| tu | es | as |
| il/elle/on | est | a |
| nous | sommes | avons |
| vous | êtes | avez |
| ils/elles | sont | ont |

- **Regular -er verbs** (the largest, most regular family):

| Subject | parler (to speak) |
|---|---|
| je | parle |
| tu | parles |
| il/elle | parle |
| nous | parlons |
| vous | parlez |
| ils/elles | parlent |

- **Adjective agreement** (usually add -e for feminine, -s for plural) and most adjectives follow the noun (*une voiture rouge*); a small set (BAGS: beauty, age, goodness, size) precede it.
- **Accents matter** — teach them as part of spelling, not decoration.

#### Sample text type (Unit 1 — informal email)
> **Objet :** Bonjour de mon collège !
> Salut Léa,
> Ça va ? Je m'appelle Hugo et j'ai douze ans. Chaque jour, je me lève à sept heures et je prends le petit-déjeuner. Je vais au collège à huit heures. Ma matière préférée, c'est le sport, parce que c'est amusant. L'après-midi, je fais mes devoirs. Et toi, comment est ta journée ? Écris-moi vite !
> À bientôt,
> Hugo

#### Common misconceptions / errors (French)
- **False cognates (faux amis):** *librairie* = "bookshop" (library = *bibliothèque*); *journée* = "day" (not "journey"); *actuellement* = "currently" (not "actually"); *sensible* = "sensitive"; *assister à* = "to attend".
- **Gender errors** transferring from English or Spanish (*le/la* swaps) — teach the article with every noun.
- **Forgetting the partitive:** *Je mange pain* ✗ → *Je mange du pain* ✓.
- **Pronouncing silent letters** (final -s, -t) and omitting **liaison**.
- **Accent omission** changing the word (*ou* "or" vs *où* "where"; *a* "has" vs *à* "to/at").
- **Adjective position:** *une rouge voiture* ✗ → *une voiture rouge* ✓.

### 4.3 HINDI (Emergent)

#### Alphabet & script — Devanagari (देवनागरी)
Hindi is written in **Devanagari**, an *abugida* (each consonant carries an inherent /a/ vowel). Written **left to right** with a connecting top line (**शिरोरेखा**). Core inventory:
- **Vowels (स्वर):** अ आ इ ई उ ऊ ए ऐ ओ औ (and अं, अः).
- **Consonants (व्यंजन):** क ख ग घ ङ · च छ ज झ ञ · ट ठ ड ढ ण · त थ द ध न · प फ ब भ म · य र ल व श ष स ह.
- **Matras (मात्रा):** vowel signs attached to consonants (का कि की कु कू के कै को कौ).
- Numbers: ० १ २ ३ ४ ५ ६ ७ ८ ९.

#### Pronunciation basics
Highly phonetic once the script is learned. Key contrasts English lacks:
- **Aspiration:** unaspirated vs aspirated pairs (क /k/ vs ख /kh/, प /p/ vs फ /ph/).
- **Retroflex vs dental:** ट/ठ/ड (tongue curled back) vs त/थ/द (tongue on teeth).
- **Nasalisation:** anusvara (ं) and chandrabindu (ँ).
- **Conjunct consonants (संयुक्त अक्षर):** क्ष, त्र, ज्ञ, श्र.

#### Key grammar peculiarity
- **Word order is Subject–Object–Verb (SOV):** *मैं सेब खाता हूँ* (I apple eat) = "I eat an apple."
- **Gender:** nouns are masculine or feminine; **adjectives and verbs agree with the noun's gender and number** (*अच्छा लड़का* good boy / *अच्छी लड़की* good girl / *अच्छे लड़के* good boys).
- **Postpositions (परसर्ग), not prepositions:** they come *after* the noun — *ने, को, से, में, पर, का/के/की* (*मेज़ पर* = "on the table"; *स्कूल में* = "in school").
- **Verb agreement & the हूँ/है/हैं pattern (present):**

| Subject | होना "to be" (present) |
|---|---|
| मैं (I) | हूँ (hūn) |
| तू (you, intimate) | है (hai) |
| तुम (you, familiar) | हो (ho) |
| आप (you, respectful) | हैं (hain) |
| यह/वह (he/she/it) | है (hai) |
| ये/वे / हम (they / we) | हैं (hain) |

Present habitual verbs combine a participle + auxiliary and agree with the subject: *मैं पढ़ता हूँ* (m.) / *मैं पढ़ती हूँ* (f.) = "I study."
- **Honorifics / respect (आदर):** **तू** (intimate) < **तुम** (familiar) < **आप** (respectful/plural). **आप** takes plural verb forms even for one person. Choosing register correctly is a cultural-communication skill.

#### Sample text type (Unit 1 — informal letter/email)
> **विषय:** मेरे स्कूल से नमस्ते!
> प्रिय रिया,
> कैसी हो? मेरा नाम आरव है और मैं बारह साल का हूँ। मैं हर दिन सात बजे उठता हूँ और नाश्ता करता हूँ। मैं आठ बजे स्कूल जाता हूँ। मेरा पसंदीदा विषय विज्ञान है क्योंकि यह दिलचस्प है। शाम को मैं गृहकार्य करता हूँ। तुम्हारा दिन कैसा होता है? जल्दी जवाब देना।
> तुम्हारा दोस्त,
> आरव

#### Common misconceptions / errors (Hindi)
- **Script confusions:** mixing up similar letters — ब (ba) / भ (bha) / म (ma); द (da) / ढ (ḍha); व (va) / ब (ba); घ (gha) / ध (dha). Missing or wrong **matra** changes the word entirely.
- **Gender-of-verb agreement dropped:** girls saying *मैं खाता हूँ* ✗ → *मैं खाती हूँ* ✓.
- **Using prepositions before nouns** (English transfer) instead of postpositions after: *में स्कूल* ✗ → *स्कूल में* ✓.
- **Honorific mismatch:** using **तू** with a teacher/elder (rude) — use **आप** with plural verb.
- **Forgetting the top line (शिरोरेखा)** or writing left-out inherent vowel.
- **Aspiration errors:** confusing क/ख or प/फ changes meaning.

## 5. TELUGU (Language & Literature style — "Telugu Vennela 2")

### 5.1 Approach & philosophy
Telugu at Grade 7 follows a **mother-tongue Language & Literature** model. Using the reader **"Telugu Vennela 2,"** the course strengthens language proficiency through **prose (వచనం), poetry (పద్యం/కవిత) and value-based lessons.** The goal is deep engagement with texts — comprehension, interpretation, appreciation and personal response — alongside strong grammar and composition, developing both **language competence and life skills** (critical thinking and self-expression).

### 5.2 Skill strands
- **Reading comprehension (పఠన అవగాహన):** understanding prose and poetry, main ideas, inference, summarising, appreciating literary features.
- **Vocabulary (పదజాలం):** synonyms (పర్యాయపదాలు), antonyms (వ్యతిరేక పదాలు), idioms (జాతీయాలు), proverbs (సామెతలు), word meanings.
- **Grammar (వ్యాకరణం):** case markers, sandhi, parts of speech, tenses (below).
- **Speaking (మాట్లాడటం):** recitation of poems, discussion, storytelling, expressing opinions.
- **Writing (రచన):** paragraph writing, essays, letters, story-completion, creative and value-based reflective writing.

### 5.3 Thematic values
Lessons are chosen to explore **education (విద్య), friendship (స్నేహం), perseverance (పట్టుదల), social responsibility (సామాజిక బాధ్యత) and cultural heritage (సాంస్కృతిక వారసత్వం).** Literature is the vehicle for reflecting on character, ethics and identity.

### 5.4 Telugu script — Varnamala (వర్ణమాల) basics
Telugu is an *abugida* written left to right with rounded letters. The **varnamala** comprises:
- **Vowels (అచ్చులు):** అ ఆ ఇ ఈ ఉ ఊ ఋ ఎ ఏ ఐ ఒ ఓ ఔ (with అం, అః).
- **Consonants (హల్లులు):** క ఖ గ ఘ ఙ · చ ఛ జ ఝ ఞ · ట ఠ డ ఢ ణ · త థ ద ధ న · ప ఫ బ భ మ · య ర ల వ శ ష స హ ళ క్ష ఱ.
- **Vowel signs (గుణింతాలు / మాత్రలు):** attached to consonants to form syllables (క కా కి కీ కు కూ …).
- **Conjuncts (ఒత్తులు):** secondary consonant forms written below the base letter.
- Numerals: ౦ ౧ ౨ ౩ ౪ ౫ ౬ ౭ ౮ ౯.

### 5.5 Grammar for Grade 7
- **Vibhaktulu (విభక్తులు) — case markers:** the eight cases attach postpositional markers to nouns (e.g., డు/ము/వు/లు; ని/ను; చేత/చే; కొఱకు/కై; వలన/పట్టి; కంటె; అందు/న; ఓ). These signal grammatical role (subject, object, instrument, etc.).
- **Sandhi (సంధి) basics:** rules for combining sounds when words join — e.g., **అకార సంధి, ఉత్వ సంధి, ఇత్వ సంధి, గుణ సంధి, సవర్ణదీర్ఘ సంధి.** Students learn to split (విడదీయు) and join (కలుపు) words.
- **Parts of speech:** noun (నామవాచకం), pronoun (సర్వనామం), verb (క్రియ), adjective (విశేషణం), adverb (క్రియా విశేషణం).
- **Tenses (కాలాలు):** past (భూతకాలం), present (వర్తమానకాలం), future (భవిష్యత్కాలం), with verb agreement in gender/number/person.
- **Word-play features:** synonyms, antonyms, జాతీయాలు (idioms), సామెతలు (proverbs), ప్రకృతి–వికృతి (tatsama–tadbhava pairs).

### 5.6 How prose & poetry are taught
- **Prose (వచన పాఠాలు):** shared reading → vocabulary building → comprehension questions → theme and message → grammar drawn from the text → summary and value discussion.
- **Poetry (పద్యాలు):** recitation with correct rhythm (ధార), meaning of each line (భావం/తాత్పర్యం), poetic devices and imagery, memorisation and appreciation, connecting the poem's message to values and life.

### 5.7 Assessment style
A blend of continuous and summative tasks: reading comprehension passages, grammar exercises (sandhi, vibhakti, synonyms/antonyms), **poem recitation and భావం**, essay/letter/paragraph writing, story completion, oral discussion and value-based reflective responses. Emphasis on accuracy of script, clarity of expression, and depth of thought.

### 5.8 Inquiry framework
- **Key Concept:** Culture (with Communication and Perspective)
- **Related Concepts:** Theme, Character, Purpose, Point of view, Structure
- **Global Context:** *Personal and cultural expression* (with links to *Identities and relationships*)
- **Statement of Inquiry:** *Literature preserves cultural heritage and shapes personal values, and skilled readers and writers use language to express identity and respond to their world.*
- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What are the themes and messages of the prose and poems studied? What grammar rules (sandhi, vibhakti) govern how words combine?
  - **Conceptual:** How do writers use literary forms and language to communicate values like perseverance and social responsibility?
  - **Debatable:** Is literature a more powerful teacher of values than direct instruction?

### 5.9 Common misconceptions / errors (Telugu)
- **Sandhi splitting/joining errors** — applying the wrong sandhi rule or failing to recognise a joined form.
- **Vibhakti confusion** — choosing the wrong case marker for the noun's role.
- **గుణింతం / ఒత్తు errors** — wrong vowel sign or dropped secondary consonant, changing the word.
- **ప్రకృతి–వికృతి mix-ups** — confusing the Sanskrit-origin (tatsama) and colloquial (tadbhava) form.
- **Spoken vs written register** — writing colloquial (వాడుక) forms where formal (గ్రాంథిక/ప్రామాణిక) is expected.

## 6. Language-Learning ATL & Strategies

Language Acquisition develops several **Approaches to Learning (ATL)** clusters:
- **Communication:** negotiating meaning; using and interpreting non-verbal cues; reading for gist and detail; note-taking; paraphrasing; using bilingual dictionaries and glossaries.
- **Self-management:** memorisation routines (spaced repetition, flashcards, vocabulary logs); setting language goals; managing anxiety about speaking; risk-taking with new language.
- **Thinking:** guessing meaning from cognates and context; recognising patterns in grammar; transferring structures to new topics; comparing the target language to known languages.
- **Social:** collaborating in role-plays and pair-work; giving peer feedback; respecting turn-taking in conversation.
- **Research:** finding and evaluating authentic sources (menus, forecasts, posts); gathering culturally relevant information.

**Practical classroom strategies:** word walls and knowledge organisers; sentence-starter scaffolds and writing frames; modelled texts before independent production; comprehensible input (visuals, gestures, target-language immersion at a manageable level); regular low-stakes speaking; using cognates as a bridge; error-correction codes; recycling vocabulary across units.
