## Subject Overview: Individuals & Societies in the MYP

History at IISH sits inside the MYP **Individuals & Societies (I&S)** subject group. I&S encourages learners to appreciate the diversity of human culture, attitudes and beliefs, and to investigate human systems (historical, geographical, economic, political, social) using disciplined inquiry. History specifically develops students as young historians who work with **evidence**, weigh **interpretations**, and construct **reasoned arguments** about the past.

### The Four MYP I&S Assessment Criteria
Each criterion is marked out of 8 (four achievement bands: 1–2, 3–4, 5–6, 7–8).

- **Criterion A — Knowing and understanding.** Use terminology in context; demonstrate knowledge and understanding of content and concepts through descriptions, explanations and examples.
- **Criterion B — Investigating.** Formulate a clear and focused research question; follow an action plan; collect and record relevant information; evaluate the process and results of the investigation.
- **Criterion C — Communicating.** Communicate information and ideas in a way appropriate to the audience and purpose; structure information logically; reference sources of information (documenting/citing).
- **Criterion D — Thinking critically.** Analyse concepts, issues, models and arguments; analyse and evaluate a range of sources/data for **origin, purpose, value and limitations (OPVL)**; recognise different perspectives and their implications; synthesise information to make valid, well-supported arguments.

> Teaching note: Source evaluation (OPVL) lives primarily in **Criterion D**. When students "evaluate a source," they are being marked on Criterion D, not just recalling facts (Criterion A).

### Key Concepts (I&S drivers used in History)
The three key concepts most often driving MYP History units are **Change**, **Global interactions**, and **Systems** (with **Time, place and space** as an important supporting concept). A key concept is a big, transferable idea that frames a whole unit.

### I&S Related Concepts (History)
Related concepts are subject-specific lenses that sharpen a unit. The IB I&S related concepts most used in History include:

- **Causality (cause and consequence)** — why events happen and what follows.
- **Perspective** — the viewpoint from which people experience and record events.
- **Significance** — why something matters, then and now.
- **Empire** — the extension of one state's control over other peoples/territories.
- **Power / Governance** — how authority is organised and exercised.
- **Innovation and revolution** — transformative change in ideas, technology or society.
- Others used across units: **Civilization, Culture, Conflict, Cooperation, Trade, Interdependence, Identity, Ideology, Resources.**

### Global Contexts (the six MYP lenses)
Units are framed by one of six global contexts: **Identities and relationships; Orientation in space and time; Personal and cultural expression; Scientific and technical innovation; Fairness and development; Globalization and sustainability.**

# HISTORIANS' TOOLKIT (Prerequisites)

Before the units, students master the historian's core skills: identifying and analysing sources, and judging bias and reliability. These skills are assessed all year, mainly under **Criterion D**.

## Identifying Sources and Their Analysis

### Primary vs Secondary Sources
- **Primary source:** produced *at the time* of the event by someone with direct experience or connection. Examples: a Roman coin, a Chola temple inscription, Ibn Battuta's travel account, a ship's log, a Renaissance painting, an eyewitness letter, a photograph, an artefact.
- **Secondary source:** produced *later*, interpreting or analysing primary material. Examples: a modern textbook, a historian's biography of Galileo, a documentary, an encyclopaedia entry.
- **Key subtlety:** The same object can be primary or secondary depending on the question. A 1960s history book is a *secondary* source about the Roman Empire, but a *primary* source about how 1960s historians thought.

**Significance ("so what"):** History is an argument built from evidence. Knowing whether a source is primary or secondary tells you how close it is to events and what kind of claim it can support.

**Perspective/interpretation note:** Neither type is automatically "better." Primary sources are close but may be biased or fragmentary; secondary sources have hindsight and can compare evidence but depend on the primaries they used.

**Misconception:** *"Primary sources are always more reliable/true than secondary sources."*
- *Why believed:* "Primary" sounds like "first-hand = truth."
- *Why wrong:* A primary source may lie, exaggerate, be propaganda, or record only one viewpoint (e.g., a king's inscription praising himself). A careful secondary source that cross-checks many primaries can be more accurate.
- *Corrective:* Reliability depends on the source's **origin, purpose, value and limitations**, not on the primary/secondary label alone.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **State** the difference between a primary and a secondary source and give one example of each from medieval India.
2. *(Apply)* **Classify** these as primary or secondary for a study of the Roman Empire, and justify: a Roman coin; a 2020 documentary; a letter by Cicero; a modern atlas.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** is a primary source always more useful to a historian than a secondary source? Support with examples.

## Source Bias and Reliability using OPVL

### Full teaching entry: The OPVL Method
OPVL is a structured way to evaluate any source. It stands for **Origin, Purpose, Value, Limitation**. It is the backbone of MYP **Criterion D** source analysis.

- **O — Origin:** *Who made it, what is it, when and where?* Author, type of source, date, place, primary/secondary. (You describe the source.)
- **P — Purpose:** *Why was it made, and for whom?* To inform, persuade, record, entertain, glorify, warn, sell? Who was the intended audience?
- **V — Value:** *What can a historian usefully learn from it?* Value flows from origin and purpose — e.g., an eyewitness gives us authentic first-hand detail; a government poster reveals what leaders wanted people to believe.
- **L — Limitation:** *What are the weaknesses or gaps?* One-sided viewpoint, missing context, propaganda, translation issues, incompleteness, hindsight, small sample. (Limitation is **not** the same as "it's a bad source" — every source has limitations.)

**How to apply it (method):**
1. Read/observe the source carefully.
2. Establish **Origin** from the caption/attribution and content.
3. Infer **Purpose** from tone, language, imagery and intended audience.
4. Derive **Value** *from* origin and purpose (link them — don't list generic values).
5. Derive **Limitation** *from* origin and purpose too.
6. Conclude with a judgement on reliability/usefulness **for a specific enquiry**.

**Worked mini-example.**
> *Source:* A stone inscription commissioned by a Chola king (c. 1010 CE) at the Brihadisvara temple, Thanjavur, listing his military conquests and temple donations.
- **Origin:** A royal inscription in stone, carved in Tamil, c. 1010 CE, at Thanjavur, commissioned by the Chola ruler — a **primary** source.
- **Purpose:** To glorify the king, record his piety and conquests, and legitimise his rule for subjects and posterity (intended audience: the public, priests, future rulers).
- **Value:** Excellent first-hand evidence of Chola self-image, administrative reach, religious patronage, place-names and the resources devoted to temple-building.
- **Limitation:** It is royal propaganda — it exaggerates victories, omits defeats and the peasant/ordinary perspective, and gives the *king's* view, not an objective account.
- **Judgement:** Very useful for studying how Chola kings projected power and legitimacy, but unreliable as a neutral record of what actually happened in battle.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **Identify** what each letter of OPVL stands for.
2. *(Apply)* **Analyse** the origin and purpose of a wartime propaganda poster and explain how each shapes its value and limitations.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* Using OPVL, **evaluate** the value and limitations of the Chola royal inscription above for a historian studying ordinary medieval life.

# UNIT 1: Rise and Fall of Empires

**Key Concept:** Systems
**Related Concepts:** Empire, Power/Governance, Causality, Significance
**Global Context:** Orientation in space and time
**Statement of Inquiry:** *The building and collapse of empires reveal how systems of power and governance rise, adapt and fail across time and place.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- *Factual:* What factors allowed empires such as the Gupta, Roman, Byzantine, Chola and Vijayanagara to form and expand?
- *Conceptual:* How do systems of governance sustain — and eventually undermine — imperial power?
- *Debatable:* Do empires collapse mainly because of internal weakness or external pressure?

## Chapter 1.1 — Why Empires Rise: Factors in Empire-Building

**Factual grounding.** An **empire** is a political unit in which a dominant centre (a state or people) rules over multiple territories and peoples, often of different cultures. Recurring factors that enable empire-building:
- **Military strength & technology** (organised armies, cavalry, war-elephants, siege craft, weapons).
- **Strong leadership & administration** (capable rulers, bureaucracy, tax systems, law codes).
- **Economic resources & trade** (fertile agriculture, control of trade routes, wealth to fund armies).
- **Geography** (defensible borders, rivers, ports, strategic location).
- **Ideology & religion** (a unifying belief system or claim to divine/legitimate rule).
- **Communication & infrastructure** (roads, ports, a common language or coinage).

**Significance ("so what").** These factors form a repeatable pattern (a "system"), so historians can compare very different empires — Roman, Gupta, Chola — using the same analytical lens of causality.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Rulers described expansion as bringing order, faith or civilisation; conquered peoples often experienced it as loss of freedom, tribute and cultural disruption. Both perspectives are part of the historical record.

**Misconception:** *"Empires were built mainly by military conquest."*
- *Why believed:* Battles and famous conquerors dominate popular stories.
- *Why wrong:* Conquest starts an empire, but **administration, trade, law and ideology** are what let it grow and last. The Guptas and Cholas expanded partly through alliances, trade and cultural prestige, not conquest alone.
- *Corrective:* Empire-building is multi-causal; military power alone rarely sustains an empire.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **List** four factors that help an empire expand.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how control of trade routes could strengthen an empire, using one example.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** is strong administration more important than military conquest in building a lasting empire?

## Chapter 1.2 — Medieval Empires and Governance

**Factual grounding.** In the medieval period (roughly 500–1500 CE), empires developed varied systems of **governance**: centralised bureaucracies (Byzantine, Chola), provincial/administrative divisions (Gupta *bhuktis* and *vishayas*; Chola *nadu*, *mandalam* and famous village self-government assemblies called **sabha/ur**), law and taxation systems, and the use of religion to legitimise rule. The **Byzantine** Empire codified Roman law under **Justinian I** (the *Corpus Juris Civilis*, c. 529–534 CE). The **Cholas** ran a remarkably organised state with local elected village councils recorded in inscriptions (e.g., **Uttaramerur**, 10th century).

**Significance.** Governance systems, not just conquest, decided how effectively an empire collected revenue, kept order and endured. Chola village self-government and Byzantine law codes are among the most studied medieval institutions.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** "Good governance" looks different depending on viewpoint: to the ruling elite it meant stability and revenue; to villagers it might mean tax burdens or, in the Chola case, genuine local participation.

**Misconception:** *"Medieval government was pure chaos with no real administration."*
- *Why believed:* The label "medieval" is popularly (and wrongly) linked with "backward" or "dark."
- *Why wrong:* Byzantine law, Chola village assemblies and Gupta provincial administration were sophisticated systems.
- *Corrective:* Medieval empires had complex, documented governance.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **Identify** one governance feature of the Chola state and one of the Byzantine state.
2. *(Apply)* **Describe** how the Uttaramerur inscriptions help us understand Chola local government.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** how far religion was used as a tool of governance in one medieval empire.

## Chapter 1.3 — Political Legacies and Their Evolution

**Factual grounding.** Empires leave **legacies** — institutions and ideas that outlive them. Roman legacy: **Roman law**, republican/administrative concepts, Latin, engineering (roads, aqueducts, arches). Byzantine legacy: preservation of Greco-Roman knowledge, **Justinian's Code** (a foundation of later European law), Orthodox Christianity, and Cyrillic script's spread. Gupta legacy: the decimal place-value number system and the concept of **zero**, advances in astronomy (Aryabhata) and Sanskrit literature (Kalidasa). Chola legacy: temple architecture, bronze sculpture, maritime and administrative models. These legacies **evolved** as later societies adapted them.

**Significance.** Studying legacies shows **continuity and change** — how the modern world inherited law, mathematics, art and governance from earlier empires.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Legacies are selectively remembered; nations sometimes emphasise a glorious imperial past to build modern identity, which historians must read critically.

**Misconception:** *"When an empire falls, its influence disappears."*
- *Why believed:* "Fall" sounds like total erasure.
- *Why wrong:* Roman law, Gupta mathematics and Byzantine scholarship survived and shaped later civilisations.
- *Corrective:* Political and cultural legacies often outlast the empire itself.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **State** two legacies of the Roman Empire.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how the Gupta contribution to mathematics still affects us today.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** is the Byzantine Empire's greatest legacy the preservation of earlier knowledge?

## Chapter 1.4 — Why Empires Fall: Causes of Collapse

**Factual grounding.** Empires collapse through combinations of causes:
- **Internal:** weak/corrupt leadership, succession disputes, civil war, economic decline, overtaxation, overextension, administrative breakdown, social unrest.
- **External:** invasions and migrations, military defeat, pressure from rival powers.
- **Environmental/economic:** plague, famine, climate stress, disrupted trade, inflation (Rome debased its coinage).
Examples: the **Western Roman Empire** fell in **476 CE** (deposition of Romulus Augustulus) after decades of overextension, economic strain, civil war and Germanic invasions. The **Gupta Empire** declined in the 6th century under **Huna (Hephthalite) invasions** and internal fragmentation. **Constantinople** (last Byzantine capital) fell to the **Ottomans in 1453**. **Vijayanagara** was crippled after the **Battle of Talikota, 1565**.

**Significance.** Collapse is rarely one cause; understanding multi-causality helps students avoid simplistic "one big reason" explanations — a core historical-thinking skill.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** "Fall" vs "transformation": some historians argue Rome didn't so much "fall" as gradually transform into medieval kingdoms and the surviving Eastern (Byzantine) Empire.

**Misconception:** *"The Roman Empire fell suddenly in 476 CE."*
- *Why believed:* Textbooks give a single dramatic date.
- *Why wrong:* Decline unfolded over centuries; only the **Western** empire ended in 476, while the **Eastern (Byzantine)** empire continued for nearly another thousand years until 1453.
- *Corrective:* 476 marks the end of the *Western* Roman Empire, a gradual process, not a single collapse of "Rome."

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **Identify** two internal and two external causes of imperial collapse.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how economic problems contributed to the fall of the Western Roman Empire.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** do empires collapse because of internal weakness rather than external attack? Use two case studies.

## Chapter 1.5 — The Rise of Feudalism in the Medieval Period

**Factual grounding.** After the Western Roman Empire's collapse, centralised authority in Western Europe weakened. **Feudalism** emerged (roughly 9th–13th centuries) as a decentralised system of mutual obligations: a **king** granted land (**fiefs**) to **lords/nobles** in exchange for military service and loyalty; lords gave protection and land use to **vassals/knights**; at the base, **peasants/serfs** worked the land (the **manor**) in exchange for protection, but were tied to it. It was reinforced by the manorial economy and the Church.

**Significance.** Feudalism was the political and social answer to the loss of imperial security — a direct **consequence** of Rome's fall and a key feature of medieval Europe. It shaped land, law, warfare and class for centuries.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** From a lord's view feudalism provided order and service; from a serf's view it meant obligation and limited freedom. Modern historians debate whether "feudalism" is even a single, coherent system or a label imposed later.

**Misconception:** *"Feudalism was a single, uniform system planned across all of Europe."*
- *Why believed:* Neat pyramid diagrams make it look designed and universal.
- *Why wrong:* It developed unevenly, varied by region, and the tidy "pyramid" is a later simplification.
- *Corrective:* Feudalism was a loose, evolving set of local arrangements, not a planned, identical system everywhere.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **List** the main levels of feudal society from top to bottom.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how the fall of the Western Roman Empire helped feudalism develop.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** the claim that feudalism benefited lords far more than peasants.

## Chapter 1.6 — Empire Case Study Profiles

Below are compact profiles for each required case study. Each covers **dates, location, rise factors, governance, achievements, and causes of decline.**

### Gupta Empire
- **Dates:** c. 320 CE – c. 550 CE (often called India's "Golden Age").
- **Location:** Northern India, centred on the Ganges plain; capital **Pataliputra**.
- **Rise factors:** Founded by **Chandragupta I**; expansion under **Samudragupta** (conquests) and **Chandragupta II / Vikramaditya**; strong agriculture, trade wealth, capable rulers.
- **Governance:** Comparatively decentralised; provinces (*bhuktis*) under governors, districts (*vishayas*); lighter central control than the earlier Mauryas; revenue from land tax and trade.
- **Achievements:** Decimal system and the concept of **zero**; **Aryabhata** (astronomy/mathematics, c. 499 CE); **Kalidasa** (Sanskrit literature); advances in medicine and metallurgy (the rust-resistant **Iron Pillar of Delhi**); flourishing art and temple architecture.
- **Causes of decline:** **Huna (Hephthalite) invasions** from the northwest, weakening later rulers, loss of revenue and provincial fragmentation by the mid-6th century.

### Roman Empire
- **Dates:** Empire from **27 BCE** (Augustus) ; Western Empire fell **476 CE**; (Republic preceded it from 509 BCE).
- **Location:** Centred on Rome/Italy, spanning the Mediterranean — Europe, North Africa, the Near East.
- **Rise factors:** Disciplined **legions**, engineering, expansion via conquest and alliances, integration through **citizenship**, roads and trade.
- **Governance:** Emperor at the head; provinces run by governors; codified **Roman law**; professional army; extensive infrastructure (roads, aqueducts).
- **Achievements:** Roman law, engineering (roads, aqueducts, concrete, the arch), Latin language, urban planning, a long period of relative peace (**Pax Romana**, c. 27 BCE–180 CE).
- **Causes of decline:** Political instability and civil wars, economic strain and **inflation/debased coinage**, overextension, division of the empire (**395 CE**), and **Germanic invasions**; Western Empire ended 476 CE.

### Byzantine Empire
- **Dates:** c. **330 CE** (Constantinople founded/dedicated by Constantine) – **1453 CE** (Ottoman conquest); i.e., the surviving Eastern Roman Empire.
- **Location:** Eastern Mediterranean; capital **Constantinople** (modern Istanbul), straddling Europe and Asia.
- **Rise factors:** Wealthy, defensible capital at a strategic trade crossroads; continuity of Roman institutions; strong walls; control of trade.
- **Governance:** Highly centralised bureaucracy; emperor with religious authority; **Justinian's Code** (*Corpus Juris Civilis*, 529–534 CE) systematised Roman law; Orthodox Christianity closely tied to the state.
- **Achievements:** Preserved Greco-Roman knowledge; **Hagia Sophia** (completed 537 CE); Justinian's legal code; art and mosaics; long survival as a Christian bulwark.
- **Causes of decline:** Wars with Persia and later Arabs and Turks; the **Fourth Crusade sack of Constantinople (1204)**; loss of territory; economic decline; final **Ottoman conquest in 1453**.

### Imperial Cholas
- **Dates:** Imperial Chola period c. **850 CE – 1279 CE** (revived under Vijayalaya c. 850; peak c. 985–1120).
- **Location:** South India (Tamil region), Kaveri delta; capital **Thanjavur** (later Gangaikonda Cholapuram); a strong maritime empire reaching Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia.
- **Rise factors:** Fertile Kaveri agriculture, naval power, control of Indian Ocean trade; strong rulers **Rajaraja I** (r. 985–1014) and **Rajendra I** (r. 1014–1044).
- **Governance:** Well-organised administration; provinces (*mandalam*), districts and **nadu**; famous **village self-government** (elected assemblies — *sabha/ur*) recorded in the **Uttaramerur** inscriptions; land surveys and taxation.
- **Achievements:** **Brihadisvara Temple, Thanjavur** (completed c. 1010 CE, a UNESCO site); superb **bronze sculpture** (e.g., Nataraja); powerful navy; overseas expeditions to Srivijaya (Southeast Asia) under Rajendra I; spread of Tamil culture.
- **Causes of decline:** Rising power of rivals (Hoysalas, Pandyas), weakening central control and succession problems; the empire faded by the late 13th century (c. 1279).

### Vijayanagara Empire
- **Dates:** c. **1336 CE – 1646 CE** (peak in the 15th–early 16th century).
- **Location:** South India (Deccan); capital **Vijayanagara** (Hampi), on the Tungabhadra River.
- **Rise factors:** Founded by **Harihara I and Bukka Raya I** (Sangama dynasty); strong military, wealth from agriculture and the cotton/spice/horse trade; role as a major Hindu power in the Deccan.
- **Governance:** Centralised monarchy with a military-fief (**nayaka**) system in which nobles held land in return for maintaining troops; provincial administration; substantial revenue system.
- **Achievements:** Splendid capital at **Hampi** (temples, markets, irrigation, UNESCO World Heritage); flourishing trade, art and literature; peak under **Krishnadevaraya** (r. 1509–1529).
- **Causes of decline:** Decisive defeat by a coalition of Deccan Sultanates at the **Battle of Talikota (1565)**, after which Vijayanagara city was sacked; the empire fragmented and declined, lingering until c. 1646.

# UNIT 2: Medieval Life

**Key Concept:** Global interactions
**Related Concepts:** Trade, Interdependence, Culture, Significance
**Global Context:** Globalization and sustainability
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Trade routes connected distant societies, and the exchange of goods and ideas shaped the culture and prosperity of medieval cities.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- *Factual:* What goods, ideas and technologies travelled along medieval trade routes such as the Silk Route?
- *Conceptual:* How does trade create interdependence and cultural exchange between distant societies?
- *Debatable:* Was medieval Baghdad's greatness the result of trade, or of ideas and learning?

## Chapter 2.1 — Trade Routes

**Factual grounding.** Medieval **trade routes** were networks (overland and maritime) linking Asia, Africa and Europe. They moved not just goods but **ideas, religions, technologies and diseases**. Major networks included the overland **Silk Route**, the **Indian Ocean maritime trade** (spices, textiles, horses), and the **Trans-Saharan** gold–salt trade. Trade fostered **interdependence**: societies specialised in what they produced best and relied on others for the rest.

**Significance.** Trade routes were the "internet" of the medieval world — the main channel for cross-cultural exchange, urban wealth and the spread of religions (Buddhism, Islam) and technologies (paper, the compass).

**Perspective/interpretation note.** A merchant saw opportunity and profit; a ruler saw taxable wealth and strategic control; a religious traveller saw a path to spread faith. The same route meant different things to different people.

**Misconception:** *"Trade routes only carried luxury goods like silk and spices."*
- *Why believed:* The famous items (silk, spices) dominate the story.
- *Why wrong:* They also carried **ideas, religions, inventions, art styles and diseases** — arguably a bigger long-term impact than the goods.
- *Corrective:* Trade routes were channels of cultural and biological exchange, not just commerce.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **Name** two major medieval trade networks.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how trade created interdependence between two regions.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** were ideas more important than goods in the exchange along medieval trade routes?

## Chapter 2.2 — The Silk Route

**Factual grounding.** The **Silk Route (Silk Road)** was a web of overland routes (flourishing especially c. 130 BCE–1450s CE) linking **China** with **Central Asia, India, Persia, the Arab world and the Mediterranean/Europe**. Named for Chinese **silk**, it also carried **paper, gunpowder, porcelain, spices, gems, horses and glassware**, plus **Buddhism, Islam, art and technologies**. Oasis cities like **Samarkand** and trading hubs prospered as waystations. It also transmitted disease — most infamously the **Black Death** in the 14th century (peaking in Europe **1347–1351**).

**Significance.** The Silk Route is the classic example of long-distance exchange shaping multiple civilisations at once; it explains how inventions (paper-making, the compass) and religions spread across Eurasia.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** "Silk Road" is a modern term (coined by geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen in 1877), not a name medieval travellers used — a reminder that historians impose labels on the past.

**Misconception:** *"The Silk Road was a single paved road that one merchant travelled end to end."*
- *Why believed:* The singular name "road" implies one route.
- *Why wrong:* It was a **shifting network of many routes**; goods usually passed through **many middlemen** in relay, rather than one trader going the whole way.
- *Corrective:* It was a network of relays, not a single highway.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **List** three goods and one idea/religion carried on the Silk Route.
2. *(Apply)* **Describe** how the Silk Route helped spread both technology and disease.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** the claim that the Silk Route's greatest impact was cultural, not economic.

## Chapter 2.3 — Medieval Baghdad

**Factual grounding.** Founded in **762 CE** by the Abbasid caliph **al-Mansur** as the round "City of Peace," **Baghdad** became the capital of the **Abbasid Caliphate** and, by the 9th–10th centuries, one of the world's largest, richest cities and the centre of the **Islamic Golden Age**. Its **House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma)** was a hub of translation and scholarship where Greek, Persian and Indian works were translated into Arabic and advanced. Scholars like **al-Khwarizmi** (algebra; the words *algorithm* and *algebra* derive from his work) and physicians and astronomers flourished. Baghdad thrived on trade, paper-making (learned from China) and learning — until it was **sacked by the Mongols under Hulagu Khan in 1258 CE**, a devastating blow to the city.

**Significance.** Baghdad shows how a trade-and-learning hub could preserve and advance human knowledge (mathematics, medicine, astronomy) that later fed into the European Renaissance.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** For Abbasid subjects Baghdad symbolised a golden age of faith and learning; the 1258 Mongol account frames the sack very differently. Sources on Baghdad's glory are often later and celebratory, requiring OPVL scrutiny.

**Misconception:** *"During the 'Dark Ages' the whole world stagnated intellectually."*
- *Why believed:* "Dark Ages" is a Eurocentric label applied to early medieval Europe and wrongly generalised.
- *Why wrong:* While parts of Western Europe were less centralised, **Baghdad, the Islamic world, Song China, and the Cholas** were experiencing golden ages of science, trade and art.
- *Corrective:* The "Dark Ages" is a misleading term; much of the medieval world was vibrant and innovative.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **State** who founded Baghdad and in what year, and name its famous centre of learning.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how Baghdad's House of Wisdom contributed to mathematics and science.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** was Baghdad's greatness due to trade rather than to scholarship?

## Chapter 2.4 — Life in Another Medieval City (Comparative Study)

**Factual grounding.** Students compare Baghdad with **another contemporary city** to see shared and distinct features of medieval urban life. Strong options include **Constantinople** (Byzantine capital, huge trade emporium, Hagia Sophia), **Córdoba** (capital of Al-Andalus, famed for libraries and its Great Mosque, a major centre of learning in 10th-century Europe), **Hangzhou/Kaifeng** (wealthy Song-China cities with markets and paper money), **Timbuktu** (West African trade and Islamic learning hub), or **Vijayanagara/Hampi** (a bustling South Indian imperial capital). Across these, medieval cities shared: **markets and trade**, **religious institutions**, **craft workers and guilds**, **walls and gates**, **social hierarchy**, and **centres of learning**.

**Significance.** Comparing cities builds the concept of **interdependence** and shows that urban prosperity everywhere depended on trade, governance and cultural exchange — not on one region alone.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Life in a medieval city differed sharply by class and gender — a wealthy merchant, an artisan, a servant and a scholar experienced the same city very differently.

**Misconception:** *"Medieval cities were tiny, dirty and unimportant compared to modern cities."*
- *Why believed:* Modern bias assumes the past was primitive.
- *Why wrong:* Cities like Baghdad, Constantinople and Córdoba had **hundreds of thousands of inhabitants**, running water/bathhouses, libraries, hospitals and thriving markets.
- *Corrective:* Major medieval cities were large, sophisticated centres of trade and learning.

# UNIT 3: Geographical Explorations and Their Impact

**Key Concept:** Global interactions
**Related Concepts:** Innovation and revolution, Causality, Perspective, Significance
**Global Context:** Scientific and technical innovation
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Motivated by faith, wealth and glory, and enabled by new technologies, exploration connected the world and transformed societies through exchange — for better and for worse.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- *Factual:* Which technologies and explorers made long-distance ocean voyages possible?
- *Conceptual:* How did motivation and innovation combine to drive the Age of Exploration?
- *Debatable:* Was the Columbian Exchange more beneficial or more harmful overall?

## Chapter 3.1 — The Age of Exploration and Motivations: The 3 Gs

**Factual grounding.** The **Age of Exploration** (roughly the 15th–17th centuries) was the European push to find new sea routes to Asia and to explore, trade and colonise. Historians summarise the motivations as the **3 Gs**:
- **God** — spreading Christianity to new lands.
- **Glory** — national prestige, competition between kingdoms (Portugal, Spain), and personal fame.
- **Gold** — wealth: direct access to Asian spices, silk and gold, bypassing costly overland/Middle-Eastern middlemen (especially after the Ottomans controlled key routes).
Portugal and Spain led early, backed by rulers such as Portugal's **Prince Henry the Navigator** (who promoted exploration in the early–mid 1400s).

**Significance.** The 3 Gs explain **causality** — why Europeans risked dangerous voyages. Understanding motivation links directly to consequences (colonisation, the Columbian Exchange, globalisation).

**Perspective/interpretation note.** "Exploration" and "discovery" are European-centred terms; the peoples of the Americas, Africa and Asia already lived in these lands. From their perspective, this was invasion or contact, not discovery.

**Misconception:** *"Explorers set sail mainly out of scientific curiosity to see the world."*
- *Why believed:* Modern admiration for explorers frames them as pure adventurers/scientists.
- *Why wrong:* The core drivers were **economic (Gold) and religious/political (God, Glory)**; curiosity was secondary and usually funded by profit-seeking monarchs and merchants.
- *Corrective:* Exploration was driven mainly by wealth, faith and national rivalry.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **State** what each of the 3 Gs stands for.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how the Ottoman control of trade routes encouraged Europeans to seek sea routes to Asia.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** was "Gold" the most important of the 3 Gs?

## Chapter 3.2 — Early Explorers

**Factual grounding.** Key figures who opened new routes and knowledge:
- **Marco Polo (1254–1324):** Venetian merchant-traveller whose account of his journey to **Kublai Khan's China** along the Silk Route (published c. 1300) fed European fascination with Asia's wealth. (He predates the sea-voyage Age of Exploration but inspired it.)
- **Zheng He (1371–1433):** Chinese Ming-dynasty admiral who led **seven massive treasure-fleet voyages (1405–1433)** across the Indian Ocean to Southeast Asia, India, Arabia and East Africa — decades before the Portuguese, with far larger ships.
- **Bartolomeu Dias (c. 1450–1500):** Portuguese explorer who in **1488** became the first European to round the southern tip of Africa (the **Cape of Good Hope**), proving a sea route toward the Indian Ocean was possible.
- **Vasco da Gama (c. 1460s–1524):** Portuguese explorer who **reached Calicut (Kozhikode), India, by sea in 1498**, opening the direct ocean route from Europe to India around Africa.

**Significance.** These voyages connected previously separate worlds, launched European maritime empires and the global spice/trade economy, and showed that non-European powers (Ming China) were world-leading navigators.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Zheng He's voyages were largely diplomatic/tributary, not colonising; the Portuguese voyages were about seizing trade and territory. Comparing them challenges a Europe-only view of exploration.

**Misconception:** *"Europeans were the first and only advanced ocean explorers."*
- *Why believed:* Western textbooks foreground Columbus and da Gama.
- *Why wrong:* **Zheng He's Chinese fleets (from 1405)** sailed vast distances in enormous ships decades before da Gama's 1498 voyage; Arab, Indian and Polynesian navigators had long crossed oceans.
- *Corrective:* Ocean exploration was a global phenomenon; Europeans were not first, only later dominant.

## Chapter 3.3 — Technologies of Exploration

**Factual grounding.** Long ocean voyages depended on navigation and ship technology, much of it adapted from Chinese, Arab and Mediterranean know-how:
- **Magnetic compass** — a magnetised needle showing direction (north), invented in **China** and transmitted westward; let sailors navigate out of sight of land and in poor weather.
- **Lateen sail** — a triangular sail (from the Arab/Mediterranean world) that let ships sail **closer into the wind** and tack, giving much greater manoeuvrability than square sails alone.
- **Sextant** — an instrument to measure the angle of the sun/stars above the horizon to find **latitude** (the earlier **astrolabe/quadrant** did this; the sextant itself was perfected in the **1730s**, slightly after the peak Age of Exploration but the key latitude-finding tools were in use earlier).
- **Dead reckoning** — estimating current position from a known starting point using **speed, heading and time elapsed** (no instruments for position); essential but error-prone.
- **The caravel** — a small, fast, highly manoeuvrable Portuguese ship developed in the 15th century, combining square and **lateen** sails, with a shallow draft ideal for coastal exploration; central to Portuguese voyages.

**Significance.** These innovations made the Age of Exploration possible — a clear example of the related concept **innovation and revolution**: without them, transoceanic voyages could not succeed.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Much "European" navigation technology was **borrowed and combined** from Chinese and Arab inventions, complicating any story of purely European genius.

**Misconception:** *"Europeans invented all the navigation technology that powered exploration."*
- *Why believed:* Exploration is told as a European triumph.
- *Why wrong:* The **compass came from China**; the **lateen sail** from the Arab/Mediterranean world; the astrolabe was refined by Muslim scholars. Europeans **combined and improved** existing technologies.
- *Corrective:* Exploration technology was a global inheritance, adapted by Europeans.

|---|---|---|---|
| Magnetic compass | Shows direction (north) | China | Navigate out of sight of land / in fog |
| Lateen sail | Triangular sail; sail into the wind | Arab/Mediterranean | Manoeuvrability; tacking against wind |
| Sextant (& astrolabe/quadrant) | Measures sun/star angle → latitude | Muslim scholars refined astrolabe; sextant perfected 1730s | Find how far north/south you are |
| Dead reckoning | Estimate position from speed, heading, time | Long-standing method | Best-guess navigation without instruments |

## Chapter 3.4 — The Columbian Exchange

**Factual grounding.** The **Columbian Exchange** is the vast transfer of **plants, animals, people, ideas, technology and diseases** between the **Old World (Europe, Africa, Asia)** and the **New World (the Americas)** after **Christopher Columbus's 1492 voyage**.
- **From the Americas to the Old World:** maize (corn), potatoes, tomatoes, cacao (chocolate), tobacco, chilli peppers, beans, vanilla, turkey. (Potatoes and maize boosted populations in Europe, Asia and Africa.)
- **From the Old World to the Americas:** wheat, rice, sugar, horses, cattle, pigs, sheep — and, catastrophically, **diseases** such as **smallpox, measles and influenza**.
- **Human cost:** Old World diseases killed a huge share of Indigenous American populations (who had no immunity) — many estimates suggest up to around 90% mortality in some regions over the following century. It also drove the **transatlantic slave trade**, forcibly moving millions of Africans to the Americas.

**Significance ("so what").** The Columbian Exchange reshaped **global diets, populations, economies and ecosystems** and truly began the interconnected modern world (globalisation). Its impact was both transformative and, for Indigenous peoples, devastating.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** From a European view it brought new foods, wealth and empire; from an Indigenous American view it meant death, conquest and cultural destruction; for Africans it meant enslavement. A single event, radically different meanings.

**Misconception:** *"The Columbian Exchange was mostly a positive sharing of new foods."*
- *Why believed:* We enjoy the foods (potatoes, tomatoes, chocolate) it spread.
- *Why wrong:* Its deadliest effect was **disease**, which killed a large fraction of Indigenous Americans, alongside conquest and the slave trade.
- *Corrective:* The exchange had enormous benefits and catastrophic harms; judging it requires weighing both.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **List** two foods that went from the Americas to Europe and two things that went from Europe to the Americas.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** why Old World diseases had such a devastating effect on Indigenous American populations.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** did the benefits of the Columbian Exchange outweigh its harms?

# UNIT 4: Renaissance and the Rise of Scientific Thought

**Key Concept:** Change
**Related Concepts:** Innovation and revolution, Perspective, Significance
**Global Context:** Personal and cultural expression / Scientific and technical innovation
**Statement of Inquiry:** *A revival of learning and a new emphasis on human reason revolutionised art and science, changing how people understood the world and their place in it.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- *Factual:* What were the key ideas, figures and breakthroughs of the Renaissance and the Scientific Revolution?
- *Conceptual:* How did humanism change the way people viewed the individual, art and knowledge?
- *Debatable:* Was the Scientific Revolution a sudden break from the past or a gradual evolution of earlier ideas?

## Chapter 4.1 — The Renaissance

**Factual grounding.** The **Renaissance** ("rebirth," roughly **14th–17th centuries**, beginning in **Italy** — especially **Florence** — c. 1300s and spreading across Europe) was a period of renewed interest in the **classical learning of ancient Greece and Rome** and a flourishing of art, literature, science and thought. It was fuelled by: wealthy trading cities and patrons (e.g., the **Medici** of Florence); the fall of Constantinople (1453) sending Greek scholars/texts west; and **Johannes Gutenberg's printing press** (c. **1440s**), which spread ideas cheaply and rapidly.

**Significance.** The Renaissance marks a major **change** in European culture — the bridge from the medieval to the modern world — reshaping art, education, religion and science.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** The term "Renaissance" (a "rebirth") was popularised later (19th century) and implies the Middle Ages were "asleep." Many historians stress **continuity**: medieval and Islamic scholars had preserved and advanced the classical knowledge that the Renaissance built on.

**Misconception:** *"The Renaissance suddenly rediscovered ancient knowledge that had been completely lost."*
- *Why believed:* "Rebirth" implies knowledge was dead and revived from nothing.
- *Why wrong:* Classical texts were **preserved and studied** through the medieval period by **Byzantine and Islamic scholars** (e.g., in Baghdad and Córdoba); the Renaissance **built on** this, aided by the printing press.
- *Corrective:* The Renaissance revived and expanded knowledge that had been kept alive, not resurrected from total loss.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **State** where and roughly when the Renaissance began, and name one factor that fuelled it.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how the printing press helped spread Renaissance ideas.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** was the Renaissance a genuine break from the medieval period?

## Chapter 4.2 — Humanism, Art and Scientific Innovation

**Factual grounding.** **Humanism** was the defining intellectual movement of the Renaissance: it emphasised the **value, potential and achievements of the individual human being**, studied the *humanities* (grammar, rhetoric, history, poetry, philosophy) based on classical texts, and encouraged **observation, reason and secular alongside religious inquiry** (humanists were still mostly religious). In **art**, this produced **realism, perspective (3-D depth on a flat surface), anatomical accuracy, and the celebration of human beauty and emotion** — a clear break from flatter medieval styles. This same spirit of **observation and questioning** fed **scientific innovation**.

**Significance.** Humanism shifted the focus of thought toward the individual and observation — a mindset that underpins both Renaissance art and the later Scientific Revolution. It is a foundational idea of modern Western thought.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Humanism did not reject religion; most humanists were devout Christians who saw studying the human and natural world as compatible with faith. Popular accounts sometimes over-state a "science vs religion" split.

**Misconception:** *"Humanism meant rejecting religion and becoming atheist."*
- *Why believed:* The modern word "humanism" is often linked to secularism/atheism.
- *Why wrong:* Renaissance humanists were **overwhelmingly religious**; humanism valued human potential *and* faith, studying classical and Christian texts together.
- *Corrective:* Renaissance humanism celebrated human reason and dignity *within* a largely Christian worldview.

**Command-term questions:**
1. *(Recall)* **Define** humanism and name one feature of Renaissance art.
2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how the use of perspective in painting reflected humanist values.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **Evaluate** how far humanism was responsible for both the artistic and scientific achievements of the Renaissance.

## Chapter 4.3 — Key Figures of the Renaissance and Scientific Revolution

**Factual grounding.**
- **Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519):** Italian painter, inventor and anatomist — the archetypal "Renaissance man." Works: **Mona Lisa**, **The Last Supper**; thousands of notebook studies of anatomy, machines and nature. Embodies the union of art and science.
- **Michelangelo (1475–1564):** Italian sculptor, painter and architect. Works: statue of **David**, the **Sistine Chapel ceiling** (1508–1512), designs for **St Peter's Basilica**. Master of the human form.
- **Nicolaus Copernicus (1473–1543):** Polish astronomer who proposed the **heliocentric model** — the **Sun**, not the Earth, at the centre of the solar system — in *On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres* (**1543**). This challenged the long-accepted Earth-centred (geocentric) view.
- **Galileo Galilei (1564–1642):** Italian scientist; improved the **telescope** (from 1609) and observed Jupiter's moons, lunar craters and the phases of Venus, providing evidence for the heliocentric model. Championed **observation and experiment**; famously tried by the Inquisition (1633) for supporting Copernicus.
- **Isaac Newton (1643–1727):** English scientist who unified physics with the **laws of motion** and **universal gravitation** in his ***Principia Mathematica* (1687)** — a capstone of the Scientific Revolution.

**Significance.** These figures show the Renaissance/Scientific Revolution's core shift: from accepting authority/tradition to trusting **observation, evidence and reason**. Their work still underpins modern art and science.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Galileo's conflict is often told as "science vs. the Church," but it also involved politics, personality and how evidence was interpreted at the time — a caution against oversimplified good-vs-evil narratives.

**Misconception:** *"Copernicus/Galileo were the first to ever suggest the Earth moves, and everyone before thought the Earth was flat."*
- *Why believed:* Popular myths about a "flat-Earth Middle Ages."
- *Why wrong:* Educated people had known the Earth was **round** since antiquity (e.g., the ancient Greeks); the ancient thinker **Aristarchus** had even proposed a Sun-centred idea. The debate was **geocentric vs heliocentric**, not flat vs round.
- *Corrective:* The Scientific Revolution changed the model of the *cosmos* (Sun-centred), not a belief in a flat Earth.

## Chapter 4.4 — The Scientific Revolution and Early Scientific Thought

**Factual grounding.** The **Scientific Revolution** (roughly **1543–1687**, from Copernicus's book to Newton's *Principia*) was the transformation in how Europeans understood the natural world. Its heart was the **scientific method**: knowledge based on **observation, hypothesis, experiment and mathematics**, rather than solely on ancient authorities (like Aristotle) or religious doctrine. Thinkers such as **Francis Bacon** (championed inductive, experimental method) and **René Descartes** (mathematical reasoning) shaped this new approach. Breakthroughs spanned astronomy (heliocentrism), physics (motion, gravity), and biology/medicine (e.g., **William Harvey's** description of blood circulation, 1628).

**Significance.** The Scientific Revolution created **modern science** and the evidence-based way of thinking that drives technology and inquiry today — a genuine **revolution** in knowledge, closely tied to the Renaissance emphasis on reason and observation.

**Perspective/interpretation note.** Historians debate whether it was a sudden "**revolution**" or a gradual **evolution** building on medieval Islamic and European scholarship (e.g., Ibn al-Haytham's optics and emphasis on experiment centuries earlier). The dramatic term "revolution" is itself an interpretation.

**Misconception:** *"Before the Scientific Revolution, no one did any real science or experiments."*
- *Why believed:* The word "revolution" implies science began from nothing.
- *Why wrong:* **Ancient Greek, Islamic (e.g., Ibn al-Haytham, al-Khwarizmi) and medieval scholars** made major advances in mathematics, astronomy, optics and medicine; the Scientific Revolution **built on** them.
- *Corrective:* The Scientific Revolution transformed and systematised science; it did not invent inquiry from scratch.

**Command-term questions:**

2. *(Apply)* **Explain** how the scientific method differed from relying on ancient authorities.
3. *(Analyse/Evaluate)* **To what extent** was the Scientific Revolution a sudden break rather than a gradual evolution of earlier knowledge?

# SUBJECT-WIDE: Common Misconceptions Master List

1. **Primary sources are always more reliable than secondary sources.** — Reliability depends on OPVL, not the label; primaries can be biased/propaganda.
2. **A source's "limitation" means it is a bad source.** — Every source has limitations; identifying them is normal analysis, not condemnation.
3. **Empires are built mainly by military conquest.** — Administration, trade, law and ideology sustain empires; conquest alone rarely lasts.
4. **The Roman Empire fell suddenly in 476 CE.** — Only the *Western* empire ended (gradually); the *Eastern/Byzantine* empire lasted until 1453.
5. **The "Middle Ages / Dark Ages" were a time of total stagnation everywhere.** — Baghdad, Córdoba, Song China and the Cholas had golden ages of learning and trade.
6. **Feudalism was a single, planned, uniform system.** — It was a loose, regionally varied set of arrangements; the neat pyramid is a later simplification.
7. **The Silk Road was one paved road travelled end-to-end by single merchants.** — It was a shifting network of relays and middlemen.
8. **Trade routes only moved luxury goods.** — They also carried ideas, religions, technologies and diseases.
9. **Europeans were the first/only great ocean explorers.** — Zheng He's Chinese fleets (from 1405), plus Arab, Indian and Polynesian navigators, came earlier.
10. **Europeans invented all navigation technology.** — The compass (China) and lateen sail (Arab/Mediterranean) were borrowed and improved.
11. **Explorers sailed mainly out of scientific curiosity.** — Core motives were the 3 Gs: God, Glory and Gold.
12. **The Columbian Exchange was mostly a happy sharing of foods.** — Its deadliest effect was disease that devastated Indigenous Americans, plus the slave trade.
13. **The Renaissance rediscovered knowledge that had been completely lost.** — Byzantine and Islamic scholars had preserved it; the Renaissance built on that.
14. **Humanism meant rejecting religion/atheism.** — Renaissance humanists were mostly devout; humanism valued human reason *and* faith.
15. **People before Copernicus thought the Earth was flat.** — Educated people knew it was round; the real shift was geocentric → heliocentric.
16. **No real science existed before the Scientific Revolution.** — Greek, Islamic and medieval scholars made major advances first.
