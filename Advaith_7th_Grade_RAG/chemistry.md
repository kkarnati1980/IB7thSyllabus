# Grade 7 Chemistry — IISH MYP Knowledge Base (2026-2027)

## Subject Overview

Chemistry in Grade 7 sits within the **MYP Sciences** subject group. Sciences in the MYP is inquiry-based: students build conceptual understanding, investigate scientific questions, process and evaluate data, and reflect on how science shapes and is shaped by the world.

### The Four MYP Sciences Assessment Criteria

- **Criterion A — Knowing and understanding**: Recall, explain and apply scientific knowledge; analyse and evaluate information to make scientifically supported judgements.
- **Criterion B — Inquiring and designing**: Formulate a testable hypothesis, explain it using scientific reasoning; design a controlled method identifying variables (independent, dependent, controlled).
- **Criterion C — Processing and evaluating**: Present, organise and interpret data (tables, graphs); draw conclusions; evaluate the validity of the method and results, and suggest improvements.
- **Criterion D — Reflecting on the impacts of science**: Explain how science is applied to address a problem or issue; discuss the implications of using science and its interaction with moral, ethical, social, economic, political, cultural or environmental factors.

Each criterion is marked out of 8 using MYP rubrics.

### Sciences Related Concepts

These recur across units: **Balance, Conditions, Consequences, Development, Energy, Environment, Evidence, Form, Function, Interaction, Models, Movement, Patterns, Transfer, Transformation.**

### Key Concepts used in Sciences

The MYP Key Concepts most used in Sciences are **Change, Relationships, Systems**, though any of the sixteen may be drawn on.

# Unit 1: Matter and its Composition

- **Key Concept**: Change
- **Related Concepts**: Form, Patterns, Evidence
- **Global Context**: Scientific and technical innovation (how humans separate, purify and use materials)
- **Statement of Inquiry**: The form and composition of matter determine how its parts can be identified and separated by physical and chemical means.
- **Inquiry Questions**:
  - *Factual*: What are elements, compounds and mixtures, and how do we separate mixtures?
  - *Conceptual*: How does the way particles are combined change the properties of a substance?
  - *Debatable*: Is any separated substance ever truly "pure"?

## Chapter 1.1 — Atoms, Elements and Compounds

### Concept: The Atom
- **Definition**: An atom is the smallest particle of an element that still has the properties of that element. It has a central **nucleus** (protons + neutrons) surrounded by **electrons**.
- **Real-world example**: A single copper atom is the smallest unit of copper in a copper wire.
- **Analogy**: An atom is like a tiny solar system — the dense nucleus is the "sun" and electrons orbit like "planets" (a simplified model).
- **Why it matters**: Everything is made of atoms; understanding them explains all chemical behaviour.
- **Subatomic particles**: protons (positive, in nucleus), neutrons (neutral, in nucleus), electrons (negative, around nucleus).
- **Misconception**: *"Atoms are solid balls with no empty space."* Believed because diagrams show shaded circles. Wrong because an atom is mostly empty space; the nucleus is tiny compared with the electron cloud. Corrective: if the atom were a stadium, the nucleus would be a pea at the centre.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the three subatomic particles found in an atom.
  2. (Apply) **Label** the nucleus and electrons on a diagram of a helium atom.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why an atom is described as electrically neutral overall.

### Concept: Elements
- **Definition**: An element is a pure substance made of only one type of atom; it cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means.
- **Real-world example**: Oxygen, gold, carbon.
- **Analogy**: An element is like a single Lego brick of one shape and colour.
- **Why it matters**: Elements are the building blocks of every compound and mixture.
- **Misconception**: *"Air is an element."* Wrong — air is a mixture of several elements and compounds (N₂, O₂, CO₂, argon, water vapour). Corrective: an element contains only one kind of atom.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Define** the term element.
  2. (Apply) **Identify** two elements from this list: water, iron, salt, helium.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Distinguish** between an element and a compound at the atomic level.

### Concept: Compounds
- **Definition**: A compound is a pure substance formed when two or more different elements are chemically bonded in a fixed ratio.
- **Real-world example**: Water (H₂O) is hydrogen chemically bonded to oxygen; table salt (NaCl) is sodium bonded to chlorine.
- **Analogy**: A compound is like a baked cake — you cannot pick the flour back out because it has chemically changed.
- **Why it matters**: Most substances around us are compounds, not free elements.
- **Elements vs compounds at the atomic/molecular level**: an element's smallest particle contains identical atoms; a compound's smallest particle (a molecule or formula unit) contains different atoms bonded together in a set ratio.
- **Misconception**: *"A compound is just a mixture of its elements."* Wrong — in a compound the elements are chemically joined and lose their own properties; a mixture keeps them separate. Corrective: sodium (explosive metal) + chlorine (poison gas) → sodium chloride (edible salt).
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the ratio of hydrogen to oxygen atoms in water.
  2. (Apply) **Classify** CO₂, He and NaCl as element or compound.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain**, using sodium chloride, how a compound's properties differ from those of its constituent elements.

## Chapter 1.2 — Mixtures and Compounds

### Concept: Mixtures
- **Definition**: A mixture contains two or more substances physically combined but **not** chemically bonded; each keeps its own properties and can be present in any ratio.
- **Real-world example**: Sand and salt; air; sea water; a fruit salad.
- **Analogy**: A mixture is like a bag of mixed sweets — you can sort them back out one by one.
- **Why it matters**: Most raw materials in nature (crude oil, ores, air, sea water) are mixtures that must be separated.
- **Misconception**: *"Mixtures always have a fixed recipe."* Wrong — mixtures can be combined in any proportion; only compounds have a fixed ratio. Corrective: you can make salty water strong or weak; water is always exactly H₂O.

### Concept: Compound vs Mixture Comparison
- Compound: elements chemically bonded; fixed ratio; new properties; separated only by chemical means; energy change on forming.
- Mixture: substances physically combined; any ratio; keep original properties; separated by physical means; little or no energy change on forming.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** one difference between a compound and a mixture.
  2. (Apply) **Classify** each as element, compound or mixture: brass, oxygen, carbon dioxide, muddy water.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Compare** how a compound and a mixture can be separated.

### Concept: Classifying Substances
- **Definition**: Sorting a substance as element, compound or mixture using the number of atom types and whether they are bonded.
- **Worked example**: 
  - Diamond → one atom type (carbon), bonded → **element**.
  - Sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) → several atom types, chemically bonded, fixed ratio → **compound**.
  - Tea → several substances, physically combined → **mixture**.

### Concept: Separation Techniques
Physical methods separate the parts of a **mixture** without changing them chemically.

| Technique | Separates | Principle |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Filtration | Insoluble solid from a liquid | Solid particles too big to pass through filter paper |
| Evaporation | Dissolved solid from solution | Liquid turns to vapour, solid left behind |
| Crystallization | Pure solid crystals from solution | Solubility falls as solution cools; crystals form |
| Distillation (simple) | Pure liquid (solvent) from solution | Liquid boils, vapour is cooled/condensed back to liquid |
| Fractional distillation | Two or more miscible liquids | Different boiling points; fractions collected at different temperatures |
| Sublimation | Solid that turns straight to gas (e.g. iodine, NH₄Cl) from a non-subliming solid | One solid sublimes on heating and re-solidifies elsewhere |
| Separating funnel | Two immiscible liquids (oil + water) | Different densities; lower layer run off through the tap |
| Chromatography | Coloured/soluble components of a solution | Different substances travel different distances with the solvent |

- **Real-world example**: A water treatment plant filters, then evaporates/distills to purify water; oil refineries use fractional distillation.
- **Analogy**: Separation is like sorting laundry — a sieve (filtration), a clothes line drying (evaporation), or sorting by weight.
- **Physical vs chemical separation**: Physical separation (all the above) uses differences in physical properties (size, boiling point, solubility, density) and gives back the original substances unchanged. **Chemical separation** breaks bonds in a compound (e.g. electrolysis of water into H₂ and O₂) and produces new substances.
- **Misconception**: *"Distillation removes all impurities from water forever."* Wrong — distillation separates dissolved solids but volatile substances with similar boiling points can carry over. Corrective: distillation separates by boiling point, not by "cleanliness".
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the technique used to separate sand from water.
  2. (Apply) **Outline** how you would obtain pure water from salty water.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Compare** physical and chemical methods of separation.

#### Practical Note — Separating a Salt/Sand Mixture
- **Method**: Add water, stir (salt dissolves). Filter → sand stays on filter paper. Evaporate the filtrate → salt crystals remain.
- **Variables**: Independent = amount of water added; Dependent = mass of salt recovered; Controlled = mass of starting mixture, temperature of evaporation.
- **Expected result**: Sand recovered on filter paper; white salt crystals from evaporation; combined mass ≈ starting mass (conservation of mass).

## Chapter 1.3 — Paper Chromatography

### Concept: Paper Chromatography
- **Definition**: A technique that separates the dissolved components of a mixture based on how far each travels up a paper as a solvent moves through it.
- **Components**: 
  - **Stationary phase** — the chromatography paper.
  - **Mobile phase** — the solvent (water, ethanol) that moves up the paper.
  - **Baseline** — pencil line where the sample spot is placed.
  - **Solvent front** — the furthest point the solvent reaches.
- **Real-world example**: Testing which dyes make up a black felt-tip ink; forensic analysis of inks; checking food colourings.
- **Analogy**: A race — lighter, more soluble components "run" faster and further up the paper than heavier, less soluble ones.
- **Why it matters**: It identifies unknown substances and checks purity using tiny samples.

### Concept: Rf Value
- **Definition**: The retention factor, a ratio comparing how far a component moves to how far the solvent moves. It is a fixed value for a substance in a given solvent.
- **Formula**: 
```
Rf = distance moved by substance / distance moved by solvent
```
- **Worked example**: A spot moves 4.0 cm; the solvent front moves 8.0 cm. 
```
Rf = 4.0 / 8.0 = 0.5
```
Rf has no units and is always between 0 and 1. An unknown with the same Rf as a known reference (run in the same conditions) is likely the same substance.
- **Misconception**: *"Rf can be greater than 1."* Wrong — a component can never travel further than the solvent front, so Rf ≤ 1. Corrective: if you calculate Rf > 1 you have swapped the numbers.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the formula for Rf value.
  2. (Apply) **Calculate** the Rf of a pigment that moves 3 cm when the solvent moves 12 cm.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how Rf values can be used to identify an unknown pigment in a mixture.

#### Practical Note — Paper Chromatography of Ink
- **Method**: Draw a **pencil** baseline 2 cm from the bottom of the paper. Place a small spot of ink on the line. Stand the paper in a beaker with solvent below the baseline. Let the solvent rise, remove before it reaches the top, mark the solvent front, let dry.
- **Variables**: Independent = the ink sample; Dependent = distance each colour travels (Rf); Controlled = solvent, paper type, temperature, spot size.
- **Expected result**: The single spot separates into several coloured spots; each has a characteristic Rf.
- **Why pencil?** Pencil (graphite) is insoluble and does not run in the solvent; pen ink would separate too.

## Chapter 1.4 — Application: Designing Separations

### Concept: Choosing and Evaluating a Separation Process
- **Definition**: Selecting the best sequence of techniques to separate a given mixture based on the properties of its components, then judging how well it worked.
- **Real-world example**: To separate a mixture of sand, salt and iron filings: use a **magnet** (removes iron), add water and **filter** (removes sand), then **evaporate** (recovers salt).
- **Analogy**: Like planning a route with several stops in the right order.
- **Why it matters**: Real industries (mining, water treatment, pharmaceuticals) chain techniques to purify products.
- **Analysing effectiveness**: judge by purity of product, percentage recovered, time and energy used, and safety.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **List** three properties used to choose a separation technique.
  2. (Apply) **Design** a process to separate a mixture of iron filings, sand and salt.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Evaluate** the effectiveness of your process, suggesting one improvement.

# Unit 2: Ambiguous Particles

- **Key Concept**: Change
- **Related Concepts**: Models, Evidence, Development
- **Global Context**: Orientation in space and time (how scientific ideas of the atom developed through history)
- **Statement of Inquiry**: Scientific models of the atom develop as new evidence changes how we understand the particles that make up matter.
- **Inquiry Questions**:
  - *Factual*: What particles make up an atom and how are they counted?
  - *Conceptual*: How does new evidence change a scientific model?
  - *Debatable*: If models keep changing, can we ever say we "know" what an atom looks like?

## Chapter 2.1 — Composition of Atoms

### Concept: Nucleus and Electrons
- **Definition**: The nucleus is the dense central core holding protons and neutrons; electrons occupy shells (energy levels) around it.
- **Charge and mass table**:

| Particle | Charge | Relative mass | Location |
|----------|--------|---------------|----------|
| Proton | +1 | 1 | Nucleus |
| Neutron | 0 | 1 | Nucleus |
| Electron | −1 | ~1/1836 (≈0) | Shells around nucleus |

- **How particles contribute**: Almost all **mass** comes from protons and neutrons in the nucleus. The **charge** balance comes from equal numbers of protons (+) and electrons (−) in a neutral atom.
- **Analogy**: The nucleus is a heavy bowling ball; electrons are like buzzing flies whose weight barely counts.

### Concept: Nuclide Notation, Atomic Number and Mass Number
- **Definition**: 
  - **Atomic number (Z)** = number of protons (defines the element).
  - **Mass number (A)** = number of protons + neutrons.
  - **Nuclide notation**: written as ᴬZ X (mass number top-left, atomic number bottom-left of the symbol), e.g. carbon-12 is ¹²₆C.
- **Calculating p, n, e**:
  - protons = Z
  - electrons = Z (in a neutral atom)
  - neutrons = A − Z
- **Worked example**: For ²³₁₁Na (sodium): protons = 11, electrons = 11, neutrons = 23 − 11 = 12.
- **Real-world example**: Every element on the periodic table is defined by its atomic number (its proton count).
- **Misconception**: *"Mass number and atomic mass are the same thing."* Wrong — mass number is a whole-number count of protons + neutrons for one atom; relative atomic mass is an average over isotopes and is usually not a whole number. Corrective: chlorine's mass number for one atom is 35 or 37, but its relative atomic mass is 35.5.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Define** atomic number and mass number.
  2. (Apply) **Calculate** the protons, neutrons and electrons in ²⁷₁₃Al.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how the subatomic particles determine an atom's mass and overall charge.

## Chapter 2.2 — Atomic Models Timeline

### Concept: The Development of Atomic Models

| Model | Scientist | Approx. date | Key feature | Evidence / experiment | Improvement over previous |
|-------|-----------|--------------|-------------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Solid sphere | John Dalton | 1803 | Atoms are tiny, indivisible solid spheres; each element has its own atoms | Laws of chemical combination | First scientific atomic theory |
| Plum pudding | J.J. Thomson | 1897 | A ball of positive charge with negative electrons embedded in it | Discovery of the electron (cathode rays) | Showed atoms contain smaller charged particles |
| Nuclear model | Ernest Rutherford | 1911 | Small, dense, positive nucleus with electrons around it; mostly empty space | Gold foil (alpha scattering) experiment | Replaced pudding with a concentrated nucleus |
| Planetary/shells | Niels Bohr | 1913 | Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed shells (energy levels) | Line spectra of hydrogen | Explained why electrons don't spiral into the nucleus |

- **Analogy**: The models are like successive drafts of a drawing — each keeps the useful parts and corrects a flaw revealed by new evidence.
- **Why it matters**: Shows science as a self-correcting process driven by evidence, not fixed truth.
- **Misconception**: *"Later scientists proved the earlier ones completely wrong."* Wrong — each model kept what worked and refined the rest; Dalton's idea that matter is made of atoms still holds. Corrective: models are improved, not simply discarded.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Outline** the key feature of Thomson's plum pudding model.
  2. (Apply) **Describe** how the gold foil experiment supported Rutherford's model.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how each model improved on the one before it.

## Chapter 2.3 — Isotopes

### Concept: Isotopes
- **Definition**: Isotopes are atoms of the **same element** (same number of protons) with **different numbers of neutrons**, and therefore different mass numbers.
- **Real-world example**: Carbon-12 and carbon-14 are both carbon (6 protons) but have 6 and 8 neutrons respectively.
- **Analogy**: Isotopes are like siblings in a family — same surname (element), different weights.
- **Why it matters**: Isotopes have identical chemistry but different masses and some are radioactive, which enables dating and medicine.
- **Worked example**: 
  - ³⁵₁₇Cl → 17 protons, 18 neutrons.
  - ³⁷₁₇Cl → 17 protons, 20 neutrons.
  Same protons (both chlorine), different neutrons → isotopes.
- **Misconception**: *"Isotopes are different elements because they have different masses."* Wrong — element identity depends only on proton number, which is unchanged. Corrective: change the neutrons and you get an isotope; change the protons and you get a new element.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Define** the term isotope.
  2. (Apply) **Calculate** the number of neutrons in ¹⁴₆C and ¹²₆C.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why isotopes of the same element behave identically in chemical reactions.

## Chapter 2.4 — Applications of Isotopes

### Concept: Uses of Radioactive Isotopes
- **Definition**: Radioactive (unstable) isotopes emit radiation as they decay; this radiation is put to practical use.
- **Uses**:
  - **Medicine**: cobalt-60 and other sources for **radiation therapy** to treat cancer; technetium-99m and iodine-131 for medical **imaging** and thyroid diagnosis/treatment.
  - **Carbon-14 dating**: living things absorb carbon-14; after death it decays at a known rate, so the amount left tells the age of ancient organic remains (archaeology).
  - **Industrial/research**: tracing leaks in pipelines, checking weld thickness/quality, sterilising equipment, and as tracers in research.
- **Real-world example**: A radiographer uses a technetium tracer to image blood flow in a patient's heart.
- **Analogy**: Carbon-14 dating is like a battery that slowly drains at a steady rate — measure the charge left to know how long ago it was "full".
- **Why it matters**: Isotopes save lives (diagnosis, treatment) and let us read Earth's and humanity's history.
- **Misconception**: *"All isotopes are dangerous/radioactive."* Wrong — most isotopes are stable and harmless (e.g. carbon-12); only some are radioactive. Corrective: radioactivity depends on nuclear stability, not on being an isotope.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** one medical use of a radioactive isotope.
  2. (Apply) **Describe** how carbon-14 is used to date ancient remains.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Discuss** one benefit and one risk of using radioactive isotopes in medicine.

# Unit 3: The Periodic Table

- **Key Concept**: Relationships
- **Related Concepts**: Patterns, Function, Development
- **Global Context**: Scientific and technical innovation (organising and predicting the behaviour of elements)
- **Statement of Inquiry**: Organising elements by their atomic structure reveals patterns that let us predict the properties and behaviour of matter.
- **Inquiry Questions**:
  - *Factual*: How are elements arranged in the periodic table?
  - *Conceptual*: How does an element's position relate to its properties?
  - *Debatable*: Is the periodic table a human invention or a discovery of nature's order?

## Chapter 3.1 — History of the Periodic Table

### Concept: Mendeleev and the Modern Table
- **Definition**: Dmitri Mendeleev (1869) arranged the known elements by increasing **atomic mass** into rows and columns so that elements with similar properties lined up; he left **gaps** and predicted undiscovered elements.
- **How atomic number led to the modern table**: Later, Henry Moseley showed elements are best ordered by **atomic number** (proton count), which fixed the few places where mass order gave the wrong sequence. The modern table is arranged by atomic number.
- **Real-world example**: Mendeleev predicted the properties of "eka-silicon" — later discovered as germanium — matching his forecast.
- **Analogy**: Mendeleev built the table like a crossword with blank squares he was confident would be filled in.
- **Misconception**: *"Mendeleev arranged elements by atomic number."* Wrong — he used atomic mass; atomic number came later with Moseley. Corrective: the switch to atomic number solved ordering problems (e.g. tellurium/iodine).
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the property Mendeleev used to order the elements.
  2. (Apply) **Explain** why Mendeleev left gaps in his table.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how ordering by atomic number improved the periodic table.

## Chapter 3.2 — Arrangement of Elements

### Concept: Groups, Periods and Atomic Number Order
- **Definition**: Elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number. **Groups** are the vertical columns (elements in a group have similar properties and the same number of valence electrons); **Periods** are the horizontal rows.
- **Position vs electronic arrangement**: the **period number** = number of occupied electron shells; the **group number** (for main-group elements) = number of outer (valence) electrons.
- **Symbols of the first 20 elements**:

| Z | Element | Symbol | Z | Element | Symbol |
|---|---------|--------|---|---------|--------|
| 1 | Hydrogen | H | 11 | Sodium | Na |
| 2 | Helium | He | 12 | Magnesium | Mg |
| 3 | Lithium | Li | 13 | Aluminium | Al |
| 4 | Beryllium | Be | 14 | Silicon | Si |
| 5 | Boron | B | 15 | Phosphorus | P |
| 6 | Carbon | C | 16 | Sulfur | S |
| 7 | Nitrogen | N | 17 | Chlorine | Cl |
| 8 | Oxygen | O | 18 | Argon | Ar |
| 9 | Fluorine | F | 19 | Potassium | K |
| 10 | Neon | Ne | 20 | Calcium | Ca |

- **Analogy**: The table is like a seating chart — each element has one reserved seat set by its atomic number.
- **Misconception**: *"Elements in the same period behave alike."* Wrong — similar chemistry runs down **groups**, not across periods. Corrective: it's the shared number of valence electrons in a group that gives similar behaviour.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** what is meant by a group and a period.
  2. (Apply) **Give** the symbols for sodium, chlorine and calcium.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why elements in the same group have similar properties.

## Chapter 3.3 — Electronic Arrangement (up to Calcium)

### Concept: Electron Shells and Valence Electrons
- **Definition**: Electrons fill shells around the nucleus. The first shell holds up to 2 electrons, the second up to 8, the third up to 8 (for the first 20 elements). The **valence electrons** are those in the outermost shell.
- **Rule of thumb**: fill 2, then 8, then 8. Write as numbers separated by commas or dots.

| Z | Element | Electronic arrangement | Valence electrons | Group |
|---|---------|------------------------|-------------------|-------|
| 1 | H | 1 | 1 | 1 |
| 2 | He | 2 | 2 | 18 |
| 3 | Li | 2,1 | 1 | 1 |
| 4 | Be | 2,2 | 2 | 2 |
| 5 | B | 2,3 | 3 | 13 |
| 6 | C | 2,4 | 4 | 14 |
| 7 | N | 2,5 | 5 | 15 |
| 8 | O | 2,6 | 6 | 16 |
| 9 | F | 2,7 | 7 | 17 |
| 10 | Ne | 2,8 | 8 | 18 |
| 11 | Na | 2,8,1 | 1 | 1 |
| 12 | Mg | 2,8,2 | 2 | 2 |
| 13 | Al | 2,8,3 | 3 | 13 |
| 14 | Si | 2,8,4 | 4 | 14 |
| 15 | P | 2,8,5 | 5 | 15 |
| 16 | S | 2,8,6 | 6 | 16 |
| 17 | Cl | 2,8,7 | 7 | 17 |
| 18 | Ar | 2,8,8 | 8 | 18 |
| 19 | K | 2,8,8,1 | 1 | 1 |
| 20 | Ca | 2,8,8,2 | 2 | 2 |

- **Relate to group number**: for main-group elements the number of valence electrons = the group number (Groups 1, 2, then 13–18 give 1, 2, 3…8).
- **Analogy**: Shells are like rows of seats in a theatre that fill from the front (nearest the nucleus) outwards.
- **Worked example**: Sulfur (Z = 16): 2 + 8 + 6 → **2,8,6**; 6 valence electrons; Group 16; period 3.
- **Misconception**: *"The third shell can only ever hold 8 electrons."* For the first 20 elements we treat it as 8, but the third shell can actually hold up to 18 later on. Corrective: the "2,8,8" rule is a simplification valid up to calcium.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the maximum number of electrons in the first two shells.
  2. (Apply) **Write** the electronic arrangement of phosphorus and potassium.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how an element's electronic arrangement is linked to its group and period.

## Chapter 3.4 — Group 1: Alkali Metals

### Concept: Alkali Metal Properties
- **Definition**: Group 1 elements (**lithium, sodium, potassium**, rubidium, caesium, francium) are soft, reactive metals with **1 valence electron**.
- **General physical properties**: soft (can be cut with a knife), low density (Li, Na, K float on water), shiny when freshly cut but tarnish quickly, good conductors, relatively low melting points for metals.
- **Real-world example**: Sodium and potassium ions are essential for nerve signals in the body.
- **Analogy**: Alkali metals are like eager party guests — they give away their single outer electron very readily, making them highly reactive.
- **Misconception**: *"Metals are always hard."* Wrong — alkali metals are soft enough to cut with a knife. Corrective: hardness varies; Group 1 metals are notably soft.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Name** three alkali metals.
  2. (Apply) **State** two physical properties of sodium.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why alkali metals are stored under oil.

## Chapter 3.5 — Group 17: Halogens

### Concept: Halogen Properties and Group Comparison
- **Definition**: Group 17 elements (**fluorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine**, astatine) are reactive non-metals with **7 valence electrons**.
- **Physical properties**: coloured, poisonous non-metals; exist as diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂); change state down the group — fluorine (pale yellow gas), chlorine (green gas), bromine (red-brown liquid), iodine (grey-black solid that sublimes).
- **Group 1 vs Group 17 comparison**:

| Feature | Group 1 (Alkali metals) | Group 17 (Halogens) |
|---------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Type | Metals | Non-metals |
| Valence electrons | 1 | 7 |
| State at room temp | Solid | Gas/liquid/solid down group |
| Reactivity trend | Increases down group | Decreases down group |
| Typical ion | Positive (loses 1 e⁻) | Negative (gains 1 e⁻) |

- **Real-world example**: Chlorine disinfects swimming pools and drinking water; fluoride strengthens teeth.
- **Analogy**: Halogens are "one electron short" — like a collector needing one last card, they grab an electron eagerly.
- **Misconception**: *"Reactivity increases down every group."* Wrong — it increases down Group 1 but **decreases** down Group 17. Corrective: metals get more reactive down a group (easier to lose an electron), non-metals get less reactive down a group (harder to gain one).
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Name** three halogens and their states at room temperature.
  2. (Apply) **Compare** the number of valence electrons in Group 1 and Group 17.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why reactivity trends run in opposite directions in Groups 1 and 17.

## Chapter 3.6 — Transition Elements

### Concept: Transition Metals
- **Definition**: Transition elements are the block of metals in the centre of the table (Groups 3–12); they are hard, dense, high-melting metals that often form coloured compounds and act as catalysts.
- **Location**: the central block between Group 2 and Group 13.
- **General physical properties**: hard, strong, dense, high melting and boiling points, good conductors of heat and electricity, often magnetic (iron), form coloured compounds.
- **Examples and uses**:
  - **Iron (Fe)** — construction, steel, machinery.
  - **Gold (Au)** — jewellery, electronics (does not corrode).
  - **Silver (Ag)** — jewellery, mirrors, electrical contacts.
  - **Mercury (Hg)** — the only metal liquid at room temperature; used in some thermometers and switches.
- **Predicting properties from position**: because they cluster together, transition metals share properties — so an unfamiliar central-block metal can be predicted to be hard, dense and high-melting.
- **Analogy**: Transition metals are the "workhorse" middle of the table — sturdy and dependable, unlike the flighty Group 1 metals.
- **Misconception**: *"Mercury is a non-metal because it's a liquid."* Wrong — mercury is a metal that happens to be liquid at room temperature; it still conducts electricity and is shiny. Corrective: state of matter does not decide metal/non-metal; properties like conductivity and lustre do.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** where transition elements are located in the periodic table.
  2. (Apply) **Give** one use each for iron, gold and mercury.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how the position of a transition metal helps predict its properties.

# Unit 4: Chemical Reactions

- **Key Concept**: Change
- **Related Concepts**: Transformation, Balance, Energy
- **Global Context**: Scientific and technical innovation (how substances transform and how we represent those changes)
- **Statement of Inquiry**: In chemical reactions matter is transformed but conserved, and balanced symbolic language lets us predict and represent these changes.
- **Inquiry Questions**:
  - *Factual*: How do we write and balance chemical equations?
  - *Conceptual*: How is matter conserved when substances react?
  - *Debatable*: Are chemical symbols and equations a true description of reality or just a useful code?

## Chapter 4.1 & 4.4 — Valency and Writing Formulas

### Concept: Valency
- **Definition**: Valency is the combining power of an element — the number of electrons an atom loses, gains or shares when forming a compound. It is related to the number of valence electrons.
- **Relation to electron arrangement**: atoms react to reach a full outer shell (usually 8, or 2 for the smallest). A sodium atom (2,8,1) loses 1 electron → valency 1; oxygen (2,6) gains 2 → valency 2.
- **Common valencies**: 
  - Group 1 (Na, K, Li) = 1
  - Group 2 (Mg, Ca) = 2
  - Group 13 (Al) = 3
  - Group 17 (Cl, F) = 1
  - Group 16 (O, S) = 2
  - Group 15 (N) = 3
- **Real-world example**: Because Na has valency 1 and Cl has valency 1, they combine 1:1 as NaCl.
- **Analogy**: Valency is like the number of hands an atom has to hold onto other atoms.
- **Misconception**: *"Valency is the same as the number of valence electrons for every element."* Wrong — for oxygen (6 valence electrons) the valency is 2, because it needs only 2 more to fill its shell. Corrective: valency = electrons gained/lost/shared, which may be (8 − valence electrons) for non-metals.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Define** valency.
  2. (Apply) **State** the valency of magnesium, aluminium and chlorine.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** how the valency of oxygen relates to its electron arrangement.

### Concept: Polyatomic Ions
- **Definition**: A polyatomic ion is a group of atoms bonded together that carries an overall charge and acts as a single unit in reactions.
- **The four required polyatomic ions**:

| Name | Formula | Charge |
|------|---------|--------|
| Ammonium | NH₄⁺ | +1 |
| Carbonate | CO₃²⁻ | −2 |
| Hydroxide | OH⁻ | −1 |
| Nitrate | NO₃⁻ | −1 |
| Sulfate | SO₄²⁻ | −2 |

- **Real-world example**: Sodium hydroxide (NaOH) contains the hydroxide ion; calcium carbonate (CaCO₃, limestone) contains carbonate.
- **Analogy**: A polyatomic ion is like a small team that moves and acts as one player.
- **Why it matters**: These ions appear in countless everyday compounds (fertilisers, cleaners, rocks).
- **Misconception**: *"You can split up a polyatomic ion when balancing."* Wrong — treat it as one unit; put brackets around it if more than one is needed, e.g. Ca(OH)₂. Corrective: keep the group whole.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the formula and charge of the carbonate and ammonium ions.
  2. (Apply) **Write** the formula of ammonium nitrate.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why brackets are used in the formula Ca(OH)₂.

## Chapter 4.5 — Formula Writing (Crisscross Method)

### Concept: Writing Chemical Formulas
- **Definition**: A chemical formula shows the ratio of atoms/ions in a compound. It is built so that total positive charge equals total negative charge (the compound is neutral).
- **Binary ionic compounds**: metal + non-metal; the metal loses electrons (positive), the non-metal gains (negative).
- **Crisscross method**: write the ions with their charges/valencies, then swap the numbers to become subscripts, and simplify.
- **Worked example 1 — Aluminium oxide**:
  - Ions: Al³⁺ and O²⁻
  - Crisscross: Al takes subscript 2, O takes subscript 3
  - Formula: **Al₂O₃**
- **Worked example 2 — Calcium hydroxide** (polyatomic):
  - Ions: Ca²⁺ and OH⁻
  - Crisscross: Ca takes 1, OH takes 2 → bracket the OH
  - Formula: **Ca(OH)₂**
- **Simple covalent compounds**: two non-metals share electrons (e.g. CO₂, H₂O); formulas reflect how many atoms bond, not swapped charges.
- **Monatomic vs polyatomic ions**: monatomic = single charged atom (Na⁺, Cl⁻); polyatomic = charged group (see table above); the crisscross method works for both.
- **Analogy**: Crisscrossing is like each ion "donating" its charge number to become the other's subscript so they balance.
- **Misconception**: *"Always keep the crossed-over numbers even if they can be simplified."* Wrong — Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ crisscross to Mg₂O₂ but must simplify to **MgO**. Corrective: reduce subscripts to the smallest whole-number ratio.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the rule that makes an ionic compound electrically neutral.
  2. (Apply) **Use** the crisscross method to write the formula of magnesium chloride and aluminium sulfate.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why Mg₂O₂ must be simplified to MgO.

## Chapter 4.2 — Balancing Chemical Equations

### Concept: Law of Conservation of Mass and Balancing
- **Definition**: In a chemical reaction, atoms are neither created nor destroyed — the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products (**Law of Conservation of Mass**). So each type of atom must be equal on both sides of the equation.
- **Coefficients**: the big numbers placed in front of formulas to balance atom counts. You may change coefficients but never subscripts.
- **Worked example — burning hydrogen**:
  - Unbalanced: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O
  - Balance O (2 on left, 1 on right) → put 2 before H₂O: H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
  - Now H: 4 on right, 2 on left → put 2 before H₂: **2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O**
  - Check: H = 4 = 4; O = 2 = 2. Balanced.
- **Real-world example**: A sealed flask's mass is unchanged before and after a reaction inside it.
- **Analogy**: Balancing is like a see-saw — both sides must carry equal numbers of each atom.
- **Misconception**: *"You can balance by changing the small subscript numbers."* Wrong — changing subscripts changes the substance (H₂O → H₂O₂ is a different compound). Corrective: only change coefficients (the big front numbers).
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the law of conservation of mass.
  2. (Apply) **Balance**: Na + Cl₂ → NaCl.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why you cannot balance an equation by altering subscripts.

## Chapter 4.3 & 4.7 — Types of Reactions

### Concept: Classifying Reactions

| Type | What happens | General form | Example |
|------|--------------|--------------|---------|
| Synthesis / combination | Two or more substances join into one | A + B → AB | 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O |
| Decomposition | One substance breaks into two or more | AB → A + B | 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ |
| Single displacement | One element replaces another in a compound | A + BC → AC + B | Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu |
| Double displacement | Ions of two compounds swap partners | AB + CD → AD + CB | NaCl + AgNO₃ → AgCl + NaNO₃ |
| Combustion | A fuel reacts with oxygen, releasing energy | fuel + O₂ → oxides | CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O |

### Concept: Reactions of Alkali Metals and Halogens
- **Alkali metals with water**: produce a metal hydroxide + hydrogen gas, e.g. 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂. Reaction gets **more vigorous down the group** (Li < Na < K).
- **Alkali metals with oxygen**: form metal oxides, e.g. 4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O.
- **Halogens with metals**: form salts (metal halides), e.g. 2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl.
- **Halogens with hydrogen**: form hydrogen halides, e.g. H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl. Reaction gets **less vigorous down the group** (F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂).

### Concept: Endothermic vs Exothermic
- **Exothermic**: releases energy (usually heat) to the surroundings; surroundings warm up. Example: combustion, alkali metals + water.
- **Endothermic**: absorbs energy from the surroundings; surroundings cool down. Example: thermal decomposition, photosynthesis, some dissolving.
- **Analogy**: Exothermic is like a campfire giving out heat; endothermic is like an ice pack taking heat in.
- **Misconception**: *"All reactions give out heat."* Wrong — endothermic reactions absorb heat and feel cold. Corrective: energy can flow either way; both types exist.

### Concept: Reactivity Trends
- **Group 1 (alkali metals)**: reactivity **increases** down the group (outer electron lost more easily).
- **Group 17 (halogens)**: reactivity **decreases** down the group (harder to attract an extra electron).
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **Name** the five types of reaction studied.
  2. (Apply) **Classify** this reaction: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain**, with an example, how reactivity changes down Group 1 and Group 17.

#### Practical Note — Testing for Gases (food-of-tests style)
- **Hydrogen**: a lit splint gives a **squeaky pop**. (Method: collect gas in a tube, insert lit splint. Variable tested: presence of H₂. Expected: pop.)
- **Oxygen**: a **glowing splint relights**. (Insert a glowing splint into the gas.)
- **Carbon dioxide**: turns **limewater milky/cloudy**. (Bubble the gas through limewater.)
- **Controlled variables**: same splint type, same gas volume, same tube size.

## Chapter 4.6 & 4.7 — Predicting Products and Practical Analysis

### Concept: Predicting Products of Simple Reactions
- **Definition**: Using the reaction type to work out what the products will be, then writing and balancing the equation.
- **Worked example**: Magnesium burns in oxygen (combination/combustion). Products = magnesium oxide. Mg + O₂ → MgO → balance → **2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO**.
- **Determining reaction type from an equation**: count reactants and products and see how atoms rearrange (one→many = decomposition; many→one = synthesis; element swaps into a compound = displacement; O₂ + fuel + heat = combustion).
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the products when a metal reacts with oxygen.
  2. (Apply) **Predict** and **balance** the reaction of potassium with water.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Determine** the reaction type of 2AgNO₃ + Cu → Cu(NO₃)₂ + 2Ag and justify your answer.

## Chapter 4.8 — Nomenclature

### Concept: Naming Compounds
- **Ionic compounds (systematic naming)**: name the metal first, then the non-metal with its ending changed to **-ide** (e.g. sodium + chlorine → sodium chlor**ide**). If a polyatomic ion is present, use its name (e.g. sodium sulf**ate**, calcium carbon**ate**, sodium hydrox**ide**, ammonium nitr**ate**).
- **Simple covalent compounds (prefix naming)**: use Greek prefixes to show how many of each atom — **mono (1), di (2), tri (3)**. The first element usually drops "mono".
  - CO → carbon **mon**oxide
  - CO₂ → carbon **di**oxide
  - N₂O₃ → **di**nitrogen **tri**oxide
- **Relating names to formulas and back**: 
  - "magnesium oxide" → Mg²⁺ and O²⁻ → **MgO**.
  - "sulfur trioxide" → SO₃.
- **Analogy**: Prefixes are like counting labels on a recipe — "di-eggs" means two eggs.
- **Misconception**: *"Prefixes (di, tri) are used for ionic compounds too."* Wrong — prefixes are for covalent (non-metal + non-metal) compounds; ionic names rely on the fixed charges, so no prefixes are needed (it's "sodium chloride", not "sodium monochloride"). Corrective: use prefixes only for covalent naming.
- **IB questions**:
  1. (Recall) **State** the prefixes for one, two and three atoms.
  2. (Apply) **Name** these compounds: CaCl₂, CO₂, N₂O.
  3. (Analyse/Evaluate) **Explain** why "sodium chloride" needs no prefix but "carbon dioxide" does.

# Common Misconceptions Master List

1. **Atoms are solid balls** — actually mostly empty space; the nucleus is minute.
2. **Air is an element** — it is a mixture of elements and compounds.
3. **A compound is just a mixture of its elements** — a compound is chemically bonded with new properties.
4. **Mixtures have a fixed recipe** — only compounds have fixed ratios.
5. **Distillation removes all impurities** — it separates by boiling point only.
6. **Rf can be greater than 1** — a spot cannot outrun the solvent front.
7. **Mass number = relative atomic mass** — mass number is a whole-number count; atomic mass is an average.
8. **Isotopes are different elements** — same protons means same element.
9. **All isotopes are radioactive** — most are stable.
10. **Later atomic models proved earlier ones totally wrong** — models are refined, not discarded.
11. **Mendeleev ordered by atomic number** — he used atomic mass; Moseley introduced atomic number.
12. **Elements in the same period behave alike** — similarity runs down groups.
13. **Reactivity increases down every group** — increases down Group 1 but decreases down Group 17.
14. **Metals are always hard/solid** — alkali metals are soft; mercury is liquid.
15. **Valency equals the number of valence electrons for all atoms** — for non-metals it is often (8 − valence electrons).
16. **You can balance equations by changing subscripts** — only change coefficients.
17. **All reactions release heat** — endothermic reactions absorb heat.
18. **Prefixes (di, tri) are used for ionic compounds** — they are only for covalent naming.
19. **You can split a polyatomic ion when balancing** — treat it as one unit; use brackets.
20. **Mercury is a non-metal because it is liquid** — state does not decide metal/non-metal.
