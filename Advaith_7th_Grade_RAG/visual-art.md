## 1. Subject Overview

Visual Art at IISH belongs to the **MYP Arts** subject group. In the Arts, students act as both **artist** and **critic** — they investigate the world of art, develop and refine their own ideas and skills, create original artwork, and evaluate their process and product. Learning is driven by **inquiry**, structured around **key and related concepts**, set in **global contexts**, and assessed against **four criteria**.

### The Four MYP Arts Criteria

| Criterion | MYP Arts name | Plain-language meaning | What it asks students to do |
|-----------|---------------|------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **A** | Investigating (Knowing & understanding) | Research and understand | Investigate a movement/genre/artist; analyse artworks; use subject-specific vocabulary; connect context to the statement of inquiry |
| **B** | Developing | Plan and experiment | Practically explore techniques and ideas; develop and communicate a clear **artistic intention**; make and record informed choices |
| **C** | Creating (Performing) | Make the artwork | Create/realise a finished artwork that applies skills and techniques and fulfils the artistic intention |
| **D** | Evaluating | Judge and reflect | Appraise the finished artwork against the intention; reflect on personal artistic development and the creative process |

> Each criterion is marked out of **8** (levels 1–8) using MYP task-specific clarifications of the published rubric strands.

### Arts Related Concepts (used across the year)

**Composition, Expression, Interpretation, Style, Genre, Visual culture, Representation, Innovation, Boundaries, Audience, Narrative, Presentation.**

Quick definitions:
- **Composition** — how the parts of an artwork are arranged within the frame.
- **Expression** — communicating feeling, mood or idea through visual choices.
- **Interpretation** — the meaning a viewer or artist reads into/from an artwork.
- **Style** — the recognisable, consistent visual "signature" of an artist or movement.
- **Genre** — a category/type of art (e.g., landscape, portrait, still life).
- **Visual culture** — the images and visual practices of a society.
- **Representation** — how something real or imagined is depicted.
- **Innovation** — new ideas, methods or ways of seeing.
- **Boundaries** — the limits (of media, convention, tradition) an artist works within or breaks.
- **Audience** — the viewers an artwork is made for or received by.
- **Narrative** — the story or sequence an artwork communicates.
- **Presentation** — how finished work is displayed and shared.

## 2. UNIT 1 — The Art of Inspiration

**Key Concept:** Communication
**Related Concepts:** Expression, Interpretation, Style
**Global Context:** Personal and cultural expression (exploration: *the ways in which we discover and express ideas, feelings, nature, culture, beliefs and values*)
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Artists communicate personal expression by transforming everyday surroundings through distinctive styles and interpretation.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- **Factual:** What are the hallmarks of Impressionism, Post-Impressionism and Fauvism, and which techniques (impasto, pointillism, broken/expressive brushwork) define them?
- **Conceptual:** How does an artist's style transform an ordinary observed scene into personal expression?
- **Debatable:** Is a painting more powerful when it accurately represents what is seen, or when it reinterprets it through colour and emotion?

**Focus / Skills:** How artists influence ideas and styles; analysis, reinterpretation, stylistic experimentation; observational drawing (indoor and outdoor); mixed media.

### 2.1 Elements & Principles of Art (taught in Unit 1)

**Elements** are the building blocks (the "ingredients"). **Principles** are the ways elements are organised (the "recipe").

| Type | Term | Definition | How to use it | Example / where you see it |
|------|------|------------|---------------|----------------------------|
| Element | **Line** | A mark with length and direction | Vary weight, speed and type (broken, continuous, hatched) to describe edges, energy and movement | Van Gogh's swirling contour lines in *The Starry Night* (1889) |
| Element | **Colour** | The visual sensation from reflected light (hue, value, intensity) | Choose warm/cool, complementary or non-natural colour to set mood | Matisse's non-natural greens/reds in *Woman with a Hat* (1905) |
| Element | **Texture** | The surface quality — real (tactile) or implied (visual) | Build real texture with thick paint (impasto) or imply it with mark-making | Van Gogh's ridged, tactile paint surface |
| Element | **Tone (value)** | The lightness or darkness of an area | Shade from light to dark to model form and create depth | Gradations that make a sphere look round in a still life |
| Principle | **Contrast** | Strong difference between elements (light/dark, warm/cool, big/small) | Place opposites side by side to create focus and drama | Bright figure against a dark background draws the eye |
| Principle | **Composition** | The arrangement of all elements in the frame | Use focal point, balance, rule of thirds, leading lines | Renoir grouping figures to lead the eye through a scene |
| Principle | **Movement** | The visual flow that guides the eye through the work | Repeat directional lines/shapes to create rhythm and a path | The rolling brushwork "moving" across an Impressionist sky |
| (Space) | **Perspective** | System for showing 3-D depth on a 2-D surface (see Unit 2) | Use horizon line + vanishing point(s) so objects recede | Streets narrowing to a point in a cityscape |

**Analogy:** Elements are like the *words* of art; principles are the *grammar* that arranges words into a sentence that makes sense.

**Why it matters:** Naming elements and principles lets you *analyse* any artwork (Criterion A) and *plan* your own with intention (Criterion B).

**Common misconception:** *"Texture only means something you can physically feel."* → **Correction:** Texture can be **implied/visual** — an artist can make a flat, smooth surface *look* rough, furry or wet through mark-making alone.

### 2.2 Art Movements (Unit 1) — taught accurately

| Movement | Dates (approx.) | Key artists | Hallmarks | How to identify it |
|----------|-----------------|-------------|-----------|--------------------|
| **Impressionism** | c. 1860s–1880s (France) | Claude Monet, Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Camille Pissarro, Berthe Morisot, Edgar Degas | Capturing fleeting **light** and atmosphere; **everyday/modern scenes** and landscapes painted *en plein air* (outdoors); **visible, broken brushstrokes**; softened edges | Loose dabs of colour, light-filled outdoor scenes, no hard outlines — looks "unfinished" up close, whole up close/far |
| **Post-Impressionism** | c. 1885–1905 (France) | Vincent van Gogh, Paul Cézanne, Georges Seurat, Paul Gauguin | Reaction *after* Impressionism — kept bright colour but added **structure, order and personal emotion/symbolism**; more deliberate | Emotion or geometric structure is emphasised over pure optical light — Van Gogh's expressive swirls, Cézanne's blocky planes, Seurat's dots |
| **Fauvism** | c. 1905–1908 (France) | Henri Matisse, André Derain, Maurice de Vlaminck | **Wild, bold, non-natural colour**; flat shapes; strong expressive brushwork; colour used for feeling, not accuracy | Skies can be pink, faces green — colour is clearly *unrealistic and emotional*. ("Fauves" = "wild beasts") |

**Named reference artworks (accurate):**
- Monet, *Impression, Sunrise* (1872) — the painting that gave Impressionism its name.
- Renoir, *Bal du moulin de la Galette* (1876) — dappled light on a crowd.
- Van Gogh, *The Starry Night* (1889) — Post-Impressionist swirling brushwork and emotion.
- Cézanne, *Mont Sainte-Victoire* (series, 1880s–1900s) — structure built from planes of colour.
- Seurat, *A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte* (1884–86) — pointillism.
- Matisse, *Woman with a Hat* (1905) — Fauvist non-natural colour.

**Common misconception:** *"Post-Impressionism came before Impressionism because 'post' just means a style."* → **Correction:** "Post" means **after**. Post-Impressionism developed *after* and *in response to* Impressionism (roughly 1885 onward).

**Common misconception:** *"Impressionism and Fauvism are the same because both use bright colour."* → **Correction:** Impressionists chased *realistic light*; Fauvists used *deliberately unrealistic* colour for emotion.

### 2.3 Techniques (Unit 1) — definition + how-to

- **Impasto** — Thick paint applied so it stands up off the surface, holding brush/knife marks and casting tiny shadows (real texture). *How-to:* load a stiff brush or palette knife with undiluted paint; drag or dab so ridges stay; don't overblend. *Seen in:* Van Gogh.
- **Pointillism** — Building an image from small, separate **dots** of pure colour that the eye blends at a distance (optical mixing). *How-to:* plan colours; apply tiny dots side by side (e.g., blue + yellow dots read as green from afar); keep dots pure, don't mix on the surface. *Seen in:* Seurat.
- **Expressive / broken brushwork** — Visible, energetic, unblended strokes that show the artist's hand and feeling ("broken" = short, separate marks rather than smooth blends). *How-to:* use directional, confident strokes; leave gaps of the surface or underlayer showing; let strokes describe form *and* energy.
- **Mixed media** — Combining more than one material in one artwork (e.g., ink + collage + pastel + paint). *How-to:* layer from thin to thick / wet to dry; let each layer dry; combine for contrast of texture and surface.

**Common misconception:** *"Pointillism is just messy dabbing."* → **Correction:** It is a *deliberate, planned* optical technique — colours are chosen so the *viewer's eye* does the mixing.

### 2.4 Colour Theory (Unit 1)

| Concept | Definition | Key facts / how to use |
|---------|------------|------------------------|
| **Primary colours** | Colours that cannot be mixed from others | Red, yellow, blue — the source of all others |
| **Secondary colours** | Made by mixing two primaries | Orange (R+Y), green (Y+B), purple (B+R) |
| **Tertiary colours** | Made by mixing a primary with a neighbouring secondary | e.g., red-orange, yellow-green, blue-violet |
| **Warm colours** | Reds, oranges, yellows | Feel energetic; *tend to* appear to advance |
| **Cool colours** | Blues, greens, violets | Feel calm; *tend to* appear to recede |
| **Complementary colours** | Opposite on the colour wheel | Red/green, blue/orange, yellow/purple — placed together they intensify each other |
| **Tint** | A colour + **white** | Makes it lighter (pink is a tint of red) |
| **Shade** | A colour + **black** | Makes it darker (maroon is a shade of red) |
| **Colour wheel** | Circular arrangement of hues | Shows relationships: primaries, secondaries, complementaries, warm/cool halves |

**Analogy:** The colour wheel is a "map" of colour relationships — complementary colours sit directly across the road from each other.

**Why it matters:** Colour choice is one of the fastest ways to control mood and focus — central to Fauvism and to your own expressive work.

**Common misconception:** *"Warm colours always advance and cool colours always recede."* → **Correction:** This is a *tendency*, not a rule. Value (light/dark), intensity and context can override it — a dark warm shape can sit behind a bright cool one.

## 3. UNIT 2 — Illusion of Space

*(Taught explicitly through the four MYP Arts criteria.)*

**Key Concept:** Aesthetics
**Related Concepts:** Representation, Composition, Boundaries
**Global Context:** Personal and cultural expression (exploration: *the ways artists create and shape depth, form and visual illusion*)
**Statement of Inquiry:** *Artists use systems of perspective and composition to represent three-dimensional space and shape how an audience reads an image.*

**Inquiry Questions**
- **Factual:** What are the horizon line, vanishing point and orthogonal lines, and how do one-point and two-point perspective differ?
- **Conceptual:** How does perspective create a convincing illusion of space on a flat surface?
- **Debatable:** Is linear perspective the "correct" way to show space, or just one culture's way of seeing?

---

### 3.1 Perspective (Unit 2) — step-by-step how-to

**Key terms:**
- **Horizon line (eye level)** — a horizontal line representing the viewer's eye level; where sky meets ground in an open view.
- **Vanishing point (VP)** — a point *on the horizon line* where receding parallel lines appear to meet.
- **Orthogonal lines** — the receding "guide" lines that travel back to a vanishing point.

**One-Point Perspective (one VP)** — used when a subject faces you straight on (e.g., looking down a straight road or corridor).
1. Draw the horizon line.
2. Mark **one** vanishing point on it.
3. Draw the front face of your object as a flat, true shape (no distortion).
4. Draw orthogonal lines from each corner back to the VP.
5. Add a parallel line to "cut off" the depth, then erase the guide lines. Objects and gaps get smaller as they near the VP.

**Two-Point Perspective (two VPs)** — used when you view an object from a corner (e.g., the edge of a building).
1. Draw the horizon line.
2. Mark **two** vanishing points, one near each end of the line.
3. Draw a single vertical line for the nearest edge/corner of the object.
4. From the top and bottom of that vertical, draw orthogonal lines to **both** VPs.
5. Add vertical lines to set the width of each side; erase guides. Both sides recede, giving a solid, 3-D corner view.

**How it creates the illusion of space:** Our eyes read *converging lines* and *diminishing size* as distance. By forcing parallel edges to meet at a vanishing point and shrinking objects toward the horizon, a flat page tricks the brain into seeing depth.

**Analogy:** Stand on straight railway tracks — the rails *look* like they meet far away even though they stay parallel. That meeting point is your vanishing point.

**Historical note:** Linear perspective was formalised in Renaissance Italy (Brunelleschi's experiments, early 1400s; codified by Alberti, 1435). Masaccio's fresco *The Holy Trinity* (c. 1427) is an early one-point example.

**Common misconception:** *"Perspective = realism, so any realistic-looking picture must use linear perspective."* → **Correction:** Linear perspective is *one system* for depth. Depth can also be shown through overlap, size, atmospheric (aerial) perspective, and placement — and many valid art traditions (e.g., some East Asian and medieval art) deliberately do **not** use linear perspective.

**Common misconception:** *"Vanishing points can float anywhere on the page."* → **Correction:** In standard linear perspective, vanishing points sit **on the horizon line** (eye level).

## 4. Art Vocabulary Glossary

**Unit-list terms:**
- **Inspiration** — the spark or source that prompts an artistic idea.
- **Transformation** — changing an observed subject into something new through artistic choices.
- **Expression** — communicating feeling, mood or idea visually.
- **Impressionism** — 1860s–80s movement capturing fleeting light in everyday scenes with visible brushwork.
- **Post-Impressionism** — c.1885–1905 movement adding structure and emotion after Impressionism.
- **Fauvism** — c.1905–08 movement using wild, non-natural colour for expression.
- **Impasto** — thickly applied paint that stands off the surface.
- **Pointillism** — image built from small dots of pure colour blended by the eye.
- **Composition** — the arrangement of elements within the frame.
- **Texture** — the surface quality, real or implied.
- **Tone** — the lightness or darkness of an area (value).
- **Contrast** — strong visual difference used to create emphasis.
- **Movement** — the visual path the eye follows through a work.
- **Perspective** — a system for representing 3-D depth on a 2-D surface.
- **Observation** — careful looking to record what is actually seen.
- **Interpretation** — the meaning read into or expressed through an artwork.

**Supporting terms:**
- **Horizon line** — horizontal line at the viewer's eye level.
- **Vanishing point** — point on the horizon where receding lines meet.
- **Orthogonal lines** — receding guide lines heading to a vanishing point.
- **En plein air** — painting outdoors, from life.
- **Optical mixing** — colours blending in the viewer's eye rather than on the surface.
- **Broken brushwork** — short, separate, unblended strokes.
- **Mixed media** — artwork combining more than one material.
- **Hue / Value / Intensity** — colour's name / lightness / brightness.
- **Complementary colours** — opposites on the colour wheel.
- **Tint / Shade** — colour + white / colour + black.
- **Artistic intention** — the artist's stated purpose and plan for a work.

## 5. Common Misconceptions — Master List

1. **"Post-Impressionism came before Impressionism."** → "Post" means *after*; it developed c.1885 in response to Impressionism.
2. **"Impressionism and Fauvism are the same because both are colourful."** → Impressionists sought realistic light; Fauvists used deliberately unnatural colour for emotion.
3. **"Texture is only what you can physically feel."** → Texture can be *implied/visual* on a smooth surface.
4. **"Pointillism is random dabbing."** → It is planned optical mixing with pure colours.
5. **"Warm colours always advance; cool colours always recede."** → A tendency, not a law; value and context can reverse it.
6. **"Perspective = realism only."** → Linear perspective is one depth system; overlap, size, and atmospheric perspective also create depth, and many traditions avoid it by choice.
7. **"Vanishing points can be placed anywhere."** → They sit on the horizon line (eye level).
8. **"A finished-looking painting is 'better' than a loose one."** → Value depends on the artist's *intention*; Impressionist looseness was a deliberate choice, not a lack of skill.

## 6. Criteria / Command-Term Question Bank

1. **Analyse** (A): Analyse how Monet uses light, colour and broken brushwork in *Impression, Sunrise* to capture a fleeting moment.
2. **Analyse** (A): Analyse how one-point perspective creates the illusion of depth in a chosen artwork, referring to the horizon line and vanishing point.
3. **Interpret** (A): Interpret the mood Matisse creates through non-natural colour in *Woman with a Hat*, and explain what the colour choices suggest.
4. **Investigate** (A): Investigate the hallmarks of Post-Impressionism and explain how it differs from Impressionism.
5. **Create** (B/C): Create a two-point perspective drawing of a building corner that fulfils a clear artistic intention about space.
6. **Develop** (B): Develop and present an artistic intention for an artwork exploring the illusion of space, showing the techniques you will use.
7. **Appraise** (D): Appraise your final artwork — how successfully did it achieve the illusion of space and its intended narrative?
8. **Reflect** (D): Reflect on your development as an artist across the unit — which skills grew, and what would you change in your process?

## 7. Cross-Subject Connections

- **History (Individuals & Societies):** Place the movements on a timeline — the **Renaissance** invention of linear perspective (Brunelleschi/Alberti, 1400s) and the 19th-century sequence Impressionism → Post-Impressionism → Fauvism. Link art movements to their social context (industrialisation, modern city life, new paint tubes enabling outdoor painting).
- **Mathematics:** Perspective is **geometry** — parallel lines, angles, ratios of diminishing size, and the vanishing point as a limit. Constructing accurate one/two-point grids uses the same reasoning as scale and proportion in maths.
- **Language & Literature:** **Critique writing** — using precise vocabulary, describing then analysing then evaluating an artwork, and structuring a paragraph (point → evidence → explanation) mirrors analytical essay skills. Narrative in art connects to storytelling and sequencing.
- **Science (extension):** Colour and light — how the eye perceives colour, optical mixing, and why complementary colours intensify each other links to the physics of light and human vision.
