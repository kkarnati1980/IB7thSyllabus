## 1. Subject Overview: Music in MYP Arts

Music sits in the **MYP Arts** subject group. Students learn as **performers, creators, and reflective artists**. They investigate how music works, develop their skills, create and perform music, and evaluate their artistic process and product.

### 1.1 The Four MYP Arts Assessment Criteria

Each criterion is marked out of **8** (four achievement bands: 1–2, 3–4, 5–6, 7–8).

| Criterion | Title | In plain language | What a Grade 7 musician does |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| **A** | Investigating / Knowing & Understanding | Research and knowledge | Investigates a style/genre, demonstrates knowledge of music elements and vocabulary, and links it to context. |
| **B** | Developing | Skills and planning | Practises and develops vocal/musical skills, plans and refines ideas, shows growth over time. |
| **C** | Creating / Performing | Making and doing | Creates (improvises/arranges) and performs music, applying skills and technique. |
| **D** | Evaluating | Reflection and appraisal | Evaluates own and others' work against criteria, reflects on progress, sets next-step goals. |

> Note: Different MYP Arts guides phrase Criterion A as "Knowing and understanding" and Criterion C as "Creating/Thinking creatively" or "Performing." For Grade 7 Music at IISH, read A = **Investigating/Knowing**, B = **Developing**, C = **Creating/Performing**, D = **Evaluating**.

### 1.2 Arts Related Concepts

Related concepts are the specialised "lenses" of the Arts. Grade 7 Music draws on:

| Related concept | Meaning in music | Quick example |
|-----------------|------------------|---------------|
| **Expression** | Communicating emotion/ideas through sound | Singing a lullaby gently to convey calm |
| **Interpretation** | The performer's personal reading of a piece | Two singers phrasing the same folk song differently |
| **Presentation** | How music is shared with an audience | Ensemble posture, entrance, stage presence |
| **Structure** | How music is organised (form, sections) | Verse–chorus–verse in a pop song |
| **Style** | The characteristic "sound" of a genre/era | The swing feel of jazz vs. straight folk |
| **Audience** | Who receives the music and how they respond | Adjusting volume/energy for a school assembly |
| **Role** | The function a musician plays | Melody singer vs. harmony singer vs. soloist |
| **Innovation** | Creating something new/original | Improvising a new melody over a known chord |
| **Boundaries** | Limits explored, stretched, or crossed | Extending vocal range; blending two genres |

## 2. Unit 1 — Core Elements of Vocal Music

### 2.1 Unit Planner (MYP Framework)

- **Key Concept:** **Aesthetics** — how we perceive, respond to, and create beauty and meaning in sound. (Secondary link: **Communication** — voice as a tool for sharing ideas and feeling.)
- **Related Concepts:** **Expression**, **Interpretation**, **Presentation**.
- **Global Context:** **Personal and cultural expression** — exploring the ways we discover and express identity, feelings, and culture through the singing voice.
- **Statement of Inquiry (SOI):**
  > *Singers use the elements of music and vocal technique to express personal and cultural identity, interpreting and presenting song for an audience.*

- **Inquiry Questions:**
  - **Factual:** What are the elements of music, and what are the parts of correct vocal technique (posture, breathing, diction, tone)?
  - **Conceptual:** How do dynamics, tempo, and phrasing shape the expression and interpretation of a song?
  - **Debatable:** Is a "good" singer born with talent, or made through technique and practice?

- **ATL (Approaches to Learning) in focus:**
  - *Self-management* — practice routines, warm-up discipline.
  - *Communication* — reading notation; conveying mood to an audience.
  - *Thinking (creative)* — improvisation and interpretation.
  - *Social* — blending and balancing in ensemble.

- **Learner Profile emphasis:** Communicators, Risk-takers (performing), Reflective (self-evaluation).

## 3. Sub-topic A — Warm-ups

**Why warm-ups matter:** The voice is produced by muscles (the vocal folds) and supported by breath. Just like a runner stretches before a race, a singer warms up to increase blood flow, relax the throat and jaw, engage the breath, and prevent strain or injury. Warming up also focuses the mind and tunes the ear before singing repertoire.

### 3.1 Breathing exercises

- **Definition:** Controlled breathing drills that activate the **diaphragm** (the dome-shaped muscle below the lungs) so the singer has a steady, supported air stream.
- **How-to cue:** Stand tall. Place a hand on your belly. Breathe in silently through the nose so the belly expands outward (not the shoulders). Exhale slowly on a steady "sss" for 4, then 8, then 12 counts — keep the hiss even to the very end.
- **Real example:** "Hissing snake" — inhale for 4, hiss out for 8, aiming for a smooth, unbroken sound.
- **Why it matters:** Steady breath = steady tone, longer phrases, and better pitch control.
- **Common misconception:** *"You breathe with your shoulders / chest."* **Correction:** Good singing breath is low and expands the belly and lower ribs; raised shoulders create tension and shallow air.
- **Practice cue:** Watch your shoulders in a mirror — if they lift when you inhale, drop them and breathe into your belly instead.

### 3.2 Vocal sirens / glissando

- **Definition:** A **glissando** (or "siren") is a smooth, continuous slide from a low pitch up to a high pitch and back, with no separate steps between notes.
- **How-to cue:** On an "ng" or "oo" sound, glide gently like a siren from your lowest comfortable note to your highest and back down. Keep it light — no pushing.
- **Real example:** Sliding "oooo" from low to high like an ambulance siren.
- **Analogy:** Like stretching an elastic band slowly and evenly — gentle and gradual, never a sudden yank.
- **Why it matters:** Sirens gently connect your low ("chest") and high ("head") voice, warming the whole range smoothly.
- **Common misconception:** *"Warm up loudly to get power."* **Correction:** Warm-ups should be light and easy; loud pushing on a cold voice risks strain.
- **Practice cue:** Keep the slide connected — imagine one unbroken line, not a staircase.

### 3.3 Scale warm-ups

- **Definition:** Singing short scale patterns (often on solfege or "ah/ee/oo") to warm the voice and train the ear for pitch.
- **How-to cue:** Sing a five-note pattern "do-re-mi-fa-sol-fa-mi-re-do," then move it up a step and repeat, keeping vowels tall and even.
- **Real example:** A rising and falling major five-note scale climbing by half-steps across the class range.
- **Why it matters:** Builds pitch accuracy, evenness of tone across the range, and vowel consistency.
- **Common misconception:** *"Scales are boring exercises with no purpose."* **Correction:** Scales train the ear and voice together — they are the "stretching and strengthening" that make songs easier.
- **Practice cue:** Match each note exactly to a keyboard or drone; listen before you sing.

## 4. Sub-topic B — Vocal Technique

Good singing is a coordinated system: **posture supports breath, breath supports tone, and shaping (diction) makes it clear.**

### 4.1 Singing posture

- **Definition:** The aligned, balanced body position that lets breath flow freely.
- **How-to cue:** Feet shoulder-width apart, weight balanced; knees soft (not locked); spine tall; shoulders relaxed and down; chest comfortably open ("noble" posture); head level, chin parallel to the floor.
- **Why it matters:** A collapsed chest or slumped spine squashes the breath and dulls the tone.
- **Common misconception:** *"Standing stiff and rigid is good posture."* **Correction:** Posture should be tall but **relaxed and flexible** — tension anywhere (jaw, neck, shoulders) hurts the sound.
- **Practice cue:** Imagine a string gently lifting the crown of your head toward the ceiling.

### 4.2 Diaphragmatic breathing (breath support)

- **Definition:** Using the diaphragm and lower-rib muscles to control a steady stream of air ("support").
- **How-to cue:** Inhale low (belly expands), then sing while keeping the ribs from collapsing too fast — as if gently holding the expansion.
- **Analogy:** Support is like slowly letting air out of a balloon by pinching the neck — controlled, not all at once.
- **Why it matters:** Support gives steady pitch, longer phrases, and dynamic control without strain.
- **Common misconception:** *"Take the biggest breath possible before every phrase."* **Correction:** You need an *appropriate*, low, relaxed breath — over-filling creates tension.
- **Practice cue:** Sing a phrase and try to end it with a little breath still in reserve.

### 4.3 Diction / articulation

- **Definition:** The clear pronunciation of **vowels** (which carry the tone) and **consonants** (which carry the words).
- **How-to cue:** Sustain sound on tall, open vowels; make consonants crisp and quick so they don't interrupt the line. Keep the jaw loose and the tongue relaxed.
- **Real example:** In "night," hold the "ah" vowel and add a light, late "t" at the very end.
- **Why it matters:** Diction lets the audience understand the lyrics and helps the ensemble stay together.
- **Common misconception:** *"Sing loudly and people will understand the words."* **Correction:** Clarity comes from crisp consonants and tall vowels, not volume.
- **Practice cue:** Speak the lyrics in rhythm first, then sing them keeping the same clarity.

### 4.4 Tone quality / resonance

- **Definition:** The **timbre** or "colour" of the voice — how warm, bright, clear, or focused it sounds. Resonance is how sound vibrates and amplifies in the throat, mouth, and head spaces.
- **How-to cue:** Keep vowels tall and open, soft palate lifted (as in the start of a yawn), throat relaxed and free.
- **Analogy:** Resonance is like the hollow body of a guitar — the same string sounds fuller because of the space it vibrates in.
- **Why it matters:** A free, resonant tone carries better and sounds pleasant without forcing.
- **Common misconception:** *"A breathy or forced tone is more expressive."* **Correction:** A free, supported tone is healthiest and most flexible; expression comes from dynamics and phrasing, not strain.
- **Practice cue:** Sing "ng → ah" and feel the buzz/vibration move to the front of the face.

### 4.5 Blending & balance in ensemble

- **Definition:** **Blending** = matching your vowel, tone, and volume to others so voices sound unified. **Balance** = adjusting so no part is too loud or too soft relative to the whole.
- **How-to cue:** Listen more than you sing; match vowel shapes; avoid sticking out; a harmony part supports and sits under the melody.
- **Why it matters:** A choir should sound like one instrument, not many soloists.
- **Common misconception:** *"The best singer should be the loudest."* **Correction:** In ensemble, the goal is to blend — loudness that sticks out breaks the unified sound.
- **Practice cue:** If you can't hear the person next to you, you're singing too loud.

### 4.6 Vocal health

- **Definition:** Habits that protect the voice from strain and injury.
- **How-to cue:** Warm up before singing; stay hydrated; avoid shouting/screaming; rest the voice when tired or sick; never push through pain.
- **Why it matters:** The voice is a lifelong instrument you cannot replace — protect it.
- **Common misconception:** *"Straining and pushing builds a stronger voice."* **Correction:** Pain or hoarseness is a warning sign; strength comes from good technique and gradual practice, not force.
- **Practice cue:** If your throat hurts or your voice is hoarse, stop and rest — do not push.

## 5. Sub-topic C — Music Literacy

Learning to read music is like learning to read words — the symbols tell you the pitch (how high/low) and the rhythm (how long) of each sound.

### 5.1 The staff (stave)

- **Definition:** The set of **5 lines and 4 spaces** on which notes are written. Higher on the staff = higher pitch.
- **Example:** A note sitting on the bottom line is lower than a note on the top line.
- **Practice cue:** Point to two notes and say which sounds higher based on its position.

### 5.2 Treble clef

- **Definition:** The symbol at the start of the staff (also called the **G clef**) that fixes the note names; its curl circles the line for the note **G**.
- **Note names on the treble staff:**
  - **Lines** (bottom→top): **E – G – B – D – F** ("Every Good Boy Deserves Fun").
  - **Spaces** (bottom→top): **F – A – C – E** (spells "FACE").
- **Analogy:** The clef is like a "key" that tells you which letters the lines and spaces stand for.
- **Practice cue:** Name three notes on the staff using the line/space mnemonics.

### 5.3 Note names & the musical alphabet

- **Definition:** Pitches are named with the letters **A B C D E F G**, then repeat. After G, it starts again at A.
- **Example:** Going up from C: C–D–E–F–G–A–B–C (an octave).
- **Misconception:** *"There are notes called H."* **Correction:** The English musical alphabet only uses **A–G**; it cycles.
- **Practice cue:** Say the alphabet forward and backward: A-B-C-D-E-F-G and G-F-E-D-C-B-A.

### 5.4 Note & rest values

- **Definition:** Symbols showing how long a sound (note) or silence (rest) lasts, measured in **beats**.

| Note | Name (US / UK) | Beats in 4/4 | Matching rest |
|------|----------------|--------------|---------------|
| ○ (open, no stem) | Whole / Semibreve | **4 beats** | Whole rest |
| ♩ with open head | Half / Minim | **2 beats** | Half rest |
| ♩ (filled, stem) | Quarter / Crotchet | **1 beat** | Quarter rest |
| ♪ (filled, flag) | Eighth / Quaver | **½ beat** | Eighth rest |

- **Analogy (fractions):** A whole note is a whole pizza; two halves make a whole; four quarters make a whole; eight eighths make a whole.
- **Misconception:** *"A rest means stop playing/relax."* **Correction:** A rest is **counted silence** — you keep the beat internally and stay in time.
- **Practice cue:** Clap a quarter note (1 clap = 1 beat), then two eighths (2 claps in 1 beat: "1-and").

### 5.5 Time signatures

- **Definition:** The two stacked numbers after the clef. **Top number = beats per bar; bottom number = which note gets one beat** (4 = quarter note).
- **Examples:**
  - **4/4** ("common time") = 4 quarter-note beats per bar. Feels like: 1-2-3-4. Most pop and folk songs.
  - **3/4** = 3 quarter-note beats per bar. Feels like a waltz: 1-2-3, strong-weak-weak.
- **Misconception:** *"The bottom number is how many beats there are."* **Correction:** The **top** number is how many beats per bar; the bottom tells you *what kind* of note gets the beat.
- **Practice cue:** Count "1-2-3-4" for 4/4 and "1-2-3" for 3/4, stamping on beat 1.

### 5.6 Beat & rhythm

- **Definition:** The **beat** is the steady, regular pulse (like a heartbeat). **Rhythm** is the pattern of long and short sounds that plays over the beat.
- **Example:** Beat = a metronome ticking evenly; rhythm = the varied pattern of the words in "Twinkle, Twinkle."
- **Analogy:** The beat is the footsteps; the rhythm is the dance done over those steps.
- **Misconception:** *"Beat and rhythm are the same thing."* **Correction:** Beat is the constant pulse; rhythm is the changing pattern on top of it.
- **Practice cue:** Tap the steady beat with your foot while clapping the rhythm of a song's words.

### 5.7 Tempo

- **Definition:** The **speed** of the beat, often given in Italian terms or in BPM (beats per minute).
- **Common terms:** *Largo* (very slow), *Adagio* (slow), *Andante* (walking pace), *Moderato* (moderate), *Allegro* (fast, lively), *Presto* (very fast). *Accelerando* = getting faster; *ritardando/rallentando* = getting slower.
- **Example:** A lullaby is *Adagio*; a celebration song is *Allegro*.
- **Misconception:** *"Faster music is always more exciting/better."* **Correction:** Tempo serves the mood — a slow tempo can be powerful and moving.
- **Practice cue:** Sing one phrase at *Andante*, then at *Allegro*, and describe how the feeling changes.

### 5.8 Dynamics

- **Definition:** How **loud or soft** the music is.

| Marking | Italian term | Meaning |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| **pp** | pianissimo | very soft |
| **p** | piano | soft |
| **mp** | mezzo-piano | medium soft |
| **mf** | mezzo-forte | medium loud |
| **f** | forte | loud |
| **ff** | fortissimo | very loud |
| **<** | crescendo | gradually getting louder |
| **>** | decrescendo / diminuendo | gradually getting softer |

- **Analogy:** Dynamics are like the volume knob and the light dimmer of a song — they add shadow and shine.
- **Misconception:** *"Singing louder = singing better."* **Correction:** Great singing uses a *range* of dynamics; soft, controlled singing is often harder and more expressive than loud singing.
- **Practice cue:** Sing one line with a crescendo (soft→loud), then a decrescendo (loud→soft).

### 5.9 Pitch

- **Definition:** How **high or low** a sound is, determined by the frequency of vibration (faster vibration = higher pitch).
- **Example:** A small child's voice or a whistle = high pitch; a large drum or low voice = low pitch.
- **Misconception:** *"High pitch means loud."* **Correction:** Pitch (high/low) and volume (loud/soft) are different — you can sing a high note softly and a low note loudly.
- **Practice cue:** Match a pitch played on a keyboard, then sing one higher and one lower.

### 5.10 Scales & solfege

- **Definition:** A **scale** is an ordered sequence of pitches going up or down. The **major scale** has a specific pattern of whole and half steps: **W–W–H–W–W–W–H**.
- **Solfege:** Names for the steps of the scale used for sight-singing: **do–re–mi–fa–sol–la–ti–do**.

| Solfege | Scale degree | In C major |
|---------|-------------|-----------|
| do | 1 (tonic/home) | C |
| re | 2 | D |
| mi | 3 | E |
| fa | 4 | F |
| sol | 5 | G |
| la | 6 | A |
| ti | 7 (leading tone) | B |
| do | 8 (octave) | C |

- **Analogy:** *do* is "home base"; the scale is a staircase up to *do* one octave higher.
- **Misconception:** *"Every scale sounds the same / solfege is just for kids."* **Correction:** A major scale sounds bright/happy; a minor scale sounds darker/sad. Solfege is a professional ear-training tool used worldwide.
- **Practice cue:** Sing up and down "do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do" with hand signs, keeping each step in tune.

### 5.11 Intervals (basics)

- **Definition:** An **interval** is the distance in pitch between two notes.
- **Examples:** do→do (same note) = *unison*; do→re = a *2nd* (a step); do→mi = a *3rd* (a skip); do→sol = a *5th*; do→do (higher) = an *octave*.
- **Analogy:** Intervals are like the gap between two rungs on a ladder — small gap (step) or larger gap (skip/leap).
- **Misconception:** *"Notes next to each other on the staff are always far apart in sound."* **Correction:** Adjacent staff positions are usually a **step (2nd)** — close in pitch.
- **Practice cue:** Sing do→re (step), then do→mi (skip), and feel which is "bigger."

### 5.12 Reference Table — Elements of Music

| Element | What it controls | Grade 7 question to ask |
|---------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Pitch** | High / low | Is this note higher or lower? |
| **Rhythm** | Long / short pattern | What's the pattern of the words? |
| **Beat/Pulse** | Steady tick | Can I tap the steady beat? |
| **Tempo** | Speed | Is it fast or slow? |
| **Dynamics** | Loud / soft | Where does it get louder or softer? |
| **Timbre/Tone** | Sound colour | Is the tone warm, bright, breathy? |
| **Texture** | Layers of sound | One voice (unison) or many parts? |
| **Structure/Form** | Organisation | Where are the verses and chorus? |
| **Harmony** | Notes sounding together | Is there more than one pitch at once? |

## 6. Sub-topic D — Repertoire & Interpretation

### 6.1 Types of repertoire

| Type | What it offers Grade 7 singers | Example feature |
|------|-------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Folk songs** | Cultural roots, simple singable melodies, storytelling | Often narrow range, repeating verses |
| **Choral pieces** | Ensemble skills, harmony, blending, part-singing | Two-part rounds or simple harmonies |
| **Contemporary selections** | Familiar styles, motivation, expressive freedom | Verse–chorus pop structure |

- **Why range matters:** Choosing songs that stretch the range **gently** builds the voice; a song should sit mostly in a comfortable range with a few reaching notes — never constant strain.
- **Why dynamics matter in repertoire:** Well-chosen songs give chances to practise soft and loud singing, crescendos, and expressive contrast.

### 6.2 How to interpret a song

- **Definition of interpretation:** The performer's personal, thoughtful choices that bring meaning and emotion to a piece — the "how," not just the "what."
- **Steps:**
  1. **Mood:** What is the song about? What feeling should the audience get (joyful, sad, hopeful)?
  2. **Phrasing:** Where do musical sentences begin and end? Breathe at the ends of phrases, not in the middle of words.
  3. **Expression:** Use dynamics, tempo, and tone colour to match the mood (e.g., soft and gentle for tenderness, brighter and stronger for triumph).
  4. **Text/word painting:** Emphasise key words; let the meaning of the lyrics guide your dynamics and energy.
- **Misconception:** *"Interpretation means changing the notes to whatever I want."* **Correction:** Interpretation works *within* the song — you shape expression, dynamics, and phrasing, while staying true to the melody and rhythm.
- **Practice cue:** Circle the most important word in each phrase and decide how you'll bring it out.

## 7. Sub-topic E — Creative Activities

### 7.1 Improvisation basics

- **Definition:** Making up music **in the moment**, spontaneously, often over a known framework (a scale, beat, or chord).
- **How-to cue:** Start with a limited palette — e.g., improvise using only do-re-mi-sol-la (a pentatonic set that always sounds good together) over a steady beat.
- **Why it matters:** Builds confidence, creativity (Related concept **innovation**), and ear-to-voice connection.
- **Misconception:** *"Improvising means there are no rules and anything goes randomly."* **Correction:** Good improvisation stays inside a chosen key/scale and keeps the beat — freedom *within* a structure.
- **Practice cue:** Improvise a 4-beat "answer" to a 4-beat phrase the teacher sings (call and response).

### 7.2 Song interpretation (creative choices)

- **Definition:** Deliberately choosing how to perform a known song to express a mood or idea.
- **How-to cue:** Take one verse and try it three ways (e.g., soft and slow; bright and lively; building with a crescendo) and choose which best fits the meaning.
- **Why it matters:** Develops expression, interpretation, and personal artistry (Criteria B & C).
- **Practice cue:** Justify your choice: "I sang it softly because the lyrics are about missing home."

### 7.3 Group performance skills

- **Definition:** The teamwork and presentation skills needed to perform together for an **audience**.
- **How-to cue:** Watch the conductor/leader; breathe together; start and stop together; keep balance; hold focus and posture from entrance to bow.
- **Why it matters:** Presentation and audience awareness turn practice into a real performance.
- **Misconception:** *"If I know my part, the group will be fine."* **Correction:** Ensemble success depends on **listening and coordinating** with everyone, not just knowing your own part.
- **Practice cue:** Rehearse a clean, together start and a clean, together final cut-off.

## 8. Music Vocabulary Glossary

- **Staff/Stave:** 5 lines + 4 spaces where notes are written.
- **Treble clef (G clef):** Symbol fixing note names on the staff; curls around the G line.
- **Note:** A symbol showing pitch and duration of a sound.
- **Rest:** A symbol showing a counted silence.
- **Beat/Pulse:** The steady, regular tick of the music.
- **Rhythm:** The pattern of long and short sounds over the beat.
- **Tempo:** The speed of the beat (e.g., Andante, Allegro).
- **Dynamics:** Loudness/softness (p, f, crescendo, decrescendo).
- **Pitch:** How high or low a sound is.
- **Scale:** An ordered set of pitches (e.g., major scale).
- **Solfege:** do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti-do syllables for sight-singing.
- **Interval:** The distance in pitch between two notes (2nd, 3rd, 5th, octave).
- **Octave:** The interval between a note and the next same-named note (8 scale steps).
- **Timbre/Tone colour:** The characteristic "colour" of a sound.
- **Resonance:** Amplification of the voice in the body's air spaces.
- **Diaphragm:** The breathing muscle below the lungs that supports the voice.
- **Diction/Articulation:** Clear pronunciation of vowels and consonants.
- **Phrasing:** Shaping music into "sentences," with breaths at phrase ends.
- **Blend:** Matching tone/vowel/volume so voices sound unified.
- **Balance:** Adjusting parts so none overpowers the whole.
- **Unison:** Everyone singing the same pitch.
- **Harmony:** Two or more different pitches sounding together.
- **Texture:** How many layers of sound there are.
- **Form/Structure:** How a piece is organised (e.g., verse–chorus).
- **Glissando/Siren:** A smooth slide between pitches.
- **Improvisation:** Making up music spontaneously.
- **Interpretation:** A performer's expressive reading of a piece.
- **A cappella:** Singing without instrumental accompaniment.
- **Tonic (do):** The "home" note of a scale.
- **Bar/Measure:** A segment of music containing a set number of beats.

## 9. Common Misconceptions Master List

1. **"Singing louder = singing better."** → Great singing uses a *range* of dynamics; controlled soft singing is often harder and more expressive.
2. **"You either can sing or you can't — it's natural talent."** → Singing is a skill built through technique and practice; almost everyone improves with training.
3. **"Breathe with your shoulders/chest."** → Support comes from low, diaphragmatic breathing; the belly and lower ribs expand, shoulders stay relaxed.
4. **"A rest means relax/stop counting."** → A rest is counted silence; keep the beat internally.
5. **"The bottom number of a time signature = number of beats."** → The **top** number gives beats per bar; the bottom tells which note gets the beat.
6. **"Beat and rhythm are the same."** → Beat is the steady pulse; rhythm is the changing pattern over it.
7. **"High pitch = loud."** → Pitch (high/low) and dynamics (loud/soft) are independent.
8. **"Faster is always more exciting/better."** → Tempo should serve the mood; slow can be powerful.
9. **"Warm up loudly and hard."** → Warm-ups are gentle; pushing a cold voice risks strain.
10. **"Straining builds a stronger voice."** → Pain/hoarseness is a warning; strength comes from good technique, not force.
11. **"The best ensemble singer is the loudest."** → The goal is to blend and balance, not stand out.
12. **"Interpretation means changing the notes."** → Interpretation shapes expression, dynamics, and phrasing within the written music.
13. **"Improvisation has no rules."** → Good improvisation stays within a chosen scale/key and keeps the beat.
14. **"There's a note called H."** → The English musical alphabet is only A–G, then repeats.

## 10. Criteria / Command-Term Question Bank

Command terms tell students *what kind of thinking* is required. Below are ready-to-use questions mapped to MYP Arts criteria and command terms.

| # | Command term | Criterion | Question |
|---|--------------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | **Identify** | A (Investigating/Knowing) | *Identify* the treble-clef note names on the lines and spaces, and *identify* the time signature of a given piece. |
| 2 | **Describe** | A (Investigating/Knowing) | *Describe* the correct singing posture and *describe* how diaphragmatic breathing supports the voice. |
| 3 | **Explain** | A (Investigating/Knowing) | *Explain* the difference between beat and rhythm, using an example from a song you know. |
| 4 | **Demonstrate/Perform** | C (Creating/Performing) | *Perform* a short folk song, applying correct posture, breathing, diction, and at least one crescendo and decrescendo. |
| 5 | **Create/Improvise** | C (Creating/Performing) | *Create* a 4-beat improvised melodic answer using do-re-mi-sol-la over a steady beat. |
| 6 | **Interpret** | B & C (Developing / Performing) | *Interpret* a given song: choose a mood, phrasing, and dynamics that fit the lyrics, and justify your choices. |
| 7 | **Evaluate** | D (Evaluating) | *Evaluate* your own performance against the assessment criteria: what went well, and what is your next practice goal? |
| 8 | **Compare** | A & D | *Compare* two singers' interpretations of the same song — how do their tempo, dynamics, and expression differ? |

## 11. Cross-Subject Connections

| Subject | Connection to Grade 7 Music |
|---------|------------------------------|
| **Mathematics** | **Rhythm as fractions and ratio:** note values are fractions of a whole (whole=1, half=½, quarter=¼, eighth=⅛); a bar in 4/4 must add up to 4 beats. Time signatures, counting, and subdividing beats are direct maths. Intervals and scale patterns involve ratios and sequences. |
| **Language & Literature** | **Lyrics and interpretation:** analysing the meaning, mood, imagery, and theme of song texts mirrors literary analysis; phrasing in music parallels punctuation and sentence structure in reading aloud. Expression and word-painting connect to figurative language. |
| **PAHE (Physical & Health Education)** | **Breathing and the body:** diaphragmatic breathing, posture, core support, and physical stamina all overlap with PAHE. Vocal health is personal well-being; warm-ups parallel physical warm-ups before exercise. |
| **Physics (Sciences)** | **The science of sound:** pitch is the *frequency* of a vibrating source (faster vibration = higher pitch); volume/dynamics relate to *amplitude*; timbre relates to the mix of harmonics/overtones; resonance is amplification of sound in air spaces. Sound travels as waves through air. |
